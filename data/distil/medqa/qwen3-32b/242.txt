The clinical scenario presents a 4-year-old girl with symptoms of fever, sore throat, and a gray-white membrane in the pharynx that bleeds when scraped. These are hallmark signs of **diphtheria**, a disease caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. The gray membrane is a result of the body’s inflammatory response and the necrotic tissue caused by the diphtheria toxin.

---

The **diphtheria toxin** is a key virulence factor. It is composed of two subunits: the **A subunit**, which is the active enzymatic component, and the **B subunit**, which facilitates entry of the toxin into the cell. Once inside, the A subunit **ADP-ribosylates elongation factor 2 (EF-2)**, an essential protein in the process of **translation**.

EF-2 plays a crucial role in the **translocation** step of protein synthesis. During translation, after a peptide bond is formed between the amino acids in the A and P sites of the ribosome, the ribosome must **translocate** to the next codon on the mRNA. This movement is catalyzed by EF-2. When EF-2 is inactivated, **translation is halted**, and the cell cannot synthesize new proteins, leading to cell death and the systemic effects observed in diphtheria.

---

Let’s briefly evaluate the other options:

- **A: Folding of completed proteins** – Protein folding is a post-translational process and not directly related to the mechanism of diphtheria toxin.
- **C: Binding of tRNA to the A site** – This is part of the initiation and elongation phase, but the diphtheria toxin does not interfere with tRNA binding.
- **D: Interference of post-translational modifications** – Again, the toxin acts before this step, during active translation.
- **E: Release of completed protein from ribosome** – This step is also not affected by the toxin.

---

Therefore, the most accurate and directly relevant step disrupted by the diphtheria toxin is **translocation of the ribosome along the mRNA**, due to the inactivation of EF-2.
