### Pathologic Distinction in Follicular Lesions

The definitive distinction between **follicular adenoma (benign)** and **follicular carcinoma (malignant)** lies in the presence of **capsular invasion** and/or **vascular invasion** on histopathology. These two features are **hallmarks of follicular carcinoma** and are not present in follicular adenomas.

In this case, the surgical pathology report notes **vascular invasion**. This is a key diagnostic criterion for **follicular carcinoma**. Therefore, we are now looking for an **additional characteristic** that is **expected in follicular carcinoma**.

---

### Evaluation of the Options

- **A: Capsular invasion**  
  This is a **classic and expected** feature of follicular carcinoma. It is often found in conjunction with vascular invasion. Given that vascular invasion is already noted, **capsular invasion is a likely accompanying feature** in this malignancy. This makes **A the most appropriate answer**.

- **B: Cystic nature**  
  Cystic changes are **common in benign nodules**, such as colloid nodules or follicular adenomas. They are not specific for malignancy and are not a defining feature of follicular carcinoma.

- **C: Psammoma bodies**  
  These are **characteristic of papillary thyroid carcinoma**, not follicular. Their presence would suggest a different histological type and is **not expected here**.

- **D: Eosinophil and macrophage invasion**

- **E: Pleomorphic cells without follicle development**
