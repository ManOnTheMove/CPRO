- **Arthritis** (bilateral knee pain, synovial fluid WBC count of 51,000/µL, indicating inflammation)

- **Conjunctivitis** (conjunctival injection and eye irritation)
- **No fever** (important to distinguish from other inflammatory arthritides)

These features are classic for **reactive arthritis**, particularly in the context of a **post-infectious** immune-mediated response. Reactive arthritis typically follows an infection in the gastrointestinal or genitourinary tract, often due to *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, *Campylobacter*, *Yersinia*, or *Chlamydia trachomatis*. The **Reiter's triad** of arthritis, conjunctivitis, and urethritis is a hallmark of this condition.

---

### Treatment Considerations:

The primary goals in treating reactive arthritis are to:
1. **Control inflammation** (especially in the joints)
2. **Manage associated symptoms** (e.g., urethritis, conjunctivitis)

The first-line treatment for reactive arthritis is **nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)**, which help manage the inflammatory arthritis effectively. In this case, **Indomethacin**, a potent NSAID, is the most appropriate initial treatment.

- **Acetaminophen** (A) is not suitable for inflammatory arthritis as it lacks anti-inflammatory effects.
- **Allopurinol** (B) and **Colchicine** (C) are used for gout, which is not supported by the clinical picture or synovial fluid findings.
- **Leflunomide** (E) is a disease-modifying antirheumatic drug (DMARD) typically used in rheumatoid arthritis and not typically first-line for reactive arthritis unless there is persistent or severe disease.

---

### Conclusion:

Given the clinical presentation and the high WBC count in synovial fluid, the most likely diagnosis is **reactive arthritis**, and the best initial treatment is an NSAID.
