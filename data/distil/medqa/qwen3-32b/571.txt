### Clinical Overview

This case presents a 56-year-old man with a sudden onset of severe back pain, hypertension, and neurological deficits. On physical examination, there is **bilateral loss of pain and temperature sensation** in the lower trunk and extremities, with **preserved proprioception** and **moderate motor weakness (4/5)** in both lower extremities. ### Understanding the Neurological Findings

The key neurological features are:
- **Bilateral loss of pain and temperature** (spinothalamic tract affected).
- **Preserved proprioception** (dorsal columns preserved).
- **Partial motor weakness** (anterior horn cells and corticospinal tract affected).

This pattern is **highly characteristic of anterior cord syndrome**, a condition resulting from occlusion of the **anterior spinal artery**, which supplies the **anterior two-thirds** of the spinal cord. It leads to:
- **Loss of motor function** (corticospinal and anterior horn cells),
- **Loss of pain and temperature** (spinothalamic tract),
- **Preservation of proprioception** (dorsal columns remain intact).

---

### Linking Aortic Dissection to Anterior Cord Syndrome

A **type B aortic dissection** involves the **descending thoracic aorta**, which is in proximity to the **thoracic spinal cord**. The **anterior spinal artery** is a **single, end artery** with **limited collateral blood supply**. A **thrombus or embolus** from the dissection can occlude this vessel, leading to **ischemia and infarction** of the anterior spinal cord. This explains the sudden onset and the specific sensory and motor deficits.

- **C: Spinal cord compression** – Would typically present with **progressive symptoms**, and **imaging would show a mass or swelling**, not just aortic dissection.
- **E: Spinal epidural hematoma** – Could cause **acute cord compression**, but this would present with **severe motor and sensory deficits**, and the **CT would show a hematoma**, not just aortic dissection.
