### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 38-year-old woman with a history of gallstones recently underwent **endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)** for presumed **common bile duct stones**. Now, she presents with **progressive right upper quadrant pain and nausea**. Her vital signs are stable, with no signs of peritonitis, and physical examination reveals **epigastric tenderness**. 

Given the **recent ERCP** and the **nature of the symptoms**, the most likely diagnosis is **post-ERCP acute pancreatitis**, a well-recognized complication of the procedure, especially when there is **pancreatic duct manipulation**.

---

### Key Diagnostic Clues

- **Timing**: ERCP was performed 3 days ago, and the onset of symptoms is consistent with **acute pancreatitis** (typically 24–48 hours post-ERCP).
- **Symptoms**: Nausea and progressive epigastric pain are classic for pancreatitis.
- **No fever or rebound tenderness** suggests **mild** rather than severe or septic pathology.
- **Imaging study confirms the diagnosis**—imaging (likely abdominal ultrasound or CT) would show pancreatic edema or fluid collections consistent with pancreatitis.

---

### Laboratory Considerations

We are asked which lab value is **most likely to be below normal** due to her current condition.

Let's evaluate the options:

- **A. Trypsinogen** – This is a precursor of trypsin and is stored in the pancreas. It is not typically used in the acute setting and is not known to be decreased in acute pancreatitis.

- **B. C-reactive protein (CRP)** – CRP is an **acute phase reactant** that is **elevated** during inflammation. In acute pancreatitis, CRP is typically **high**, not low.

- **C. Lipase** – **Lipase is elevated** in acute pancreatitis due to pancreatic cell injury. So this is **not** the correct answer.

- **D. Glucose** – Acute pancreatitis can cause **hyperglycemia**, not hypoglycemia, due to impaired insulin production. So glucose is **not** likely to be low.

- **E. Calcium** – **Hypocalcemia** is a recognized **late complication** of severe acute pancreatitis. It occurs due to **calcium soap formation** from fat necrosis in the pancreas, reducing ionized calcium levels in the blood. While mild cases may not show hypocalcemia, in more severe cases, **calcium levels fall below normal**.

### Conclusion

The most likely abnormal lab finding in this patient, due to **acute pancreatitis** following ERCP, is **low calcium**.
