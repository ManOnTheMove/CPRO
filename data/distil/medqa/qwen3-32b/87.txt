To interpret the results of the study on dietary fiber intake and coronary heart disease, we need to carefully consider both the **Pearson correlation coefficient** and the **p-value**.

---

### Understanding the Pearson Correlation Coefficient

- The Pearson correlation coefficient (r) is a measure of the **linear relationship** between two variables.
- It ranges from **-1 to +1**:
  - **+1** = perfect positive correlation
  - **0** = no correlation
  - **-1** = perfect negative correlation
- In this study, the coefficient is **0.11**, which is a **very weak positive correlation**. This suggests that as fiber intake increases, heart incidents tend to increase slightly, but the relationship is not strong.

---

### Interpreting the p-value

- The p-value is a measure of **statistical significance**, indicating the probability of observing the data (or more extreme data) if the null hypothesis is true.
- Here, the p-value is **0.006**, which is **less than 0.05**, the standard threshold for statistical significance.
- A low p-value implies that the observed correlation is **unlikely to have occurred by chance**, allowing us to **reject the null hypothesis** that there is no correlation.

---

### Clarifying the Relationship Between Correlation Strength and Significance

- **Statistical significance** ≠ **practical significance**.
- A **small correlation coefficient** can still be **statistically significant** if the sample size is large enough.
- In this case, although the correlation is **weak (0.11)**, the **p-value of 0.006** indicates that this result is **not due to chance**, and thus, the correlation is **statistically significant**.
