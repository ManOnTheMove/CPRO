### Clinical Scenario
A 20-year-old woman presents to a physician after unprotected coitus and is prescribed an emergency contraceptive pill containing **1.5 mg of levonorgestrel**. ---

### Understanding Levonorgestrel as an Emergency Contraceptive

Levonorgestrel is a **synthetic progestin** and is the most commonly used form of **emergency contraception** (EC). It is effective when taken **within 72 hours** of unprotected intercourse, though the sooner it is taken, the more effective it is.

The primary goal of emergency contraception is to **prevent pregnancy** after a single act of unprotected sex. It is important to distinguish between **contraception** (preventing pregnancy) and **abortion** (terminating an existing pregnancy), as EC does **not** cause abortion.

---

### Mechanisms of Action

Levonorgestrel exerts its effects through several **hormonal mechanisms**, but the **primary mechanism** is:

- **Inhibition of ovulation** by suppressing the **luteinizing hormone (LH) surge**, which is essential for the release of the mature egg from the ovary.

This is particularly important in the context of **emergency contraception**, where the timing of the dose is **after intercourse but before ovulation**. If ovulation has not yet occurred, levonorgestrel **delays or prevents it**, thereby preventing fertilization.
