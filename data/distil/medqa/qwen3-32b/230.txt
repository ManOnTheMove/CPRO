### Clinical Interpretation

The clinical picture is most consistent with **allergic contact dermatitis**, a **Type IV hypersensitivity reaction**. This is a delayed-type immune response, typically triggered by **allergens in surgical gloves**, such as **latex proteins**, **rubber accelerators** (e.g., thiurams, carbamates), or **preservatives** in the gloves.

The location (hands and forearms), morphology (erythema, vesicles, and plaques), and the temporal association with a new glove product strongly point toward **contact allergy** as the underlying cause.

### Evaluating the Options

- **A: Bacterial culture** – This is used for suspected **bacterial infection**, but the patient shows no signs of infection such as purulent drainage, fever, or neutrophilic infiltration. This is not the best next step.

- **B: Patch testing** – This is the **gold standard for diagnosing allergic contact dermatitis**. It identifies delayed-type hypersensitivity to specific allergens and is the **next best step** in this case to confirm the suspected allergy to the new gloves.

- **C: KOH examination** – Used to detect **fungal infections**, such as tinea. The clinical presentation here is not consistent with a fungal infection.

- **D: Skin biopsy** – Useful for **atypical or chronic presentations** or to rule out other conditions like psoriasis or eczema. However, in a classic case of allergic contact dermatitis with a clear trigger, it is not the first step.

- **E: Radioallergosorbent test (RAST)** – This detects **IgE-mediated allergies (Type I hypersensitivity)** such as food or pollen allergies. It is **not useful for delayed-type hypersensitivity** (Type IV), which is the mechanism in allergic contact dermatitis.

---

Given the clinical suspicion of **allergic contact dermatitis** due to a new surgical glove, the **next best step in management is patch testing**, which will help identify the specific allergen and guide future avoidance strategies.
