### Clinical Scenario Summary

A 44-year-old homeless man presents to the emergency department after being found intoxicated in a garage. He is acutely altered, with blurry vision and signs of potential toxic ingestion (e.g., covered in bodily fluids, altered mental status). His **serum bicarbonate is significantly reduced at 14 mEq/L**, indicating a **metabolic acidosis**, and he reports **visual disturbances**.

### Differential Diagnosis

Given the clinical context and lab findings, the most likely toxicologic cause is **methanol or ethylene glycol poisoning**. Both substances are metabolized by **alcohol dehydrogenase** into toxic metabolites:

- **Methanol → formic acid** (causes metabolic acidosis and visual disturbances, including blindness)
- **Ethylene glycol → glycolic acid and oxalic acid** (causes metabolic acidosis and renal toxicity)

Both conditions are medical emergencies and require prompt intervention.

---

### Key Clinical Features Supporting Methanol/Ethylene Glycol Poisoning

- **Altered mental status**
- **Visual disturbances** (highly suggestive of methanol)
- **Metabolic acidosis** (bicarbonate 14 mEq/L)
- **History of intoxication** in a homeless individual (at risk for ingestion of non-beverage alcohols)

---

### Treatment Strategy

The **initial treatment** for suspected methanol or ethylene glycol poisoning is to **block the metabolism of the parent toxin** to prevent the formation of toxic metabolites. This is done by **inhibiting alcohol dehydrogenase**.

- **Fomepizole** is the drug of choice for this purpose.
- **Ethanol** may also be used as an alternative if fomepizole is unavailable, as it acts as a competitive inhibitor of alcohol dehydrogenase.

**Dialysis** may be required in more severe cases or if renal failure develops, but it is **not the first-line initial treatment**.
