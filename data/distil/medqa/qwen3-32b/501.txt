This case presents a 63-year-old man with a sudden onset of facial swelling and respiratory distress, occurring after initiating lisinopril, an angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor. The clinical findings—swelling of the lips and inspiratory stridor—point toward airway compromise due to subcutaneous and submucosal edema. The normal C1 esterase inhibitor level is particularly informative, as it rules out hereditary or acquired angioedema associated with C1 deficiency.

---

### Key Clinical Clues:

- **Sudden onset** of symptoms is consistent with an acute allergic or pharmacologic reaction.
- **Lisinopril use** is a red flag for **drug-induced angioedema**, a known but potentially life-threatening side effect of ACE inhibitors.
- **Inspiratory stridor** is a hallmark of upper airway involvement and indicates the need for urgent airway assessment.
- **Normal C1 esterase inhibitor level** excludes hereditary angioedema, which is a differential in recurrent angioedema without urticaria.

---

- **A: Scleredema** – A chronic condition with fibrotic skin changes, typically not presenting with acute swelling or stridor.
- **B: Erysipelas** – A bacterial infection with erythematous, tender, and warm skin, often with fever, not consistent with this presentation.
- **C: Facial lymphedema** – A chronic condition due to lymphatic obstruction, not consistent with acute swelling or airway compromise.
- **D: Drug-induced angioedema** – **Most consistent** with the clinical scenario, especially in the context of recent ACE inhibitor initiation.
- **E: Contact dermatitis** – Presents with rash and pruritus, not with angioedema or stridor.

---

Given the **acute presentation**, **lisinopril use**, **normal C1 esterase inhibitor**, and **clinical signs of airway involvement**, the most likely diagnosis is **drug-induced angioedema**.
