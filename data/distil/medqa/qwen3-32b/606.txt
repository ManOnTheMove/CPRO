### Clinical Context

Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disease of unknown etiology that predominantly affects individuals of African descent. The presence of **hypercalcemia** is a key clue, as sarcoidosis granulomas can produce **1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D**, which increases intestinal calcium absorption and leads to hypercalcemia. This is not typically seen in other granulomatous diseases like tuberculosis.

The **bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy** and **nodular lung opacities** are also classic for sarcoidosis, especially in early stages. The **histopathological hallmark** of sarcoidosis is the presence of **non-caseating granulomas** in the affected tissues.

---

### Immunopathogenesis

The immune response in sarcoidosis is primarily **T-cell mediated**, involving **CD4+ T helper (Th) cells**, particularly the **Th1 subset**. These cells secrete **cytokines**, such as **TNF-α and IFN-γ**, which activate **macrophages** and lead to the formation of granulomas. These granulomas are composed of activated macrophages and lymphocytes and lack the caseation seen in tuberculosis.

This immune response is distinct from:
- **CD8+ T cell activation via MHC I** (relevant for viral infections or cytotoxic responses),
- **IgM binding** (early B cell response in infections),
- **TGF-β from regulatory T cells** (involved in immune suppression),
- **IL-5 from Th2 cells** (involved in allergic or parasitic responses).

---

### Conclusion

Given the clinical and radiographic findings, the most likely diagnosis is **sarcoidosis**, and its pathogenesis is best characterized by **TNF-α secretion by CD4+ T cells**. This cytokine plays a central role in granuloma formation and the persistent inflammatory state seen in this disease.
