### Clinical Scenario Overview

This 55-year-old male has a history of an **inferior wall myocardial infarction (MI)** two months ago, with **stent placement in the right coronary artery**, and is on appropriate secondary prevention medications (aspirin, clopidogrel, atorvastatin, enalapril). He now presents with **dry cough, severe chest pain, fever (38.5°C), tachycardia, tachypnea**, and a **high-pitched, scratchy heart sound** that is best heard when **sitting upright and during expiration**—a classic **pericardial friction rub**.

### Key Findings

- **ECG:** *Diffuse ST elevations* (suggestive of pericarditis)
- **Troponin I:** *0.2 ng/mL* (mildly elevated, not consistent with acute reinfarction)
- **Clinical Signs:** Fever, chest pain, pericardial rub
- **Timing:** *Two months post-MI*

### Differential Diagnosis

#### A. **Cardiac Tamponade**  
- **Unlikely** due to the absence of Beck’s triad (hypotension, jugular venous distension, muffled heart sounds). The patient’s BP is normal (130/80 mm Hg), and the heart sound is described as scratchy, not muffled.

#### B. **Constrictive Pericarditis**  
- A **chronic process** typically presenting with signs of **right-sided heart failure**, such as elevated JVP, Kussmaul’s sign, and peripheral edema. The acute presentation with fever and pericardial rub is **inconsistent** with this diagnosis.

#### C. **Reinfarction**  
- While the patient has a history of MI, **reinfarction** would typically show **localized ST elevations** in the leads corresponding to the affected area (e.g., inferior leads in this case). The **diffuse ST elevations** are more consistent with **pericarditis** than a new infarction. Additionally, the troponin level is only **mildly elevated**, not typical of a reinfarction.

#### D. **Early Infarct-Associated Pericarditis**  
- This typically occurs **within the first few days to weeks** after an MI. The **two-month time frame** in this case is **beyond the acute phase**, making this **less likely**.

#### E. **Dressler Syndrome (Post-Myocardial Infarction Syndrome)**  
- **Most consistent** with this case. Dressler syndrome is an **autoimmune-mediated pericarditis** that typically occurs **weeks to months** after an MI. It presents with:
  - **Pericardial friction rub**
  - **Fever**
  - **Chest pain**
  - **Diffuse ST elevation on ECG**
  - **Mild troponin elevation** due to inflammation

The **timing (two months post-MI)**, **clinical presentation**, and **ECG findings** all align with **Dressler syndrome**.

Given the **clinical context**, **ECG findings**, and **timing**, the **most likely diagnosis** is:
