To determine which conditions can be predicted by analyzing fetal DNA in the mother’s blood, it is important to understand the scope and limitations of **Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing (NIPT)**.

---

### **Understanding the Test**
NIPT is a blood test that analyzes **cell-free fetal DNA** circulating in the maternal bloodstream. It is typically performed from around **10 weeks of gestation** and is used to **screen for certain chromosomal abnormalities**. It is not diagnostic (i.e., it does not confirm a condition) but rather provides a **highly accurate risk assessment**.

---

### **Chromosomal Abnormalities Detected by NIPT**
NIPT is most commonly used to screen for the following **trisomies**:
- **Trisomy 21** (Down syndrome)
- **Trisomy 18** (Edwards syndrome)
- **Trisomy 13** (Patau syndrome)

In addition, it can also determine the **fetal sex**, as it can detect the presence or absence of Y-chromosomes or identify sex chromosome abnormalities (e.g., Turner syndrome, Klinefelter syndrome).

---

### **Conditions Not Detected by NIPT**
- **Spina bifida** and **neural tube defects** are **structural abnormalities**, not chromosomal. They are typically screened via **maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (MSAFP)** testing or **fetal ultrasound**, not through DNA analysis in maternal blood.
- **Ebstein anomaly**, a congenital heart defect, is also a structural issue and is best detected by **fetal echocardiography**, not NIPT.

---
