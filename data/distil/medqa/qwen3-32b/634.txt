### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 65-year-old man with a history of **dental caries** and **poor oral hygiene** presents with **nontender, indurated masses** at the **angle of the jaw** that are **draining thick yellow pus**. Microscopy of the pus reveals **yellow granules filled with bacteria**.

---

### Key Diagnostic Clues

- **Yellow granules in pus** are a hallmark of **actinomycosis**, a chronic, suppurative, granulomatous infection caused by the **anaerobic gram-positive bacillus *Actinomyces israelii***.
- These granules are often referred to as **"sulfur granules"** and contain colonies of *Actinomyces*.
- The **cervicofacial form** of actinomycosis is the most common and typically arises from **dental infections**, especially in patients with poor oral hygiene — a **key risk factor** in this case.
- The **nontender, indurated masses** and **draining abscesses** are typical of the disease's progression.

---

### Treatment Principles

- **Penicillin G** is the **first-line antibiotic** for actinomycosis due to its **excellent in vitro activity** against *Actinomyces* species.
- Treatment usually begins with **high-dose intravenous penicillin G** (e.g., 10–20 million units/day) for **2–4 weeks**, followed by **oral penicillin V or amoxicillin** for **4–6 months** to prevent recurrence.
- In patients with **penicillin allergy**, **tetracycline**, **clindamycin**, or **erythromycin** may be used as alternatives.
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** is **not the preferred agent** for actinomycosis because *Actinomyces* species do **not produce beta-lactamase**, so the **clavulanate component is unnecessary** in this context.
