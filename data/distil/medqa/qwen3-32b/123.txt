A careful review of her clinical presentation and laboratory findings is essential in determining the most likely diagnosis.

- **Surgical context**: Recent femoral neck fracture repair under general anesthesia.
- **Symptoms**:
  - Dyspnea
  - Cyanosis
  - Drowsiness and decreased mental status (oriented only to person)
  - Fever (38.6°C)
  - Tachycardia and tachypnea
  - Hypoxemia (SpO₂ 81% on room air)

  - Petechiae on the anterior chest wall

  - Mild anemia (Hb 10.5 g/dL)
  - Normal leukocyte and platelet counts
  - Mildly elevated creatine kinase (CK 190 U/L)

### Differential Diagnosis Evaluation

**A. Traumatic Rhabdomyolysis**  
- Typically presents with severe muscle pain, myoglobinuria, and very high CK levels (often >10,000 U/L).  
- The CK here is only mildly elevated, and there is no history of crush injury or prolonged immobilization.  
- **Unlikely**.

**B. Fat Embolism**  
- Classic post-traumatic complication of long bone fractures, especially femoral fractures.  
- Clinical triad: **respiratory distress**, **neurological changes**, and **petechiae**.  
- This patient shows all three: dyspnea, altered mental status, and petechiae.  
- The timing (shortly after surgery) and the presence of fever and hypoxemia are also consistent.  
- **Highly likely**.

**C. Thyrotoxic Crisis**  
- Presents with tachycardia, sweating, tremor, and hyperthermia.  
- No prior history of hyperthyroidism is given, and the clinical features do not align.  
- **Unlikely**.

**D. Malignant Hyperthermia**  
- A rare, life-threatening condition triggered by certain anesthetics (e.g., sevoflurane).  
- Characterized by **hyperthermia (often >40°C)**, muscle rigidity, and metabolic acidosis.  
- While the patient was under sevoflurane, the fever is only 101.5°F, and no other signs (e.g., rigidity, acidosis) are described.  
- **Unlikely**.

**E. Meningitis**  
- Presents with fever, headache, neck stiffness, and altered mental status.  
- Petechiae can occur in septicemia, but the absence of neck stiffness and the post-operative context make this less likely.  
- **Unlikely**.

---

### Conclusion

The most consistent and clinically relevant diagnosis is **Fat Embolism Syndrome**, given the **recent femoral fracture**, **classic triad of symptoms**, and the **timing** of symptom onset post-surgery. This condition is a known, albeit rare, complication of long bone surgery and trauma.
