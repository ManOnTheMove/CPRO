### Case Summary

A 6-year-old boy with a **2-year history of asthma** is experiencing **increased shortness of breath**, **nocturnal symptoms**, **increased use of a rescue inhaler (>3 times/week)**, and **exercise intolerance**. On physical examination, he is **fatigued, pale**, and has **expiratory wheezes and crackles** in both lungs. These findings suggest **inadequately controlled asthma**.

The pediatrician initiates **a new once-daily inhaled medication**. Three months later, the child presents with a **painless white plaque on the tongue**, which scrapes off to reveal a **red base with minimal bleeding**. This is a **classic presentation of oral candidiasis**, a known side effect of **inhaled corticosteroids**.

### Medication Analysis

Let’s evaluate the options in the context of the clinical scenario and the side effect:

- **A: Nebulized albuterol** – This is a **short-acting beta-agonist (SABA)**, used for **acute bronchodilation**. It is not a long-term controller and is not associated with oral thrush. **Unlikely**.
  
- **B: Inhaled beclomethasone** – This is a **inhaled corticosteroid (ICS)**, used for **long-term asthma control**. It is **associated with oral candidiasis**, especially if not followed by **rinsing the mouth** after use. **Highly likely**.

- **C: Inhaled ipratropium bromide** – This is an **anticholinergic bronchodilator**, used in **COPD or in acute asthma exacerbations**. It is not a controller and is not linked to oral thrush. **Unlikely**.

- **D: Nebulized theophylline** – This is an **oral/mucosal bronchodilator**, not inhaled in this form. It is not commonly used in children and is not associated with oral thrush. **Unlikely**.

- **E: Inhaled salmeterol** – This is a **long-acting beta-agonist (LABA)**, which is usually **combined with an ICS** for asthma control. It is not associated with **oral candidiasis** and is **not a monotherapy** in asthma management. **Unlikely**.

Given the **increase in symptoms**, **inadequate control with rescue medication**, and the **addition of a once-daily inhaled medication**, the most likely new prescription is an **inhaled corticosteroid** like **beclomethasone**.
