### Pathogenesis of Atherosclerosis

Atherosclerosis is a complex, progressive disease of the arteries. The earliest and most fundamental step in its development is **endothelial cell dysfunction**. This occurs when the endothelium, the inner lining of blood vessels, is damaged or impaired in its function. Endothelial dysfunction is often triggered by various risk factors such as:

- Hyperlipidemia (especially elevated low-density lipoprotein, or LDL cholesterol)
- Hypertension
- Diabetes
- Smoking
- Inflammation

Once endothelial cells are dysfunctional, they become more permeable, allowing **LDL cholesterol to enter the subendothelial space**. This initiates a cascade of inflammatory and immune responses.

---

### Subsequent Steps in Atherosclerosis

Following endothelial dysfunction:

1. **Monocytes** infiltrate the intima and differentiate into macrophages.
2. These macrophages take up oxidized LDL to become **foam cells**, forming a **fatty streak**—the earliest lesion in atherosclerosis.
3. Over time, **smooth muscle cells** migrate from the media to the intima and proliferate, forming a **fibrous cap** over the lipid core.
4. This results in a **fibrous plaque**, which can progress to a **complex atheroma**.
5. If the plaque ruptures, it can lead to **platelet activation and thrombosis**, potentially causing an acute MI.

- **D: Endothelial cell dysfunction** – The foundational and earliest event in atherosclerosis.
- **E: Fibrous plaque formation** – A later stage of atheroma development.
