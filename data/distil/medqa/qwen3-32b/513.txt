### Differential Diagnosis

Given her symptoms and the absence of urgency, frequency, or pain, **stress incontinence** is the most likely diagnosis. This is supported by the following:

- **Leakage with exertion**, not at rest or during the urge to urinate.
- **No signs of overactive bladder** (e.g., no urgency or nocturia).
- **Normal urinalysis and cystometrogram**, ruling out infection or neurogenic bladder.
- **Postmenopausal status**, which is associated with urogenital atrophy and weakening of the pelvic floor.

---

### Management Approach

For stress urinary incontinence, the standard management pathway is **stepwise**, starting with **non-invasive and conservative measures** before progressing to pharmacologic or surgical interventions.

1. **First-line therapy** includes **pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT)**, also known as **Kegel exercises**. These exercises help strengthen the pelvic floor and urethral sphincter, which are essential for controlling urine flow during stress situations.
2. **Pharmacologic options** (e.g., imipramine or bethanechol) are typically reserved for urge incontinence or neurogenic bladder and are not first-line for SUI.
3. **Surgical interventions**, such as **urethropexy** or **tension-free vaginal tape**, are considered when conservative measures fail or symptoms are severe and impacting quality of life.
