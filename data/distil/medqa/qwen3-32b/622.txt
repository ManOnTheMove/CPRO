### Clinical Scenario Summary

A 58-year-old man is being treated with **clindamycin** for a **pulmonary abscess**. During his hospitalization, he develops **abdominal pain**, **bloody diarrhea**, and **leukocytosis (WBC = 14,000)**. The teaching round highlights that the pathogen responsible produces a **multi-unit toxin** that causes **actin depolymerization**, leading to **cell death and mucosal necrosis**.

### Key Diagnostic Clues

- **Recent clindamycin use** is a red flag for **Clostridioides difficile (C. difficile)** infection. Clindamycin disrupts normal gut microbiota, allowing C. difficile to proliferate.
- **Symptoms** (abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea, leukocytosis) are consistent with **C. difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD)** or **pseudomembranous colitis**.
- The **pathogenesis** described (toxin causing actin depolymerization and mucosal damage) aligns with **C. difficile toxins A and B**, particularly **Toxin B**, which is known to interfere with the actin cytoskeleton in intestinal epithelial cells.

---

### Treatment Options and Rationale

We are asked to identify the **preferred treatment** for this condition from the given options:

- **A: Continue Clindamycin**  
  ❌ **Incorrect.** Clindamycin is a risk factor for C. difficile infection and should be **discontinued immediately**.

- **B: Metronidazole**  
  ✅ **Correct.** Metronidazole is a first-line treatment for **mild to moderate** C. difficile infections. It is well-absorbed orally and achieves effective concentrations in the colon. It is not affected by the gut flora disruption caused by antibiotics.

- **C: Penicillin G**  
  ❌ **Incorrect.** Penicillin is not effective against C. difficile. It is used for other bacterial infections (e.g., streptococcal infections).

- **D: Tetracycline**  
  ❌ **Incorrect.** Tetracycline is not a standard treatment for C. difficile. Its use may worsen the condition by further altering gut flora.

- **E: Erythromycin**  
  ❌ **Incorrect.** Erythromycin is a macrolide antibiotic and is not recommended for C. difficile. Like other broad-spectrum antibiotics, it may worsen the infection.
