### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 42-year-old man with no significant medical history presents with symptoms of **headache, nausea, and dizziness**. These symptoms began while he was working on his car and recurred upon resuming the activity. His **vitals are stable**, and the **neurologic exam is unremarkable**. He states the symptoms improved temporarily after drinking water but returned when he went back to his work.

---

### Key Considerations

The central clue in this case is the **activity at the time of symptom onset**: working on his car. This raises the possibility of **exposure to environmental toxins**, particularly **carbon monoxide (CO)**, a common hazard when working in enclosed spaces with an idling car engine. 

**Carbon monoxide** is a **colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas** that binds to hemoglobin with much higher affinity than oxygen, leading to **hypoxia**. Classic symptoms of CO poisoning include:
- **Headache**
- **Dizziness**
- **Nausea**
- **Confusion**
- **Fatigue**

The **reappearance of symptoms** when the patient resumes working on his car is a strong indicator of **re-exposure to a toxic agent**, likely CO. The **temporary relief** he experienced indoors may be due to a **reduction in CO exposure**.

---

### Management Plan

The **next best step** in the management of a suspected carbon monoxide poisoning is the **administration of 100% oxygen via non-rebreather mask**. This accelerates the **displacement of CO from hemoglobin**, reducing the risk of complications such as delayed neurologic sequelae.

While **CO-oximetry** would confirm the diagnosis, it is **not immediately necessary** if clinical suspicion is high and the patient is being treated empirically. This is especially true in a setting with a **strong clinical correlation** and **normal vital signs and exam**.
