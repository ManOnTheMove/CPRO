  - **Wrist drop** suggests **lead-induced peripheral neuropathy**.
  - **Bluish line above the gums** is a **lead line (Burton’s line)**.
  - **Microcytic anemia** is a hallmark of **chronic lead exposure**.

  - **Neurobehavioral changes** (irritability, academic decline) are consistent with **lead neurotoxicity**.

### Management Considerations

#### **Chelation Therapy Options**

- **Dimercaptosuccinic acid (succimer)**: Oral, well-tolerated, **first-line agent** for chelation in children, especially when renal function is impaired.
- **Dimercaprol (BAL)**: Used in combination with **calcium disodium EDTA (CaNa2EDTA)** for **severe poisoning** (usually >80 mcg/dL), but has more side effects and is **not first-line** in children.
- **EDTA (calcium disodium)**: Effective chelator, but **nephrotoxic**, especially in patients with **baseline renal dysfunction**.

#### **Renal Function and Chelation Safety**

The patient has **mild renal impairment** (elevated creatinine), making **EDTA use potentially unsafe**. Therefore, **succimer** is the **preferred chelating agent** in this case.

Given the **elevated lead level (72 mcg/dL)**, **clinical signs of lead toxicity**, and **renal impairment**, the **most appropriate next step** is to initiate **chelation therapy with succimer**, which is **safe, effective, and well-tolerated in children**, particularly when renal function is compromised.
