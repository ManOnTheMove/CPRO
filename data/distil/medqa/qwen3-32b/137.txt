### Case Summary

A 7-year-old girl, who recently emigrated from India, is undergoing a routine well-child examination. She is asymptomatic, with normal vital signs and physical findings. Laboratory results show a **positive interferon-γ release assay (IGRA)**, indicating **latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI)**. Her **Hepatitis B surface antigen is negative**, and she has **positive antibody**, likely from vaccination or prior exposure. A **chest x-ray is normal**, and no other abnormalities are noted.

---

### Key Considerations

1. **Interpretation of IGRA Positive**  
   The IGRA (e.g., QuantiFERON-TB Gold or T-SPOT.TB) is a blood test that detects TB infection by measuring the immune response to TB antigens. A **positive IGRA in a child from a high TB prevalence area like India** strongly suggests **latent TB infection**.

2. **Clinical Implications of LTBI in Children**  
   - Children with LTBI are **asymptomatic** and **not contagious**.
   - However, there is a **higher risk of progression to active TB** in early childhood (especially under age 5) or in immunocompromised individuals.
   - Therefore, **treatment is recommended** to prevent future reactivation.

3. **Diagnostic and Treatment Guidelines**  
   - **Tuberculin skin test (TST)** is **not necessary** if IGRA is already positive.
   - **Sputum testing** (e.g., acid-fast stain) is **not indicated** in this case since the x-ray is normal and she is asymptomatic.
   - For **LTBI treatment**, the **standard regimen** in children is:
     - **Isoniazid monotherapy** for 9 months.
     - **Rifampin** for 4 months is an alternative in some cases, especially if adherence to a longer course is a concern.

4. **Exclusion of Active TB**  
   - The **normal chest x-ray** and **lack of symptoms** make active TB **unlikely**.
   - Therefore, a **multi-drug regimen** like **isoniazid, rifampin, ethambutol, and pyrazinamide** (option E) is **not appropriate**, as this is used for **active TB**.

### Evaluation of Answer Choices

- **A: Perform acid-fast stain on induced sputum**  
  Not necessary; sputum is hard to obtain in children, and the child is asymptomatic with normal x-ray.

- **B: Administer isoniazid** ✅  
  Correct next step. Isoniazid is the standard treatment for LTBI in children.

- **C: Reassurance and follow up**  
  Not sufficient. Treatment is recommended to reduce the risk of progression to active TB.

- **D: Perform tuberculin skin test**  
  Redundant; IGRA is more specific and already positive.

- **E: Administer isoniazid, rifampin, ethambutol and pyrazinamide**  
  This is a **regimen for active TB**, not LTBI. Not indicated in this case.
