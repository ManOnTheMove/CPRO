### Pathogenesis of Edema in Nephrotic Syndrome

In the context of **nephrotic syndrome**, the primary mechanism of edema is **loss of plasma proteins**, especially **albumin**, into the urine. This results in a **reduction of plasma oncotic pressure**.

The **Starling forces** govern the movement of fluid across capillary walls:

- **Oncotic pressure** (colloid osmotic pressure) in the plasma is mainly due to **albumin**.
- When albumin levels drop significantly (as seen here), the **oncotic pressure gradient across the capillary wall decreases**.
- This **reduced oncotic pressure** allows fluid to **leak out of the capillaries into the interstitial space**, resulting in **edema**.

This mechanism is **distinct** from other causes of edema such as:

- **Increased capillary hydrostatic pressure** (e.g., in heart failure)
- **Increased capillary permeability** (e.g., in sepsis or burns)
- **Lymphatic obstruction** (e.g., filariasis or post-surgical changes)
- **Increased interstitial oncotic pressure** (e.g., in some rare protein-accumulating disorders)
