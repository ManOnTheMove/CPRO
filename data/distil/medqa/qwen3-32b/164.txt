#### 2. **Clinical Features**
- **Fever, malaise, weight loss** – seen in many tropical infections.
- **Generalized lymphadenopathy** – highly suggestive of **African trypanosomiasis**.
- **Hepatosplenomegaly** – can occur in both visceral leishmaniasis and late-stage trypanosomiasis.
- **Pancytopenia (anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia)** – can be seen in both diseases due to bone marrow suppression or infiltration.

#### 3. **Disease-Specific Features**
- **African Trypanosomiasis (Tsetse fly vector):**
  - Caused by *T. brucei rhodesiense* (East Africa) or *T. brucei gambiense* (West Africa).
  - Characterized by **fever, headache, malaise**, and **generalized lymphadenopathy**.
  - **Winterbottom’s sign** (posterior cervical lymphadenopathy) is classic but more common in *gambiense*.
  - **Early stage** is hemolymphatic, with fever and lymphadenopathy.
  - **Late stage** involves CNS involvement and more severe systemic symptoms.
  - **Incubation period** is 1–4 weeks for *rhodesiense* and 1–3 months for *gambiense*.
  - **Pancytopenia** occurs in later stages due to bone marrow involvement.

- **Visceral Leishmaniasis (Sand fly vector):**
  - Caused by *Leishmania donovani*.
  - Presents with **prolonged fever, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly, and pancytopenia**.
  - **Incubation period** is **3–6 months**, which aligns with the patient's timeline (returned 6 months ago).
  - **Lymphadenopathy** is less prominent and not generalized.
  - More **chronic in nature**, with gradual onset.

#### 4. **Differential Diagnosis by Vector**
- **Tsetse fly (A):** African trypanosomiasis.
- **Sand fly (B):** Visceral leishmaniasis.
- **Freshwater snail (C):** Schistosomiasis – less likely due to acute presentation and lack of rash or urogenital symptoms.
- **Anopheles mosquito (D):** Malaria – possible, but fever would likely be more cyclical, and pancytopenia is not as pronounced.
- **Triatomine bug (E):** Chagas disease – not relevant in Kenya.
