To determine the best screening test for a new infectious disease, the primary goal is to identify **as many true cases as possible** during the initial phase. This is especially important in public health, where early detection can prevent further spread and enable timely intervention.

---

### Understanding the Terminology

Let’s briefly define the key terms in the context of diagnostic testing:

- **Sensitivity**: The ability of a test to correctly identify individuals who have the disease.  
  $$
  \text{Sensitivity} = \frac{\text{True Positives}}{\text{True Positives} + \text{False Negatives}}
  $$

- **Specificity**: The ability of a test to correctly identify individuals who do **not** have the disease.  
  $$
  \text{Specificity} = \frac{\text{True Negatives}}{\text{True Negatives} + \text{False Positives}}
  $$

- **True Positive Rate (TPR)**: This is **exactly the same** as sensitivity.  
  $$
  \text{TPR} = \text{Sensitivity}
  $$

- **False Positive Rate (FPR)**: The proportion of individuals without the disease who are incorrectly identified as having it. This is the complement of specificity.

- **Negative Predictive Value (NPV)**: The probability that an individual who tests negative truly does not have the disease. This is influenced by disease prevalence and is not directly a test characteristic.

---

### Applying This to the Question

In **screening**, the priority is to **maximize detection of true cases**, even if it means some **false positives** (which can be resolved by confirmatory testing). This means we want to **minimize false negatives**—people who have the disease but are not identified by the test.

Thus, the **most important characteristic** is **high sensitivity**, or equivalently, **a high true positive rate**.

---

### Evaluating the Options

- **A: Highly sensitive test** – This is correct. It ensures that most people who have the disease are correctly identified.
- **B: Highly specific test** – This is **not** ideal for screening. High specificity reduces false positives but increases false negatives.
- **C: Low false positive rate** – This is the same as high specificity, which is **not** the priority in screening.
- **D: High true positive rate** – This is the **same as sensitivity**, so this is also correct.
- **E: Low negative predictive value** – This is **undesirable** in screening; we want a high NPV to ensure a negative test result is reliable.
