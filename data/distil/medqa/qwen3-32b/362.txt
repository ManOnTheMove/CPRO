### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 25-year-old male arrives in the emergency department after a **motor vehicle collision**, presenting with **chest pain**, **shortness of breath**, and **confusion**. His **vital signs** are concerning:

- **Blood Pressure**: 94/54 mmHg – **hypotension**
- **Heart Rate**: 170 bpm – **tachycardia**
- **Respirations**: 12/min – within normal limits
- **Oxygen Saturation**: 90% on room air – **hypoxia**

After a **14-gauge needle is placed in the 2nd intercostal space on the left**, the patient shows **improvement**: **blood pressure rises** and **heart rate decreases**.

---

### Interpretation of Findings

The **needle placement at the 2nd intercostal space** is the **standard site for needle decompression** in the management of **tension pneumothorax**. This procedure is typically performed in trauma patients with signs of respiratory compromise and hemodynamic instability.

The **immediate improvement in vitals** (increased blood pressure and decreased heart rate) is a **strong indicator** that the patient was suffering from **tension pneumothorax**, and the needle decompression **successfully relieved** the pressure.

---

### Next Step in Management

Needle decompression is a **temporary and emergency intervention**. Once the tension is relieved, the next critical step is to **secure a more definitive air leak management**. This is achieved by placing a **chest tube (tube thoracostomy)**, which allows for **ongoing drainage of air and/or fluid** and **prevents recurrence** of the tension pneumothorax.

---

### Evaluation of Other Options

- **A: Chest radiograph** – While useful for diagnosis, **imaging is not the next step in an unstable trauma patient**. Treatment must come first.
- **B: Pericardiocentesis** – This is used for **cardiac tamponade**, but the **improvement after needle decompression** suggests the issue was **not pericardial**.
- **C: Supportive therapy and observation** – **Inappropriate** given the **trauma setting and the hemodynamic instability**.
- **D: Thoracotomy** – A **more invasive procedure** reserved for **massive hemorrhage or failed chest tube** placement, not the **immediate next step** here.

---

### Conclusion

The best next intervention is to **place a chest tube (tube thoracostomy)** to fully decompress and stabilize the patient after the initial needle decompression.
