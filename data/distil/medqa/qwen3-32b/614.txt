This case involves a burn patient who has developed a wound infection 10 days after admission, presenting with blue-green pus and a culture showing aerobic gram-negative rods. The clinical and microbiological findings strongly suggest *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* as the causative agent, a common pathogen in burn wound infections and known for producing a blue-green pigment called pyocyanin.

---

### Key Considerations:

- **Clinical Context**: The patient has a large surface area of first-degree burns and is immunocompromised due to diabetes, both of which increase the risk of opportunistic infections like *Pseudomonas*.
- **Microbiology**: *P. aeruginosa* is a gram-negative rod that is often resistant to multiple antibiotics. It is known to cause severe wound and systemic infections in burn patients.
- **Therapeutic Goals**: The treatment must be effective against *P. aeruginosa* and ideally include a combination regimen to prevent resistance, especially in a high-risk patient.

---

### Antibiotic Options:

- **Azithromycin with rifampin (A)**: These are ineffective against *P. aeruginosa*. Azithromycin is a macrolide used for atypical respiratory pathogens and some gram-positive organisms. Rifampin is primarily used for mycobacterial infections.
  
- **Piperacillin with gentamicin (B)**: This combination is considered a **gold standard** for treating serious *P. aeruginosa* infections. Piperacillin is a broad-spectrum penicillin with activity against many gram-negative organisms, including *P. aeruginosa*. Gentamicin, an aminoglycoside, provides synergistic activity and enhances the bactericidal effect. This is a **first-line choice** in such infections.

- **Piperacillin alone (C)** or **Ticarcillin (D)**: Both antibiotics are effective against *P. aeruginosa*, but monotherapy may not be sufficient for serious or resistant infections. Combination therapy is often preferred in critical settings.

- **Azithromycin alone (E)**: As discussed, not effective against *P. aeruginosa*.

---

### Conclusion:

Given the high suspicion of *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and the need for effective and synergistic treatment in a burn patient, the best option is **piperacillin with gentamicin**.
