### Pathophysiological Mechanism

Spinal anesthesia blocks **sympathetic nerve fibers**, leading to **vasodilation** in the anesthetized region. If the block extends **high into the thoracic spinal cord**, it can also block **sympathetic outflow to the heart and blood vessels**, resulting in:

- **Massive vasodilation** → **severe hypotension**
- **Loss of cardiac sympathetic tone** → **bradycardia**
- **Reflex bradycardia** due to baroreceptor response to hypotension
- **Reduced cardiac output** → **loss of consciousness**
- **Diaphoresis and flushing** are due to **intact sympathetic activity above the block**, particularly in the upper body.

This constellation of symptoms is classic for a **high spinal or total spinal block**, which is a **rare but serious complication of spinal anesthesia** when the local anesthetic spreads too high in the subarachnoid space.
