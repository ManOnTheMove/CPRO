To solve this question, we must determine the correct epidemiological term for the **25% protective proportion** described in the scenario.

---

### Understanding the Scenario

- **Study Design**: A multicenter cohort study comparing a **vaccinated group** to a **non-vaccinated (control) group** in elderly individuals.
- **Outcome**: Incidence of shingles (herpes zoster).
- **Observed Rates**:
  - **Vaccinated group**: 15% developed shingles.
  - **Control group**: 20% developed shingles.
- **Key Statement**: The 25% protective effect is **independent of baseline risk**, i.e., it remains constant even if baseline risk is 40% or 10%.

---

### Calculating the 25% Protective Effect

The 25% protective proportion is calculated as follows:

$$
\text{Relative Risk Reduction (RRR)} = \frac{\text{Risk in Control Group} - \text{Risk in Treated Group}}{\text{Risk in Control Group}} \times 100
$$

$$
\text{RRR} = \frac{20\% - 15\%}{20\%} \times 100 = \frac{5\%}{20\%} \times 100 = 25\%
$$

This is a **relative measure** of risk reduction, and it is **independent of baseline risk**, which matches the description in the question.

---

### Why Not the Other Options?

- **A: Event Rate** – This refers to the proportion or rate of events in a specific group. Here, the 15% and 20% are event rates, but the 25% is not.
- **B: Risk Difference** – This is the absolute difference in event rates: 20% - 15% = 5%. The 25% is not a risk difference.
- **C: Attributable Difference** – This is typically used in the context of attributable risk in causal inference, not in vaccine efficacy.
- **E: Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR)** – This is also 5%, as above. Again, not 25%.

---

### Final Answer

The **25% protective proportion** is a **relative risk reduction**, and it is **independent of baseline risk**, which is why it remains constant across different risk groups. This makes **D** the correct answer.
