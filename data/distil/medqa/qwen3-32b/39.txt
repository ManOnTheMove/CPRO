When considering the regeneration of the alveolar epithelium in the context of acute lung injury, it is essential to identify the key cell type involved in this process. The alveolar epithelium is composed primarily of two cell types:

- **Type I pneumocytes**: These are thin, flat cells that cover approximately 95% of the alveolar surface area and are critical for gas exchange. However, they have limited proliferative capacity and are generally not involved in regeneration.
- **Type II pneumocytes**: These are smaller, cuboidal cells that are interspersed among Type I cells. Crucially, they are the only alveolar epithelial cells capable of division and differentiation into Type I cells, making them the primary cells responsible for **regeneration of the alveolar epithelium** after injury such as that caused by a viral infection.

---

In addition to their regenerative role, **Type II pneumocytes** have another vital function: the **synthesis and secretion of pulmonary surfactant**. Surfactant is a complex mixture of lipids and proteins that coats the inner surface of the alveoli. Its primary function is to reduce surface tension, thereby preventing alveolar collapse during exhalation and improving lung compliance.
