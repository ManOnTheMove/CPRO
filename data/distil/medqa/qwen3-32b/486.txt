### Case Summary

A 39-year-old woman presents with **acute, severe epigastric pain** that is unrelieved by ibuprofen and associated with **nausea and emesis**. She has a history of **diabetes** and a **prior appendectomy**, and she is **overweight**. On physical examination, she is in **acute distress** with **diffuse abdominal tenderness**, and her **vital signs are stable**.

Laboratory findings include:

- **Significantly elevated lipase** (650 U/L; normal < 160 U/L)
- **Mildly elevated liver enzymes** (AST 387 U/L, ALT 297 U/L, ALP 168 U/L)
- **Hypocalcemia** (Ca²⁺ 6.9 mg/dL)
- **Normal white blood cell count and hemoglobin**

These findings are **highly suggestive of acute pancreatitis**, which is typically defined by **two of the following three criteria**:

1. **Abdominal pain consistent with pancreatitis**
2. **Elevated pancreatic enzymes** (amylase or lipase ≥ 3× upper limit of normal)
3. **Characteristic findings on imaging**

This patient meets **two of the three criteria**: **abdominal pain** and **elevated lipase**, which is **four times the upper limit of normal** (650 U/L), so the **clinical diagnosis of acute pancreatitis is well established**.

---

### Objective of the Next Step in Workup

The **next step in the workup** is to **assess the severity of the pancreatitis** and to **identify the underlying cause**. This is essential for:

- Determining the need for **intensive care** or **early surgical intervention**
- Identifying the **etiologic factor** (e.g., gallstones, alcohol, hypertriglyceridemia, etc.)

---

### Imaging Modalities and Rationale

- **Abdominal Ultrasound (D)**:
  - First-line for **gallstone detection**, a common cause of pancreatitis.
  - However, it may be **limited in obese patients** or when **bowel gas obscures the pancreas**.
  - It does **not evaluate the extent of pancreatic inflammation or necrosis**, which is critical for assessing severity.

- **Abdominal CT with IV contrast (A)**:
  - **Gold standard** for evaluating the **anatomy and severity** of acute pancreatitis.
  - Allows for **assessment of pancreatic necrosis**, **peripancreatic fluid collections**, and **complications** such as pseudocysts.
  - Especially important in this patient, who already has **hypocalcemia**, a **marker of severe disease**.

- **Abdominal CT without IV contrast (B)**:
  - Less sensitive for evaluating pancreatic inflammation and necrosis.
  - Not the best choice in this setting.

- **Abdominal MRI (C)**:
  - Useful for **long-term follow-up** or **chronic pancreatitis**, but **not first-line in acute settings**.
  - **MRCP** (magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography) is better for biliary causes, but not readily available in most ER settings.

- **Upright chest and abdominal radiograph (E)**:
  - **Low sensitivity** for diagnosing pancreatitis.
  - May be used to rule out **free air** (perforation) or **pneumoperitoneum**, but **not diagnostic** for pancreatitis.

Given the **high lipase**, **severe epigastric pain**, and **hypocalcemia**, the patient likely has **severe acute pancreatitis**, and the **next step is to assess the severity and complications**. 

While **ultrasound may be considered** for evaluating **gallstones**, in this case, the **clinical picture is strong enough** to proceed directly to **CT with IV contrast** for a **comprehensive evaluation of the pancreas and surrounding structures**.
