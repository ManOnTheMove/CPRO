In this clinical scenario, a 68-year-old man with **advanced dementia** is scheduled for an **elective cholecystectomy**. He now refuses the procedure, becomes agitated, and demands to leave. The **designated medical power of attorney (POA)**, who had already signed the consent forms, is present.

---

### Key Considerations:

1. **Capacity and Dementia**:
   - **Advanced dementia** typically results in a **loss of decision-making capacity**, especially for complex medical decisions like surgery.
   - However, **capacity is decision-specific**, and even individuals with dementia may have moments of lucidity or express preferences that should be taken into account.
   - The patient's current **verbal refusal** must be evaluated in the context of his **current cognitive state** and **capacity** to understand the nature, risks, and benefits of the surgery.

2. **Role of the Medical Power of Attorney (POA)**:
   - The **POA** has legal authority to make decisions on the patient’s behalf **when the patient lacks capacity**.
   - However, **a prior consent does not override the patient’s current expressed wishes if they are made while the patient has capacity**.

3. **Current Behavior and Valid Refusal**:
   - The patient is **yelling and demanding to leave**, which may reflect **agitation, confusion, or pain** rather than a reasoned refusal.
   - Given the **advanced dementia**, it is **uncertain** whether the patient currently has the **capacity to make a valid decision** about the surgery.

---

### Evaluating the Options:

- **A: Consult psychiatry for capacity evaluation**  
  This is the **most appropriate next step**. Given the patient’s **advanced dementia** and **current refusal**, a formal **capacity evaluation** is necessary to determine whether the patient can understand the surgery and its consequences. If he lacks capacity, the **POA’s prior consent remains valid**.

The **most appropriate next step** is to **consult psychiatry for a capacity evaluation** to determine whether the patient currently has the capacity to refuse the surgery. This is essential before proceeding with the surgery or honoring the patient’s current wishes.
