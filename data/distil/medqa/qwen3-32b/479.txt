### Clinical Scenario Overview

The patient is a 37-year-old male presenting with **progressive dysphagia** to both solids and liquids, **regurgitation of undigested food**, and **difficulty belching**. He reports that **lifting his neck helps** him swallow better, and despite being on **omeprazole** for acid reflux, he still has **persistent heartburn**. Importantly, **esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD)** showed **no mechanical obstruction or inflammation**, ruling out structural or inflammatory causes.

---

### Differential Diagnosis

Key features of this case point toward a **motility disorder** of the esophagus. The most likely diagnosis is **achalasia**, a condition characterized by:

- **Failure of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) to relax** in response to swallowing.
- **Aperistalsis** (lack of coordinated peristaltic contractions) in the **distal two-thirds of the esophagus**.
- Gradual **progression of symptoms**, which fits the 1-year timeline.
- **Regurgitation of undigested food** and **difficulty belching** are hallmark signs.
- **Neck elevation helping swallow** is a classic compensatory mechanism.

---

### Role of Esophageal Manometry

Esophageal manometry is the **gold standard** for diagnosing esophageal motility disorders. In **achalasia**, the typical findings include:

- **Aperistalsis** in the **distal two-thirds** of the esophagus.
- **Failure of the LES to relax** (incomplete relaxation).
- Often, **increased resting LES pressure** (hypertension), although this is not always required for diagnosis.

These findings are **specific to achalasia** and differentiate it from other motility disorders like **diffuse esophageal spasm**, **scleroderma**, or **GERD**.

### Evaluation of Answer Choices

- **A: Hypomotility with lower esophageal sphincter incompetence** – Incorrect. In achalasia, the LES is **not incompetent**, it is **hypertonic and fails to relax**.
  
- **B: Aperistalsis in the distal two-thirds of the esophagus with incomplete lower esophageal relaxation** – **Correct**. This is the classic manometric finding in achalasia.

- **C: Normal amplitude, uncoordinated, simultaneous esophageal contractions** – Incorrect. This describes **diffuse esophageal spasm**, not achalasia.
