### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 22-year-old woman presents with a **5-kg weight gain**, **irregular menstrual cycles**, and **amenorrhea** for the past 2 months. She has **obesity (BMI 30)**, **hair loss**, and **no evidence of pregnancy**. Laboratory results show **elevated luteinizing hormone (LH: 32 mIU/mL)**, **normal prolactin (11 ng/mL)**, and **normal thyroid function**. A **progestin challenge test** induces **withdrawal bleeding**, indicating that the endometrium is estrogen-primed.

---

### Key Diagnostic Clues

1. **Obesity and Amenorrhea**:
   - Obesity is a well-known cause of **anovulatory cycles**.
   - Adipose tissue contains **aromatase**, an enzyme that converts androgens to estrogens.
   - This results in **chronic estrogen exposure** without the cyclical changes needed for ovulation.

2. **Elevated LH**:
   - In anovulatory cycles, **progesterone is absent**, so the negative feedback on the hypothalamic-pituitary axis is incomplete.
   - This can lead to **elevated LH** as a compensatory response.
   - However, this is a **consequence**, not the **primary cause**, of the anovulation.

3. **Progestin Challenge Test**:
   - Positive result confirms that the endometrium is **estrogen-primed**, suggesting that **estrogen is being produced**.
   - Given the absence of ovulation, this estrogen is most likely coming **from non-ovarian sources**, such as **adipose tissue**.

---

### Evaluation of Answer Choices

- **A: Progesterone in corpus luteum**  
  - Not relevant because the patient is anovulatory and has **no corpus luteum**.

- **B: Androgens in the adrenal gland**  
  - While increased adrenal androgens can cause hirsutism and acne, the patient does **not show signs of virilization**, and the primary issue is **amenorrhea**, not hirsutism.
  - Adrenal androgen excess is more characteristic of conditions like **congenital adrenal hyperplasia**, which is not supported by the clinical picture.

- **C: Estrogen in adipose tissue**  
  - This is a **strong candidate**.
  - Obesity leads to **increased aromatase activity** in adipose tissue, converting androgens to **estrogens**.
  - These **chronically elevated estrogen levels** disrupt the normal hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis, leading to **anovulation** and **amenorrhea**.
  - The **progestin challenge test** being positive confirms **estrogen priming** of the endometrium.
  - The **elevated LH** is a **secondary consequence** of the lack of progesterone feedback in anovulatory cycles.

- **D: Follicle stimulating hormone in the adenohypophysis**  
  - There is **no evidence of elevated FSH** in the lab results.
  - Elevated FSH would suggest **ovarian failure**, which is **not consistent** with this young woman's presentation.

- **E: Dopamine in the hypothalamus**  
  - Dopamine **inhibits prolactin**. The patient has **normal prolactin**, so dopamine is **not the issue**.

---

### Conclusion

The **most likely cause** of the patient’s **amenorrhea** is the **increase in estrogen production from adipose tissue** due to **obesity**. This leads to **anovulation** and **disruption of the normal menstrual cycle**, despite the body’s attempt to compensate with elevated LH.
