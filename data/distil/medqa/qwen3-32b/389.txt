The clinical presentation of this 9-year-old girl includes **difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)**, **generalized muscle rigidity**, and a **characteristic "smiling" appearance** (likely due to **risus sardonicus**), all of which are classic signs of **tetanus**. ### Pathophysiology of Tetanus

The disease is caused by the **neurotoxin tetanospasmin**, one of the most potent toxins known. This toxin is secreted by *Clostridium tetani* and travels via the **peripheral nerves to the central nervous system**, particularly the **spinal cord** and **brainstem**. Its key action is to **inhibit the release of inhibitory neurotransmitters**, such as **GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)** and **glycine**, from **inhibitory interneurons** in the CNS. This leads to **uncontrolled muscle contractions and spasms**.

---

### Mechanism of Action of Tetanospasmin

Tetanospasmin exerts its effect by **cleaving synaptobrevin (also known as VAMP)**, a protein that is a critical component of the **SNARE complex** involved in **neurotransmitter vesicle fusion** with the presynaptic membrane. By cleaving synaptobrevin, the toxin **prevents the release of GABA**, which normally inhibits muscle contraction. The absence of this inhibition results in **hyperexcitability of motor neurons**, leading to the **characteristic spastic paralysis** seen in tetanus.
