The clinical scenario presents a 15-year-old girl with **hypertension**, **hypokalemia**, and **elevated morning renin activity and aldosterone levels**, all of which point toward **hyperaldosteronism**. ---

### Pathophysiological Context

- **Primary hyperaldosteronism** (e.g., Conn’s syndrome) is due to autonomous aldosterone overproduction by the adrenal glands, independent of the renin-angiotensin system. This leads to **low plasma renin activity** and **high aldosterone**.
- **Secondary hyperaldosteronism**, on the other hand, results from **activation of the renin-angiotensin system** due to conditions like **renal artery stenosis**, **congestive heart failure**, or **juxtaglomerular cell tumors**. In such cases, **both renin and aldosterone are elevated**.

In this patient, the **elevated renin** suggests **secondary hyperaldosteronism**, and the **hypokalemia** is consistent with the effects of aldosterone promoting potassium excretion in the distal nephron.

---

### Clinical Evaluation and Diagnostic Clues

Given the young age of the patient, **acquired causes** like renal artery stenosis are less likely. A more plausible explanation is a **rare but relevant cause**: a **juxtaglomerular cell tumor**.

- These tumors are **mesenchymal** in origin and arise from the **juxtaglomerular apparatus** of the kidney.
- They secrete **renin**, leading to **excessive angiotensin II** and **aldosterone**, resulting in **hypertension** and **hypokalemia**.
- The **histopathological hallmark** of such a tumor is the presence of **pleomorphic modified smooth muscle cells**, typically in the **renal cortex**, as described in **option D**.
