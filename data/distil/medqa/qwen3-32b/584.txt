---

### **Clinical Presentation and Key Findings:**

- **Hypertension (180/140 mmHg):** Suggests a chronic, possibly hormone-mediated cause.
- **Hypokalemia (3.2 mEq/L):** Low potassium levels are often associated with excessive renal potassium excretion.
- **Low plasma renin levels:** Indicates a disruption in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
- **Adrenal lesion (3 cm, right side):** Suggests a localized, possibly functional tumor.

---

### **Pathophysiology:**

The combination of **hypertension**, **hypokalemia**, and **low renin** is classic for **primary hyperaldosteronism**, also known as **Conn’s syndrome**. In this condition, there is an overproduction of **aldosterone** from the **zona glomerulosa**, the outermost layer of the adrenal cortex.

- **Aldosterone** promotes sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion in the distal tubules of the kidney.
- Excess aldosterone leads to:
  - **Hypertension** (from sodium and fluid retention),
  - **Hypokalemia** (from increased potassium excretion),
  - **Suppression of renin** (due to negative feedback from high sodium levels).

---

### **Anatomy and Function of the Adrenal Gland:**

- **Zona Glomerulosa (A):** Produces **aldosterone**.
- **Zona Fasciculata (B):** Produces **glucocorticoids** (e.g., cortisol).
- **Zona Reticularis (C):** Produces **androgens**.
- **Adrenal Medulla (D):** Produces **catecholamines** (e.g., epinephrine, norepinephrine).
- **Posterior Pituitary (E):** Not part of the adrenal glands and not relevant here.

---

### **Why Other Options Are Incorrect:**

- **B (Zona fasciculata):** Tumors here lead to Cushing’s syndrome, with symptoms like central obesity, moon face, and hirsutism—not hypokalemia or low renin.
- **C (Zona reticularis):** Tumors would cause androgen excess and virilization, not the described electrolyte or blood pressure abnormalities.
- **D (Adrenal medulla):** Pheochromocytomas cause episodic hypertension, palpitations, and sweating—not sustained hypokalemia.
- **E (Posterior pituitary):** Not involved in aldosterone production or adrenal pathology.

---

The patient’s clinical presentation is most consistent with **primary hyperaldosteronism**, likely due to a **zona glomerulosa** adenoma. This explains the **hypertension**, **hypokalemia**, and **low renin levels**.
