This case presents a 3-week-old male infant with a head tilt to the left, resistance to head rotation, and a firm, well-circumscribed mass in the left lower neck. These clinical findings are classic for **congenital muscular torticollis**, a condition caused by shortening or fibrosis of the **sternocleidomastoid muscle**, often due to **birth trauma**.

---

### Pathophysiology and Clinical Features

Congenital muscular torticollis typically results from **intrauterine malposition** or **birth trauma**, particularly during **vaginal delivery**. The affected sternocleidomastoid muscle becomes fibrotic and shortened, leading to the characteristic head tilt and rotation. A palpable **fibromatous mass** (often called a “torticollis tumor”) is frequently present in the first few months of life.

---

### Predisposing Factors

The question asks for the **strongest predisposing factor** for this condition. The options include:

- **A. Fetal macrosomia**
- **B. Cesarean delivery**
- **C. Intrauterine exposure to haloperidol**
- **D. Polyhydramnios**
- **E. Preterm birth**

Let’s analyze each:

- **Fetal macrosomia (A)**: A large fetal size increases the risk of **birth trauma** during vaginal delivery, particularly **shoulder dystocia** or stretching of the sternocleidomastoid muscle. This is a **well-established risk factor** for congenital muscular torticollis.
  
- **Cesarean delivery (B)**: While C-section may **reduce** the risk of birth trauma, it is not a **predisposing factor**—in fact, it is likely **protective** against this condition.

- **Intrauterine exposure to haloperidol (C)**: Haloperidol is an antipsychotic that can cause dystonic reactions, but this is **not a recognized cause** of congenital muscular torticollis.

- **Polyhydramnios (D)**: Excess amniotic fluid may allow for greater fetal movement, but it is not directly linked to torticollis.

- **Preterm birth (E)**: Prematurity is more commonly associated with **neuromuscular** disorders rather than **musculoskeletal** conditions like torticollis. In fact, preterm infants are **less likely** to have torticollis due to smaller size and less likelihood of birth trauma.
