### Clinical Scenario Summary

A 51-year-old man, with a history of hypertension and diabetes, was admitted after a gunshot wound to the abdomen. He underwent a diagnostic laparotomy without evidence of intestinal injury. Post-operatively, he developed fever and non-bloody diarrhea. A sigmoidoscopy revealed **pseudomembranous plaques**, a classic sign of **Clostridioides difficile (C. diff)** infection.

---

### Pathophysiological Insight

C. diff infection is a well-known complication of **antibiotic use**, particularly those that **disrupt the normal gut microbiota**, allowing C. diff to proliferate and release toxins that cause colonic inflammation and pseudomembranous colitis.

Among the antibiotics commonly associated with this condition, **clindamycin** stands out as a **classic culprit**. Clindamycin belongs to the **lincosamide class** of antibiotics and works by **binding to the 50S ribosomal subunit**, thereby **inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis**.

---

### Antibiotic Enzyme Inhibition Overview

Let’s evaluate the options in the context of enzyme inhibition:

- **A: 30S ribosomal subunit** – Inhibited by **aminoglycosides** and **tetracyclines**. Not typically linked to C. diff.
- **B: 50S ribosomal subunit** – Inhibited by **clindamycin**, **macrolides**, and **chloramphenicol**. **Clindamycin** is a well-known cause of C. diff.
- **C: Dihydropteroate synthetase** – Inhibited by **sulfonamides**. Diarrhea may occur, but not pseudomembranous colitis.
- **D: DNA-dependent RNA polymerase** – Inhibited by **rifampin**. Not associated with C. diff.
- **E: DNA gyrase** – Inhibited by **fluoroquinolones**. While fluoroquinolones can cause C. diff, the enzyme they inhibit is **not 50S**.

---

### Conclusion

Given the clinical picture of **pseudomembranous colitis**, the most likely underlying antibiotic is **clindamycin**, which inhibits the **50S ribosomal subunit**.
