These symptoms, combined with a gait disturbance and specific findings on a head impulse test (corrective saccades), point toward a vestibular disturbance. The patient is currently receiving intravenous **vancomycin** and **gentamicin**, both of which are known to have **ototoxic potential**, especially when used in combination with loop diuretics such as **furosemide**, which she is also taking.

---

### Key Findings and Interpretation:

- **Bilateral tinnitus and vertigo** are classic signs of **ototoxicity**, particularly when caused by **aminoglycosides** like gentamicin. While vancomycin is not an aminoglycoside, it can also contribute to ototoxicity, especially in combination with furosemide, which enhances the risk by reducing inner ear perfusion and increasing drug concentration in the cochlea and vestibular system.

- **Corrective saccades** during the head impulse test indicate **vestibular hypofunction**, consistent with damage to the vestibular system—likely due to toxicity.

- **No fever or signs of acute infection**, and **normal vital signs** make **suppurative labyrinthitis** or **acute otitis media** less likely.

- **Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)**, which involves dislodged otoliths, typically presents with **positionally triggered vertigo** and **unilateral** symptoms, not bilateral tinnitus or saccadic eye movements.

- **Ménière's disease**, which involves endolymphatic hydrops and decreased endolymph resorption, is **episodic** and usually unilateral, and does not align with the acute and progressive presentation here.

- **Delirium** is a broad term for acute confusion and does not explain the specific vestibular and visual findings described.

Given the **combination of ototoxic medications** (gentamicin, vancomycin, and furosemide), the **clinical presentation** of bilateral tinnitus, vertigo, and gait instability, and the **findings on the head impulse test**, the most likely diagnosis is **medication-induced ototoxicity**.
