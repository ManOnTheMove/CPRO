### Differential Diagnosis

The key clinical features—**sudden hypoxia**, **neurologic changes**, **petechiae**, and **recent femoral fracture**—strongly suggest a diagnosis of **fat embolism syndrome (FES)**. This is a well-recognized complication of **long bone fractures**, particularly of the **femur**, and typically presents **24–72 hours post-injury**.

The **classic triad** of FES includes:
- **Neurologic symptoms** (e.g., confusion, coma)
- **Respiratory distress** (e.g., hypoxia, tachypnea)
- **Petechiae** (most commonly on the **conjunctiva**, **axillae**, **neck**, and **chest**)

The **mild anemia** and **thrombocytopenia** also align with the systemic effects of fat embolism, which can cause bone marrow suppression and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
