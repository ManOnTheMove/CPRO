This 45-year-old woman presents with **involuntary passage of urine** accompanied by **urgency**, occurring both **day and night**. Notably, these episodes are **not associated with physical exertion, laughing, or coughing**, which helps **exclude stress urinary incontinence**. The physical and pelvic exams, including a **negative Q-tip test**, further support this exclusion.

Her **cystometric studies** are critical to understanding the underlying issue:
- **Residual volume**: 10 mL (within normal limits)
- **Sensation-of-fullness volume**: 190 mL (slightly low)
- **Urge-to-void volume**: 700 mL (significantly **above** normal range of 400–500 mL)
- **Involuntary contractions**: **Present** (normal: absent)

These findings are **highly suggestive of detrusor overactivity**, the hallmark of **overactive bladder (OAB)** with **urge incontinence**. The bladder is not leaking due to pressure changes (as in stress incontinence) but rather due to **involuntary contractions** that occur when the bladder is relatively full.

### Treatment Considerations

Given the **absence of structural or neurological abnormalities** and the **clear evidence of detrusor overactivity**, the most appropriate first-line treatment is a **muscarinic antagonist**.

**Muscarinic antagonists** (e.g., oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin) are **standard first-line pharmacologic therapy** for urge incontinence. They work by **blocking the muscarinic receptors** in the bladder, reducing involuntary contractions and increasing bladder capacity.
