In this scenario, a 36-year-old woman is admitted to the hospital following a drug overdose involving alcohol, benzodiazepines, and barbiturates. Her condition is improving, and she is being evaluated for psychiatric concerns. Three days later, a **physician colleague** who is also a **neighbor and childhood friend** of the patient approaches the treating physician and inquires about her condition. The key ethical and legal issue here is **patient confidentiality**, governed by laws such as HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) in the U.S.

---

### Analysis of the Options:

- **Option A**: *"You can find the information in the electronic medical record."*  
  This is **inappropriate** because it assumes the colleague has the right to access the patient’s records, which they do not unless specifically authorized by the patient or under exceptional circumstances (e.g., imminent harm to the patient or others). This would be a **breach of confidentiality**.

- **Option B**: *"Why don't you go ahead and ask her directly? She is currently in the psychiatric ward."*  
  While this might be a well-intentioned suggestion, it **shifts responsibility** to the friend without addressing the ethical obligation of the physician to protect patient privacy. It also assumes the patient is capable and willing to share her condition, which may not be the case given her psychiatric status.

- **Option C**: *"Her urine toxicology screening was positive for alcohol, benzodiazepines, and barbiturates."*  
  This **directly discloses sensitive medical information** and is a clear **violation of patient confidentiality**. Even though the colleague is a physician, this does not grant them access to the patient’s medical details without consent.

- **Option D**: *"I'm not the right person to ask. Please ask her primary care physician."*  
  This **does not resolve the ethical issue** and may instead **shift the responsibility** to another physician. It does not address the colleague’s concern and still implies that the information should be shared.

- **Option E**: *"I understand your concern, but I am unable to provide information about this situation."*  
  This is the **most appropriate response**. It **acknowledges the colleague's concern**, **shows empathy**, and **upholds the patient's right to confidentiality** without disclosing any medical details.

---

### Conclusion:

The treating physician must prioritize the **ethical duty of confidentiality** and the **legal obligations under HIPAA**, regardless of the requester’s personal relationship to the patient or their professional status. The correct and most ethical response is to politely decline to share any information.
