An 11-month-old boy presents with **intermittent episodes of severe abdominal pain**, **non-bloody vomiting**, and **reddish, mucoid stools**. ### Differential Diagnosis and Reasoning:

1. **Intussusception**:
   - **Classic presentation**: Intermittent severe abdominal pain (crying episodes), drawing up of legs, vomiting, and **currant jelly stools** (mucoid, bloody).
   - **Age**: Most common in 3–24 months, with peak incidence at 6–12 months.
   - **Supporting features**:
     - Leukocytosis (inflammatory response).
     - Ultrasound likely showed the **"target sign"** or **"bull's eye"** appearance.
     - No free air on x-ray suggests **no perforation yet**.
   - **Management**: First-line treatment is **air or contrast enema** for **non-operative reduction** if the patient is stable. This is **option A**.

2. **Appendicitis** (Option B):
   - Less common in infants.
   - Typically presents with **low-grade fever**, **anorexia**, **localizing pain**, and **vomiting**.
   - **Not typically associated with mucoid bloody stools**.
   - **Appendectomy** would not be first-line in this setting.

3. **Meckel’s Diverticulum** (Option C):
   - Can cause **acute intestinal obstruction** or **gastrointestinal bleeding**.
   - **Painless rectal bleeding** is more typical.
   - **Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan** is used to detect **ectopic gastric mucosa**, but this is **not the immediate next step** in a child with **acute obstructive symptoms**.

4. **Pyloric Stenosis** (Option D):
   - Usually presents with **projectile non-bilious vomiting**, often after 3–5 months of age.
   - **No abdominal pain or blood in stool** is typical.
   - **Pyloromyotomy** is not indicated here.

5. **Sepsis or Infection** (Option E):
   - The **fever and leukocytosis** could suggest sepsis.
   - However, **symptoms are more consistent with a mechanical obstruction**.
   - **Antibiotic therapy** (ampicillin, tobramycin, and metronidazole) is not first-line in a child with **no signs of peritonitis or perforation**.
