To address this question, we need to understand the concept of **statistical power** in hypothesis testing. 

---

### **What is Statistical Power?**

Statistical power is defined as the probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis. In other words, it is the likelihood that a study will detect an effect when there truly is one. Mathematically, it is expressed as:

$$
\text{Power} = 1 - \beta
$$

Where:
- $\beta$ is the **Type II error rate** (the probability of failing to reject a false null hypothesis).
- $1 - \beta$ is the **power** of the test.

---

### **Factors That Influence Power**

Several factors influence the power of a statistical test:

1. **Sample size** – Larger sample sizes increase power.
2. **Effect size** – Larger true differences between groups increase power.
3. **Significance level (α)** – A lower α (e.g., 0.01 instead of 0.05) reduces power, while a higher α increases it.
4. **Variability in the data** – Less variability increases power.

In this question, the investigators are **constrained to keep the significance level (α) at 5%**, so any changes involving α (like increasing or decreasing it) are not permissible.
