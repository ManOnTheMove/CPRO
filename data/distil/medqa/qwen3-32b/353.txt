### Clinical Scenario Overview

This 70-year-old woman underwent a total knee arthroplasty and developed a **deep vein thrombosis (DVT)** in her right leg after hospital discharge. She was re-admitted and started on **unfractionated heparin**. However, **the clot extended** to involve multiple veins in the right leg and a new clot appeared in the left leg. Importantly, her **platelet count dropped significantly** from 195,000/uL to 83,000/uL over five days.

---

### Key Clues in the Case

- **Heparin therapy** was initiated after DVT diagnosis.
- **Extension of existing clots** and **new clots** developed.
- **Thrombocytopenia** (low platelet count) occurred during heparin therapy.
- **No prior history** of bleeding or other coagulation disorders is mentioned.

These findings are classic for **Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT)**, an immune-mediated complication of heparin therapy.

---

### Pathophysiology of Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT)

HIT is caused by the formation of **antibodies against a complex of heparin and platelet factor 4 (PF4)**. This antibody-antigen complex activates platelets, leading to:

- **Platelet aggregation and consumption**, resulting in **thrombocytopenia**.
- **Activation of the coagulation cascade**, promoting **thrombosis**.

This paradoxically increases the risk of **new or extending clots**, even in the presence of anticoagulant therapy. HIT typically presents 5–10 days after heparin initiation, aligning with the timeline in this case.

### Why the Other Options Are Incorrect

- **A: Excessive irreversible inactivation of the cyclo-oxygenase enzyme**  
  This describes the mechanism of aspirin or other NSAIDs, not heparin. It is unrelated to the thrombocytopenia or clot extension in this patient.

- **C: Decreased hepatic synthetic function leading to antithrombin deficiency**  
  Antithrombin deficiency is a rare inherited or acquired condition. It would not explain the acute thrombocytopenia or the response to heparin.

- **D: Deposition of immune complex nuclear antigens in kidneys**  
  This is characteristic of **lupus nephritis** or other immune complex-mediated diseases, not relevant to the acute setting of a post-operative patient on heparin.

- **E: Inherited mutation in clotting factor V, which cannot be degraded by activated protein C**  
  This describes **Factor V Leiden**, a hereditary thrombophilia. It is not an immune-mediated reaction and does not cause thrombocytopenia.
