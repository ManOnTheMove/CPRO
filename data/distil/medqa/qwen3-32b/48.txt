### Clinical Overview

This case involves a **3-year-old Cuban-American male** with a history of **recurrent Pseudomonas and Candida infections**, **low IgG levels**, and a **CT scan showing absence of the thymus**. The **laboratory results show no electrolyte abnormalities**. These findings are crucial in narrowing down the differential diagnosis among immunodeficiency disorders.

---

### Key Clues in the Case

1. **Recurrent Infections**:
   - Both **Pseudomonas** and **Candida** are opportunistic pathogens typically seen in patients with **cellular and humoral immunodeficiencies**.
   - **Pseudomonas** is associated with **T-cell dysfunction**, while **Candida** infections are often seen in **T-cell and/or Th17 cell deficiencies**.

2. **Low IgG Levels**:
   - This indicates a **defect in B-cell function or maturation**, or a **lack of T-cell help** for B-cell maturation and antibody production.

3. **Absent Thymus (Thymic Aplasia)**:
   - The thymus is the primary site of **T-cell maturation**.
   - Absence of the thymus strongly suggests a **T-cell immunodeficiency**.

4. **No Electrolyte Abnormalities**:
   - This is important because **DiGeorge syndrome** is classically associated with **hypoparathyroidism**, which leads to **hypocalcemia**. The **absence of this electrolyte abnormality** may suggest a **milder or atypical presentation** of DiGeorge syndrome.

5. **Age of Onset (3 years old)**:
   - **Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)** typically presents **within the first few months of life** with **life-threatening infections**. A 3-year-old child surviving with SCID is **unusual** unless there is **some residual immune function** or **delayed diagnosis**.

---

### Analysis of Options

- **A. Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)**:
  - Characterized by **severe deficiency in both T and B cells**.
  - May present with **thymic hypoplasia or aplasia**, but **very severe infections** usually appear **in infancy**.
  - A 3-year-old with SCID would be **rare**, unless it's an atypical or **milder variant**, but the **clinical course and survival** at 3 years make this **less likely**.

- **B. X-linked Agammaglobulinemia (XLA)**:
  - Involves **B-cell deficiency** with **normal T-cell function**.
  - The **thymus is present** in XLA.
  - This is **inconsistent** with the CT scan showing **thymic absence**, so **excluded**.

- **C. DiGeorge Syndrome**:
  - Caused by **22q11.2 deletion**, resulting in **thymic and parathyroid hypoplasia or aplasia**.
  - Presents with **T-cell deficiency**, **low IgG** (due to impaired B-cell maturation), and **recurrent infections**.
  - While **hypocalcemia** is common due to **hypoparathyroidism**, the **absence of electrolyte abnormalities** in this case may suggest **mild or compensated hypoparathyroidism**.
  - The **absent thymus** is a **hallmark of DiGeorge syndrome**, making it a **strong candidate**.

- **D. Isolated IgA Deficiency**:
  - Most common primary immunodeficiency.
  - Characterized by **low IgA**, but **IgG and IgM are normal**, and **thymus is normal**.
  - Inconsistent with **low IgG** and **absent thymus**, so **excluded**.

- **E. Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)**:
  - Usually presents in **late childhood or adulthood**.
  - Characterized by **low IgG and IgA**, with **normal T-cell function**.
  - Thymus is **present** in CVID.
  - This does **not match** the **absent thymus** or **early age of presentation**.

The most consistent diagnosis with the clinical findings—**recurrent infections, low IgG, and absent thymus**—is **DiGeorge syndrome**.
