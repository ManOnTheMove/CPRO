### Differential Diagnosis

Given the **paroxysmal, brief, and stimulus-induced nature** of the pain, the most likely diagnosis is **trigeminal neuralgia (TN)**. TN is characterized by:

- **Sudden, severe, electric-shock-like or stabbing pain**
- **Triggered by innocuous stimuli** (e.g., touch, chewing, speaking)
- **Short duration** (seconds to minutes)
- **Unilateral distribution** along one or more branches of the trigeminal nerve

The absence of neurological deficits and normal imaging findings (not mentioned here but assumed absent due to no focal findings) supports a diagnosis of classic TN rather than secondary causes like multiple sclerosis or tumors.

---

### Treatment Considerations

The **first-line medical treatment** for trigeminal neuralgia is **carbamazepine**, an anticonvulsant that reduces the hyperexcitability of the trigeminal nerve. It is **highly effective** in managing pain in the majority of patients and is the **standard initial therapy** before considering more invasive options.

Let’s briefly evaluate the other options:

- **Amitriptyline (A):** A tricyclic antidepressant used for neuropathic pain, but **not first-line** for TN.
- **Microvascular decompression (B):** A **surgical option** considered for **medication-resistant cases**, not the first step in a young woman with no contraindications to drug therapy.
- **Botulinum toxin injection (C):** **Not FDA-approved** for TN and not a standard treatment.
- **Radiofrequency rhizotomy (D):** A **destructive procedure** used in **elderly or medically unfit patients** who cannot tolerate medications.
