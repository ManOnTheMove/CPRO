A 32-year-old construction worker sustained a crush injury to his right leg from a block of concrete. The injury was not removed for 30 minutes, leading to severe soft tissue damage, including pulpified muscle and absent distal pulses. The key concern is **rhabdomyolysis** due to extensive muscle damage.

### Pathophysiology

Crush injuries can lead to **rhabdomyolysis**, a condition where damaged muscle cells release intracellular contents into the bloodstream. These include:

- **Myoglobin**
- **Potassium**
- **Phosphate**
- **Creatine kinase (CK)**

The release of **myoglobin** is particularly significant. It can cause **myoglobinuria**, which is characterized by **dark red or brown urine**. However, if myoglobin levels are high enough in the **plasma**, the serum itself may appear **red-tinted**, which is a rare but notable finding.

---

- FeNa > 2% is **suggestive of acute tubular necrosis (ATN)**, which can result from **myoglobin-induced nephrotoxicity**.
- However, FeNa is not a **direct** or **early** marker of rhabdomyolysis itself. It is more a **secondary finding** in the context of renal dysfunction.
- While plausible, it is **not the most direct** expected abnormality in the initial laboratory workup.

**B: Urine osmolality > 500 mOsmol/kg**  
- High urine osmolality is a feature of **prerenal azotemia**, not ATN.
- In ATN, urine osmolality is **lower** due to impaired concentrating ability.

**C: Hypercalcemia**
