The clinical presentation of this 55-year-old man—bloody emesis, black stools, a history of cirrhosis and alcohol abuse, and physical signs of jaundice and ascites—is highly suggestive of upper gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding secondary to ruptured esophageal varices. Esophageal varices are a common and life-threatening complication of portal hypertension, which is frequently seen in patients with cirrhosis.

---

### Pathophysiology and Management Context

In cirrhosis, portal hypertension leads to the formation of collateral vessels, particularly in the esophagus. These varices can rupture due to increased portal venous pressure, leading to acute, potentially massive upper GI bleeding. The primary goals of acute management include:
1. **Stabilization** (fluid resuscitation, blood transfusion).
2. **Control of bleeding**.
3. **Prevention of rebleeding**.

In the acute setting, **vasoactive agents** are used to reduce portal pressure by decreasing splanchnic blood flow. Among these, **somatostatin analogs** (e.g., octreotide) are preferred due to their efficacy and favorable side effect profile.

---

### Mechanism of Action of Octreotide

**Octreotide**, a synthetic somatostatin analog, acts by:
- **Binding to somatostatin receptors**, particularly SSTR2 and SSTR5.
- **Reducing splanchnic blood flow**, which in turn lowers portal venous pressure.
- **Inhibiting the release of vasodilatory substances** such as glucagon and vasoactive intestinal peptide.
- **Constricting the splanchnic vasculature**, which is critical in the management of variceal bleeding.

These actions make octreotide a cornerstone in the acute treatment of variceal hemorrhage, especially in combination with endoscopic interventions such as band ligation.

### Evaluation of Other Options

- **B: Inhibition of gastric parietal cell H+/K+ ATPase** – Proton pump inhibitors are used in **peptic ulcer bleeding**, not variceal bleeding.
- **C: Reversible block of histamine H2 receptors** – Similar to PPIs, H2 blockers are used for **acid-related** GI bleeding, not variceal.
