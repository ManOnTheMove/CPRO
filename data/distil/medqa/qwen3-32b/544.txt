Erectile dysfunction (ED) is a common condition in aging men and is often treated pharmacologically with drugs that enhance the physiological mechanisms involved in achieving and maintaining an erection. The physiological basis of an erection involves the release of nitric oxide (NO) in the corpus cavernosum of the penis. NO activates guanylate cyclase, leading to the production of cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP). cGMP causes smooth muscle relaxation and vasodilation, allowing increased blood flow into the penile tissue.

However, cGMP is broken down by the enzyme phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE5), which is primarily responsible for degrading cGMP in the corpus cavernosum. Inhibiting PDE5 leads to increased levels of cGMP, thereby prolonging the effects of NO and improving erectile function.

---

Common medications used to treat ED, such as sildenafil (Viagra), tadalafil (Cialis), and vardenafil (Levitra), are all PDE5 inhibitors. These drugs do not increase testosterone or estrogen levels, nor do they block estrogen or testosterone receptors. They also do not inhibit 5-alpha reductase, which is involved in the conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone and is targeted in conditions like benign prostatic hyperplasia, not ED.

---

Therefore, the most accurate description of the drug’s mechanism of action in this scenario is the inhibition of **cGMP phosphodiesterase**.
