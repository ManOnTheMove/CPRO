### Pathophysiology of Anemia in Chronic Kidney Disease

Chronic kidney disease (CKD), especially in its advanced stages (e.g., ESRD), leads to **anemia of chronic disease**, but in this case, the underlying mechanism is primarily due to **reduced erythropoietin (EPO) production**. EPO is a hormone produced mainly by the kidneys, and it is essential for the **stimulation of erythroid progenitor cells** in the bone marrow to produce red blood cells (RBCs).

With renal failure, **EPO levels fall**, leading to **decreased RBC production**, which is reflected by the **low reticulocyte count**. This is not a nutritional deficiency or a hemolytic process, as the MCV is normal and platelet count is within range.

---

### Appropriate Treatment

The **standard treatment** for anemia in patients with CKD is the use of **erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs)**, such as **epoetin alfa** or **darbepoetin alfa**, which are synthetic forms of EPO. These agents **bind to and activate EPO receptors** on erythroid progenitor cells, thereby **stimulating RBC production** and improving hemoglobin levels.
