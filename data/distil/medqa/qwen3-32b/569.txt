### Pathophysiology of Marfan Syndrome in the Aorta

Marfan syndrome is characterized by widespread connective tissue abnormalities. One of the most serious and life-threatening complications is **aortic root dilation**, which can progress to **aortic dissection or rupture**. The underlying histopathological process in the aorta involves **degeneration of the medial layer of the arterial wall**.

This degeneration, known as **cystic medial degeneration**, is defined by:

- **Loss of elastic fibers** in the media
- Accumulation of **mucopolysaccharides** (especially hyaluronic acid)
- **Fragmentation and disorganization** of smooth muscle and elastic fibers
- **Thinning of the vessel wall**, leading to increased risk of aneurysm formation and dissection

This is not an inflammatory process and does not involve immune cell infiltration like neutrophils or lymphocytes. It is a **structural defect** of the extracellular matrix, not an acute or granulomatous inflammatory response.
