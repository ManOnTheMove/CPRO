### Leukocyte Extravasation and α4-Integrin

Leukocyte migration from the bloodstream to tissues involves several well-defined steps:

1. **Rolling Adhesion**: Leukocytes initially roll along the endothelial surface via interactions between selectins on the endothelium and their ligands on leukocytes.
2. **Firm Adhesion**: This is mediated by integrins such as **α4β1 (VLA-4)** and **α4β7**, which bind to **vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 (VCAM-1)** and **mucosal addressin cell adhesion molecule-1 (MAdCAM-1)**, respectively, on endothelial cells.
3. **Transmigration (Diapedesis)**: Leukocytes cross the endothelial barrier through a process involving interactions with proteins like **PECAM-1 (CD31)**.

---

### Role of α4-Integrin Inhibition

The drug in question inhibits α4-integrin, a key component of VLA-4 and α4β7. This inhibition **prevents the firm adhesion** of leukocytes to endothelial cells. Without this firm adhesion, leukocytes are unable to undergo transmigration and reach the site of infection in the tissues.

This mechanism explains why the patient is more prone to **upper respiratory tract infections** and **urinary tract infections**—the immune cells cannot effectively reach these areas to combat pathogens.
