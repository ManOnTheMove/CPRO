Gallstones can be broadly classified into **cholesterol stones**, **pigment stones**, and **mixed stones**. The color of the stones is a key clinical clue to their etiology.

---

### Understanding Gallstone Color and Composition

- **Cholesterol stones** are typically **yellow or pale green** in color and are often associated with bile supersaturated with cholesterol.
- **Pigment stones**, on the other hand, can be **black** or **brown**:
  - **Black pigment stones** are more commonly associated with **hemolytic disorders** and **chronic hemolysis**, leading to increased unconjugated bilirubin.
  - **Brown pigment stones**, as seen in this case, are often linked to **biliary tract infections**, especially those caused by **bacteria that produce the enzyme beta-glucuronidase**.

---

### Role of Beta-Glucuronidase in Brown Pigment Stones

The enzyme **beta-glucuronidase**, produced by certain bacteria, deconjugates **bilirubin diglucuronide**, releasing free bilirubin. This free bilirubin can then polymerize and form **brown pigment stones**, especially in the **bile ducts**. The most common bacteria associated with this process include:

- **Escherichia coli**
- **Klebsiella pneumoniae**
- **Enterococcus species**

These bacteria are often implicated in **chronic biliary tract infections**, such as **cholecystitis** or **cholangitis**, which promote stone formation.
