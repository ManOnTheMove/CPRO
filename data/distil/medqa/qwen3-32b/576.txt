### Clinical Scenario Analysis

This 37-year-old woman, with a history of **three previous cesarean deliveries**, presents in **active labor at 35 weeks' gestation** with **no prenatal care**. She delivers **vaginally**, but the **placenta is not delivered**. A **manual separation of the placenta** results in **profuse vaginal bleeding**, and the **uterus is firm and nontender at the level of the umbilicus**.

---

### Key Features of the Case

- **History of multiple cesarean deliveries** → increases risk for placental abnormalities, especially **placenta accreta**.
- **Retained placenta** after vaginal delivery → not typical of normal placental separation.
- **Manual removal of placenta causes massive bleeding** → indicates **abnormal placental adhesion**.
- **Firm, nontender uterus** → rules out uterine atony and suggests **retained placental tissue** rather than postpartum hemorrhage due to uterine relaxation.

---

### Evaluation of Diagnostic Options

**A. Ruptured vasa previa**  
- Vasa previa involves **fetal vessels over the cervical os**, and typically presents with **painless, bright red bleeding** during labor.  
- It is not associated with **retained placenta** or **postpartum hemorrhage due to placental adhesion**.  
- **Not consistent with the clinical scenario**.

**B. Uterine rupture**  
- A history of **multiple C-sections** is a risk factor.  
- However, **uterine rupture typically presents with severe pain, fetal distress**, and signs of **hemorrhagic shock**.  
- The patient’s **nontender abdomen and stable vital signs** make this **less likely**.

**C. Placenta previa**  
- Involves **placenta covering the cervix**, leading to **painless bleeding**.  
- Often diagnosed **pre-labor**, and **not typically associated with retained placenta**.  
- A **vaginal delivery in a patient with placenta previa** is **contraindicated** and **highly risky**, but the patient delivered vaginally.  
- **Not the most likely diagnosis** given the **retained placenta and post-delivery hemorrhage**.

**D. Placenta accreta** ✅  
- This condition involves **abnormal adherence of the placenta to the uterine wall**, often due to **previous C-sections** or **placenta previa**.  
- It leads to **retained placenta**, **difficulty in manual removal**, and **profuse bleeding**.  
- The **firm, nontender uterine fundus** is **not consistent with uterine atony**, but is **consistent with retained placental tissue**.  
- **This is the most likely diagnosis** given the **clinical presentation and risk factors**.

**E. Abruptio placentae**  
- Characterized by **premature separation of the placenta**, with **abdominal pain**, **uterine tenderness**, and **fetal distress**.  
- The patient’s **nontender abdomen and reactive fetal heart rate** do **not support this diagnosis**.

---

### Conclusion

Given the **history of multiple C-sections**, **retained placenta**, and **profuse bleeding following manual separation**, the most likely diagnosis is **placenta accreta**.
