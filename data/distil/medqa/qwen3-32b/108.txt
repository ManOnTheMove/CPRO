To determine the appropriate need for anticoagulation in a patient with atrial fibrillation, the CHADS₂ score is commonly used. This score helps estimate the risk of stroke by evaluating the presence of specific clinical risk factors. Each component of the acronym is assigned a certain number of points:

- **C**ongestive heart failure: 1 point  
- **H**ypertension: 1 point  
- **A**ge ≥75 years: 1 point  
- **D**iabetes mellitus: 1 point  
- **S**troke or transient ischemic attack (TIA): 2 points  

---

Let's apply this to the patient in question:

- **Congestive heart failure (C):** No evidence of congestive heart failure in the patient’s past medical history or on physical examination. **0 points**
- **Hypertension (H):** The patient has a history of high blood pressure. **1 point**
- **Age ≥75 (A):** The patient is 73 years old, which is less than 75. **0 points**
- **Diabetes mellitus (D):** The patient has type II diabetes. **1 point**
- **Stroke or TIA (S):** The patient has **never** had a stroke or TIA. **0 points**

---

Adding these together:  
1 (hypertension) + 1 (diabetes) + 0 (age) + 0 (stroke/TIA) + 0 (CHF) = **2 points**

The CHADS₂ score is therefore **2**, which places the patient at moderate risk for stroke, and typically indicates that anticoagulation should be considered.
