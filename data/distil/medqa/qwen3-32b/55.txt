---

### Physiological Basis of the Condition

When a person consumes a meal high in fat, the **duodenum** detects the presence of these lipids and releases **cholecystokinin (CCK)**. This hormone is critical in the digestive process and acts on the gallbladder and pancreas:

- **Gallbladder Contraction:** CCK stimulates the gallbladder to contract, releasing stored bile into the small intestine to emulsify fats.
- **Sphincter of Oddi Relaxation:** It also causes relaxation of the sphincter of Oddi, allowing bile and pancreatic enzymes to flow into the duodenum.
- **Pancreatic Enzyme Secretion:** CCK stimulates the release of digestive enzymes from the pancreas.

In a person with gallstones, especially if the **cystic duct is partially obstructed**, the gallbladder contraction induced by CCK can lead to **pain** (biliary colic) due to increased intraluminal pressure or stone migration.

---

### Analysis of the Hormone Options

- **A. Somatostatin:** Inhibits the release of several digestive hormones, including CCK and gastrin. It suppresses gallbladder contraction, so it is not the cause of postprandial symptoms.
  
- **B. Cholecystokinin (CCK):** Directly stimulates gallbladder contraction in response to fat. In a patient with gallstones, this contraction leads to the pain described. **This is the hormone most directly responsible for the postprandial aggravation of symptoms.**

- **C. Gastrin:** Stimulates gastric acid secretion, not gallbladder contraction. Not relevant to this case.

- **D. Pepsin:** A digestive enzyme, not a hormone. Does not play a role in gallbladder function.

- **E. Secretin:** Stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate secretion and inhibits gastric acid. Not involved in gallbladder contraction.
