This clinical scenario describes a 73-year-old woman with progressive memory loss, disorientation, impaired abstract reasoning, and social withdrawal, all of which are hallmark features of **dementia**, most likely **Alzheimer’s disease**, given the patient’s age and symptom progression.

---

### Key Clinical Features:
- **Memory impairment** (both short- and long-term)
- **Disorientation** (oriented only to person)
- **Impaired abstract reasoning**
- **Apathy and indifference** to cognitive decline
- **Social withdrawal**

These features are classic for **Alzheimer’s disease**, a neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the accumulation of **amyloid plaques** and **neurofibrillary tangles**, leading to the death of neurons, particularly in the **cortex and hippocampus**.

---

### Neurotransmitter Involvement in Alzheimer’s Disease:
One of the most well-established biochemical abnormalities in Alzheimer’s is the **loss of cholinergic neurons** in the **basal forebrain**, particularly the **nucleus basalis of Meynert**. These neurons project to the **cortex and hippocampus**, areas critical for **learning and memory**.

- **Acetylcholine** is the primary neurotransmitter of these neurons.
- The **cholinergic hypothesis of Alzheimer’s** posits that the cognitive decline seen in the disease is, in part, due to a **deficiency in acetylcholine**.
- This is why **cholinesterase inhibitors** (e.g., donepezil, rivastigmine) are commonly used in the treatment of Alzheimer’s — they increase acetylcholine levels in the brain.

---

- **E: Dopamine** – Deficiency is more characteristic of **Parkinson’s disease**, which presents with motor symptoms (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia), not the cognitive symptoms described here.
