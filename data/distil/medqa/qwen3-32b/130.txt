The clinical scenario presents a 64-year-old man with a history of alcohol abuse and atrial fibrillation who is currently on warfarin. He is experiencing new-onset confusion, forgetfulness, and has a scalp ecchymosis following a recent fall. These findings, particularly in the context of anticoagulation, raise concern for a **chronic subdural hematoma (CSDH)**.

---

### Key Clinical Clues:
- **History of fall** and **ecchymosis** on the scalp, suggesting a head injury.
- **Warfarin use** with an **elevated INR (2.5)**, increasing the risk of bleeding and delayed hematoma development.
- **Recent behavioral changes**: forgetfulness, not attending family events, and poor task management.
- **Normocytic anemia** could be due to chronic blood loss or possibly related to the hematoma.
- **No focal neurological deficits** or abnormal gait, suggesting a subacute rather than acute presentation.

---

### Differential Diagnosis Considerations:
- **Chronic Subdural Hematoma (CSDH):** Most likely diagnosis. The delayed onset of symptoms following a minor head injury in an anticoagulated patient is classic for CSDH. Symptoms often include confusion, memory loss, and behavioral changes.
- **Wernicke’s Encephalopathy:** Possible given the history of alcohol abuse, but the absence of ophthalmoplegia or ataxia makes it less likely.
- **Alzheimer’s Disease / Dementia:** Donepezil (option C) is used for this, but the acute onset and history of trauma make it a less likely primary diagnosis.
- **Ischemic Stroke:** Alteplase (option A) is used for acute stroke, but the presentation is not acute, and there is no evidence of focal deficits.
- **Vitamin B12 Deficiency:** Can cause cognitive symptoms, but again, the history of trauma and anticoagulation makes CSDH more likely.

---

### Treatment Considerations:
For a **chronic subdural hematoma**, especially in an anticoagulated patient, **surgical intervention** is often required if the hematoma is symptomatic or causing mass effect. The **burr hole craniostomy** is a common and effective procedure to evacuate the hematoma, especially in chronic cases.

---

### Conclusion:
Given the clinical presentation and risk factors, **chronic subdural hematoma** is the most likely diagnosis. The appropriate treatment is a **burr hole** to drain the hematoma.
