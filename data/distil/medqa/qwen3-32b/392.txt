A 25-year-old woman with **no significant medical history** except for **seasonal allergies** and a **history of a broken arm** is found to have **elevated blood pressure (152/95 mmHg)** during a routine physical exam. ### Understanding the Pathophysiology

The **elevated renin and aldosterone levels** suggest **activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)**. This is a key clue in narrowing down the diagnosis.

- **ACE inhibitors** work by blocking the conversion of **angiotensin I to angiotensin II**, a potent vasoconstrictor.
- In **unilateral renal artery stenosis**, especially due to **fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD)**, the **affected kidney** secretes excessive renin in response to reduced perfusion.
- When **ACE inhibitors are administered**, they **eliminate the vasoconstrictive effects of angiotensin II**, which is critical for maintaining perfusion in the stenotic kidney.
- This results in **further hypoperfusion of the affected kidney**, triggering **a compensatory surge in renin secretion**, leading to a **paradoxical worsening of hypertension**—a **hypertensive crisis** or **hypertensive urgency**.

### Evaluation of the Answer Choices

- **A: Bilateral fibromuscular dysplasia**  
  Bilateral involvement would lead to **bilateral activation of RAAS**, and ACE inhibitors may cause **acute kidney injury** due to loss of compensatory angiotensin II-mediated vasoconstriction. However, **hypertensive urgency** is **not a typical presentation** in this scenario.

- **B: Conn syndrome (Primary aldosteronism)**  
  This is **not consistent** with the findings. In Conn syndrome, **aldosterone is elevated**, but **renin is typically suppressed** due to negative feedback. The presence of **elevated renin** makes this unlikely.

- **C: Renal artery atherosclerosis**  
  This is more common in **older individuals** with a history of **atherosclerosis or diabetes**, which is **not consistent with this 25-year-old patient**.

- **D: Renin-secreting tumor**  
  While these tumors can cause **elevated renin**, the **response to ACE inhibitors** is typically **hypotension**, not a **hypertensive crisis**, unless there is a **tumor-induced feedback mechanism**, which is rare.
