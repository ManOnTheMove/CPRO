### Clinical Scenario Overview

The scenario presents a 25-year-old woman with signs of physical trauma, including bruising in the left infraorbital area and a right periorbital hematoma. Initially, she provides an implausible explanation (a fall), but when left alone with the physician, she discloses that her husband is responsible for her injuries. The husband appears controlling and has been reluctant to cooperate with the medical team.

This is a classic case of **suspected domestic violence**, where the patient’s safety is at immediate risk. In such cases, the physician has an ethical and legal duty to:

1. **Ensure the patient’s safety.**
2. **Provide confidential support.**
3. **Offer resources and referrals without pressuring the patient.**

---

### Evaluation of Options

Let’s examine each option in the context of these responsibilities:

- **Option A**: *“May I add some information about local domestic violence agencies to your discharge records?”*  
  While this is a supportive action, it is not the *immediate* priority. Providing information is important, but the first step should be to assess whether the patient is in immediate danger when leaving the hospital.

- **Option B**: *“Do you feel safe leaving the emergency department?”*  
  This is a direct and sensitive inquiry into the patient’s **immediate safety**. It allows her to express any fears or concerns and opens the door for the physician to take protective measures (e.g., contacting social services, arranging a safe discharge). It is both non-threatening and patient-centered.

- **Option C**: *“Don't you think you should report this incident to the police?”*  
  This is a **directive** and potentially coercive. While there may be legal obligations to report in some jurisdictions, the decision to involve law enforcement must be made by the patient, not the physician. Pressuring the patient could endanger her further.

- **Option D**: *“How often has your husband physically hurt you in the past?”*  
  While this provides useful context, it is a **historical** inquiry and does not address the **immediate safety** of the patient. It may be appropriate later in the clinical or social work follow-up, but not as the first step.

- **Option E**: *“May I ask your husband to join us so we can talk about this matter together?”*  
  This is **dangerous and inappropriate**. Inviting the abuser into the room could escalate the situation and put the patient at greater risk. The physician has already taken the appropriate step of asking the husband to leave.

---

### Conclusion

The most appropriate **immediate** response is to assess the patient’s **safety** when she is about to leave the emergency department. This allows the physician to determine if any further protective measures or referrals are needed.
