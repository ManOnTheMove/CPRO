### Clinical Context and Diagnosis

A 35-year-old woman presents with **polyuria and polydipsia** two days after **transsphenoidal surgery for a pituitary macroadenoma**. Her **serum sodium is elevated at 153 mEq/L**, and her **serum glucose is normal at 92 mg/dL**. These findings are highly suggestive of **central diabetes insipidus (DI)**.

---

### Pathophysiology of Diabetes Insipidus

The **pituitary gland** is responsible for **storing and releasing antidiuretic hormone (ADH)**. ADH acts on the **collecting ducts** of the nephron to increase **water permeability**, allowing for **water reabsorption** and **urine concentration**. In the absence of ADH (as may occur post-surgically due to damage or disruption of the posterior pituitary), the **collecting ducts remain impermeable to water**, leading to the excretion of **large volumes of dilute urine**.

This results in **dehydration**, **increased serum osmolality**, and **hypernatremia** — all consistent with the patient's presentation.

---

### Nephron and Osmolality Changes

To answer the question, we must focus on the **osmolality of the tubular fluid** in the **collecting duct** in the context of **ADH deficiency**.

- **In normal conditions**, when ADH is present, the **collecting duct** becomes **permeable to water**, and the **tubular fluid becomes hypertonic** relative to the serum as water is reabsorbed.
  
- **In the absence of ADH**, the **collecting duct remains impermeable to water**, and thus the **tubular fluid is hypotonic** relative to the **serum**, which is hyperosmolar due to dehydration and hypernatremia.

---

### Evaluation of the Options

- **A: Distal ascending loop of Henle is hypertonic**  
  → The ascending limb is impermeable to water and actively transports solutes, making the **tubular fluid hypotonic**, not hypertonic. Also, this is **not the site of ADH action**.

- **B: Distal descending loop of Henle is hypotonic**  
  → The descending limb is permeable to water, and the **tubular fluid becomes hypertonic** as it descends into the hypertonic medulla. This is **not consistent with the patient’s condition**.

- **C: Proximal convoluted tubule is hypertonic**  
  → The proximal tubule reabsorbs solutes and water in **parallel**, maintaining **isotonicity**. It is **not the site affected by ADH deficiency**.

- **D: Collecting duct is hypotonic**  
  → **Correct.** In the absence of ADH, the collecting duct is **impermeable to water**, leading to **dilute (hypotonic) urine** despite **hypertonic serum** due to hypernatremia.

- **E: Distal convoluted tubule is hypertonic**  
  → The DCT is **not the primary site** of ADH action. Its osmolality is influenced by other mechanisms and is **not the main concern** in this case.
