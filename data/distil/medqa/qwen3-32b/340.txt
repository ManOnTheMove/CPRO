As individuals age, several physiological changes occur, particularly in the regulation of sleep. In this case, a 69-year-old man reports sleeping less than he did in his youth and feeling less rested, despite maintaining a healthy lifestyle and adequate sleep duration (seven hours per night). His wife also observes that he appears to sleep peacefully, suggesting no overt signs of sleep fragmentation or apnea.

---

**Key Consideration: Age-Related Sleep Changes**

With aging, the architecture of sleep shifts. Older adults typically experience:

- **Decreased slow-wave sleep (SWS)**, which is the most restorative stage of sleep.
- **Increased light sleep** and more frequent awakenings.
- **Earlier sleep onset and earlier awakening**, often referred to as a phase advance.
- **Reduced total sleep time**, with many individuals sleeping less than they did in their younger years.

These changes are not necessarily indicative of sleep disorders but are a natural part of aging. However, they can lead to a perception of poor sleep quality or inadequate rest, even when objective measures (like sleep duration or absence of apnea) appear normal.

---

**Role of Melatonin in Sleep Regulation**

Melatonin, a hormone produced by the pineal gland, plays a central role in regulating the circadian rhythm and promoting sleep onset. Its levels typically rise in the evening, peak during the night, and decline in the morning.

**Importantly**, **melatonin production decreases with age**, which may contribute to the following changes:

- **Earlier sleep onset and awakening** (due to altered circadian rhythm).
- **Reduced sleep continuity and depth**, particularly in SWS.
- **Difficulty achieving restorative sleep**, even when sleep duration is adequate.

This aligns with the patient's complaint: he sleeps seven hours a night, but does not feel as rested as he did in his youth. A decline in melatonin may lead to a less consolidated and less restorative sleep pattern, even if sleep duration remains stable.
