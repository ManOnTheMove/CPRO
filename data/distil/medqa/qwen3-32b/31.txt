To understand the effect of acetazolamide on the kinetics of the reaction catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase, we need to recall some basic biochemical principles.

---

### Role of Carbonic Anhydrase

Carbonic anhydrase is a highly efficient enzyme that catalyzes the reversible reaction:

$$
\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \leftrightarrow \text{HCO}_3^- + \text{H}^+
$$

This reaction is central to maintaining acid-base balance and CO₂ transport in the body. The enzyme significantly lowers the **activation energy (Ea)** of the reaction, allowing it to proceed rapidly under physiological conditions.

---

### Effect of Acetazolamide

Acetazolamide is a **carbonic anhydrase inhibitor**. It binds to the active site of the enzyme, thereby **blocking its catalytic activity**. When the enzyme is inhibited:

- The **reaction proceeds more slowly**.
- The enzyme's role in **lowering the activation energy** is reduced or eliminated.
- As a result, the **activation energy of the reaction increases**, since the enzyme is no longer accelerating the reaction.

---

### Thermodynamic Considerations

It is important to distinguish between **kinetics** and **thermodynamics**:

- **Enzymes affect kinetics**, not thermodynamics.
- The **free energy change (ΔG)** of the reaction is a thermodynamic property and is **not affected** by the presence of an enzyme or its inhibitor.
- The **free energy (G) of the reactants or products** also remains unchanged. Only the **activation energy (Ea)** is altered.

---

### Correct Answer

Given that acetazolamide inhibits carbonic anhydrase, the **activation energy (Ea) of the reaction increases**, as the enzyme is no longer helping to lower it. This results in a **slower reaction rate**, consistent with the expected effect of an enzyme inhibitor.
