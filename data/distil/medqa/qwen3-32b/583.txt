When a surgical incision such as a midline laparotomy is made, the body initiates a complex wound-healing process. This process involves three main phases: **inflammation**, **proliferation**, and **remodeling**.

---

### Wound Healing and Collagen Types

- **Inflammation Phase**: Immediately after injury, the body works to clean the wound and prevent infection. This phase is short-lived and dominated by immune cells and the formation of a blood clot.

- **Proliferation Phase**: New tissue, called **granulation tissue**, forms. This tissue is rich in **Type III collagen**, which is more flexible and allows for initial tissue strength. Fibroblasts begin to lay down new extracellular matrix.

- **Remodeling Phase**: Over weeks to months, the scar matures. **Type I collagen** gradually replaces Type III collagen, increasing the tensile strength of the scar tissue. By **3 months post-surgery**, the scar is largely composed of **Type I collagen**, which is the primary structural component of the dermis and provides the bulk of the scar's strength and rigidity.

---

### Evaluating the Options

- **A: Keratin**  
  Keratin is a structural protein found in epithelial tissues, such as the skin's epidermis. While it's part of the outer skin layer, it is **not the primary component of scar tissue**.

- **B: Type I collagen**  
  This is the **main collagen type in scar tissue**, especially after the remodeling phase, which is well underway at 3 months. It provides structural strength and is the dominant collagen in the skin and connective tissues.

- **C: Type II collagen**  
  Found primarily in **cartilage**, not in skin or scar tissue.

- **D: Type III collagen**  
  This is involved in the **early proliferation phase** of wound healing but is **replaced by Type I** during the remodeling phase.

- **E: Type IV collagen**  
  Found in the **basement membrane** of epithelial tissues, not in the dermis or scar tissue.

---

### Conclusion

At three months post-surgery, the scar is predominantly made up of **Type I collagen**, which provides the structural integrity and rigidity of the mature scar.
