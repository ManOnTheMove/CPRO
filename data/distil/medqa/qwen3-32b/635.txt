### Clinical Scenario Summary

A 23-year-old man with signs and symptoms of acute appendicitis is undergoing a laparoscopic appendectomy. During the induction of anesthesia, he is administered **isoflurane** and a **second compound**. Approximately 25 minutes after induction, he develops **muscle rigidity** and a **rapid rise in core body temperature (40.8°C or 105.4°F)**, along with **elevated blood pressure (158/94 mmHg)**.

---

### Key Diagnostic Clues

The combination of:
- **Hyperthermia**
- **Muscle rigidity**
- **Elevated blood pressure**
- **Triggered by volatile anesthetic (isoflurane) and an additional agent**

is highly suggestive of **malignant hyperthermia (MH)**.

Malignant hyperthermia is a rare but life-threatening **genetic disorder** triggered by **volatile anesthetics** (e.g., isoflurane, sevoflurane) and **depolarizing muscle relaxants** (e.g., **succinylcholine**). It is characterized by a **hypermetabolic state** of skeletal muscle, leading to:
- Muscle rigidity (especially in the jaw and trunk)
- Hyperthermia (often >40°C)
- Tachycardia, tachypnea, and hypertension
- Metabolic acidosis and rhabdomyolysis (noted in lab values like elevated creatine kinase)

---

### Evaluation of the Options

Let’s examine the second compound used:

- **A. Atracurium**: A non-depolarizing muscle relaxant. **Not a trigger** for malignant hyperthermia.
- **B. Bupivacaine**: A local anesthetic. **Not a known trigger**.
- **C. Nitrous oxide**: A volatile anesthetic, but **not a strong trigger** for MH, and usually not used alone.
- **D. Neostigmine**: An anticholinesterase agent used to reverse neuromuscular blockade. **Not a trigger**.
- **E. Succinylcholine**: A **depolarizing muscle relaxant** and a **classic trigger** for malignant hyperthermia when combined with volatile anesthetics.

Given the **classic clinical presentation** and the known **trigger pair** (volatile anesthetic + succinylcholine), **succinylcholine** is the most likely second compound administered.

---

### Conclusion

The patient’s symptoms are consistent with **malignant hyperthermia**, a condition classically triggered by the combination of **volatile anesthetics** and **succinylcholine**. This makes **succinylcholine** the most likely second compound administered.
