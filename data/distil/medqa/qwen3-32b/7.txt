### Clinical Overview and Key Features

This case centers around a 67-year-old man with a complex medical history (Alzheimer’s dementia, diabetes, hypertension) who presents with **fever (102.9°F), confusion, and a flushed, warm complexion**. He is **bed-bound** and has no history of recent physical activity. Notably, he was administered **haloperidol and diphenhydramine** due to combative behavior before the physical exam.

The **initial laboratory findings** show **normal white blood cell count**, **normal differential**, and **no signs of infection in the urine**. A **normal chest radiograph** further supports the absence of an obvious source of sepsis. After **fluid resuscitation and cooling**, the patient's **temperature normalizes, mental status improves**, and **vital signs stabilize**, all of which are **consistent with a reversible hyperthermic condition**.

### Differential Diagnosis Considerations

#### 1. **Non-Exertional Heat Stroke (Correct Answer)**  
- **Definition**: A form of heat stroke that occurs in **vulnerable populations** (e.g., elderly, debilitated, or chronically ill individuals) **without physical exertion**, often during **hot weather** or **impaired thermoregulation**.
- **Clinical Features**:
  - High fever (≥40°C or 104°F), though this patient had 39.4°C (102.9°F)
  - Altered mental status
  - Dry, flushed skin
  - Tachycardia and tachypnea
- **Response to Treatment**:
  - **Aggressive cooling and fluid resuscitation** lead to rapid improvement in symptoms and vital signs, as seen in this case.
- **Epidemiology**:
  - The patient is **elderly, bed-bound**, and has **comorbidities** that impair thermoregulation (e.g., dementia, diabetes).
  - No evidence of physical exertion, making **exertional heat stroke unlikely**.

#### 2. **Exertional Heat Stroke**  
- **Definition**: Occurs in **athletes or physically active individuals** due to **prolonged exertion in hot environments**.
- **Why not likely here**:
  - The patient is **not physically active** and is **bed-bound**.
  - No history of recent exertion is provided.

#### 3. **Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)**  
- **Definition**: A life-threatening reaction to **neuroleptic medications**, characterized by **hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, altered mental status**, and **autonomic instability**.
- **Why not likely here**:
  - **No muscle rigidity or lead-pipe rigidity** is reported in the physical exam.
  - **Significant improvement** after **cooling and fluids** is more consistent with **heat stroke** than NMS.
  - Although the patient was given **haloperidol**, the timing and rapid resolution of symptoms do not align with NMS.

#### 4. **Sepsis / Septic Shock**  
- **Definition**: A systemic inflammatory response to infection, with or without hypotension.
- **Why not likely here**:
  - **No source of infection** is identified (normal CXR, negative urinalysis).
  - **White blood cell count is normal**, not elevated or with left shift.
  - **Rapid response to fluids** is more typical of **heat stroke** than **septic shock**, which typically requires vasopressors and prolonged resuscitation.
  - **Septic shock** is usually associated with **persistent hypotension**, which is not the case here (BP improved after fluid administration).

---

### Final Diagnosis and Justification

Given the **clinical picture**, **absence of an infectious source**, **response to cooling and fluids**, and **lack of exertion or signs of NMS**, the **most likely diagnosis is non-exertional heat stroke**.
