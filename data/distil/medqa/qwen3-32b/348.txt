### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 30-year-old man presents to the emergency department after sustaining a **stab wound to the right side of the neck**, followed by **difficulty breathing**, **chest pain**, and **signs of respiratory distress**. His **vital signs** are concerning: tachycardia (110 bpm), tachypnea (28 breaths/min), hypoxia (SpO₂ 85% on 4 L O₂), and hypotension (100/60 mmHg). On physical examination, **distended neck veins**, **tracheal deviation to the left**, **decreased right-sided lung inspiration**, and **right-sided chest tenderness** are noted.

---

### Key Findings and Differential Diagnosis

The **tracheal deviation to the left** and **decreased breath sounds on the right** are **highly suggestive of a right-sided tension pneumothorax**. This is a life-threatening condition where **air accumulates in the pleural space**, increasing intrathoracic pressure and compressing the mediastinum, which can lead to **cardiac compromise and respiratory failure**.

- **Distended neck veins** may also be seen in tension pneumothorax due to **increased intrathoracic pressure**, which impedes venous return to the heart.
- **Hypotension and hypoxia** further support a critical process affecting cardiopulmonary function.

Other differentials like **cardiac tamponade** or **hemorrhagic shock** are less likely given the **pattern of tracheal deviation** and the **penetrating injury to the neck**, which is more consistent with **pneumothorax** than pericardial injury.

---

### Immediate Management

In the setting of **suspected tension pneumothorax**, the **next best step** is **needle thoracostomy** to **relieve the pressure in the pleural space**. This is a **life-saving intervention** and should be performed **immediately** on the affected side (right side in this case).

- **Option A (Pericardiocentesis)** is not the priority in this clinical context and is reserved for **cardiac tamponade**, which typically presents with **equalization of heart sounds**, **pulsus paradoxus**, and **not tracheal deviation**.
- **Option B (IV fluids)** may be appropriate later if hemodynamic instability persists, but they are **not the first step** in tension pneumothorax.
- **Option C (Dobutamine)** is a **inotropic agent** used in **cardiogenic shock**, not appropriate in this scenario.
- **Option E (Pain relief and observation)** is **inappropriate** for a patient in **respiratory and hemodynamic distress**.

---

### Conclusion

Given the **clinical presentation and physical findings**, the most likely diagnosis is **tension pneumothorax**, and the **next best step** in management is **needle thoracostomy**.
