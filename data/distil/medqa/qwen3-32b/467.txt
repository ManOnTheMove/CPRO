### Case Summary

A 58-year-old woman, recently discharged after resection of a **parasagittal meningioma**, presents with **confusion, fatigue, muscle cramps, nausea, and vomiting**. Laboratory findings reveal **hyponatremia (Na⁺ 126 mEq/L)**, **low serum osmolality (255 mOsmol/kg)**, and **high urine osmolality (523 mOsmol/kg)** with **elevated urine sodium (44 mEq/L)**. She is on **hydrochlorothiazide**, but her symptoms began after surgery.

---

### Clinical Evaluation

The key features of this case are:

- **Hyponatremia** (serum Na⁺ < 135 mEq/L)
- **Hypoosmolality** (serum osmolality < 275 mOsmol/kg)
- **Hypo-osmolar urine** (urine osmolality > 100 mOsmol/kg)
- **High urine sodium (> 40 mEq/L)**

These findings are **highly suggestive of Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH)**.

---

### Differential Diagnosis Consideration

#### **A. Cerebral Salt Wasting (CSW)**
- **Key difference**: CSW is associated with **volume depletion** and **low blood pressure**, whereas this patient has **normal or elevated BP (142/88 mmHg)**.
- **Urine sodium is also high**, but CSW is **not associated with hypoosmolality** in the same way as SIADH.
- **Physical signs of hypovolemia** (e.g., tachycardia, dry mucous membranes) are **absent**.
- **Less likely** in this case.

#### **B. Hydrochlorothiazide**
- Thiazide diuretics can cause **hyponatremia**, but usually with **low urine sodium** due to **sodium conservation**.
- In this case, the **urine sodium is high (44 mEq/L)**, which is **inconsistent** with thiazide-induced hyponatremia.
- Also, the **timing** of symptom onset (post-surgery) suggests a **new-onset condition**, not a chronic effect of medication.
- **Unlikely** as the primary cause.

#### **C. Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH)**
- SIADH is characterized by:
  - **Euvolemic hyponatremia**
  - **Low serum osmolality**
  - **High urine osmolality**
  - **High urine sodium**
- **Etiology** can include **CNS pathology**, such as **post-operative meningioma surgery**.
- The patient's **recent neurosurgery** and **absence of volume depletion** support this diagnosis.
- **Most consistent** with the clinical and laboratory findings.

#### **D. Primary Polydipsia**
- Would lead to **low urine osmolality** due to **excessive water intake**.
- This patient has **high urine osmolality**, so this is **not consistent**.
- **Unlikely**.

#### **E. Mineralocorticoid Deficiency (e.g., Addison’s Disease)**
- Would present with **hyponatremia and hyperkalemia**.
- The patient's **K⁺ is normal**, and she has **no other signs of adrenal insufficiency** (e.g., hyperpigmentation, hypotension).
- **Unlikely**.

Given the **post-neurological surgery setting**, **euvolemia**, **high urine osmolality and sodium**, and **low serum osmolality**, the **most likely diagnosis is SIADH**.
