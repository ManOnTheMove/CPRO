### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 21-year-old woman presents with **sudden-onset left-sided back pain radiating to the groin**, a **history of similar episodes**, **tachycardia (103 bpm)**, and **left costovertebral angle tenderness**. The physical exam shows a **soft, non-tender abdomen**, and **urinalysis** reveals **blood** and a **positive cyanide nitroprusside test**, which is used to detect **ketones** in the urine. An **abdominal x-ray is normal**.

---

### Differential Diagnosis

Given the clinical presentation and findings, the **most likely diagnosis is renal colic due to a ureteral stone**. The **recurrent episodes**, **costovertebral angle tenderness**, and **hematuria** are classic signs of **urinary tract stone disease**.

The **negative abdominal x-ray** suggests a **radiolucent stone**, such as a **uric acid stone**. These are known to **not be visible on plain X-rays** and are **associated with acidic urine**. Uric acid stones are more common in patients with **low urinary pH**, **hyperuricosuria**, or **metabolic acidosis**.

---

### Interpretation of Laboratory Findings

- **Blood in the urine (hematuria)**: Often due to **trauma from a stone** moving through the urinary tract.
- **Ketones in the urine (positive cyanide nitroprusside test)**: This could be secondary to **dehydration or pain-induced fasting**, which are common in acute renal colic.

---

### Evaluation of the Answer Choices

Let’s examine each of the options in the context of this clinical picture:

- **A: pH of 7.8**  
  This indicates **alkaline urine**, which is more consistent with **struvite stones** (due to urease-producing bacteria like *Proteus*). However, **alkaline urine is not typical of uric acid stones**, and the **x-ray is normal**, which is **inconsistent with radiopaque struvite stones**.

- **B: Urease-producing bacteria**  
  These would cause **alkaline urine** and are associated with **struvite stones**, again **not consistent with a normal x-ray** and **no strong evidence of infection** in this case (e.g., pyuria, fever, or leukocytosis beyond 11,000/mm³).

- **C: Low citrate concentration**  
  Hypocitraturia is a known risk factor for **calcium oxalate stones**, but it is **not directly linked to uric acid stones**, which are more influenced by **uric acid levels and urine pH**.

- **D: High uric acid concentration** ✅  
  This is **highly likely** in a patient with **recurrent episodes of flank pain**, **hematuria**, and a **normal x-ray**, all of which are **hallmarks of uric acid stones**. These stones form in **acidic urine**, and elevated uric acid levels in the urine support this diagnosis.

- **E: Hexagon-shaped crystals**  
  These are characteristic of **cystine stones**, which are rare and typically seen in **genetic disorders like cystinuria**. There is **no clinical or laboratory evidence** supporting this in the given case.
