### Differential Diagnosis for Syncope

Syncope can be caused by several conditions, including:

- **Cardiac causes**: arrhythmias, structural heart disease (e.g., aortic stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy), ischemic heart disease.
- **Neurologic causes**: seizures, transient ischemic attack (TIA).
- **Vascular causes**: orthostatic hypotension, carotid sinus hypersensitivity.
- **Other causes**: vasovagal syncope, hypoglycemia.

Given the **exertional nature** of the symptoms and the **delayed pulse**, the most likely diagnosis is **aortic stenosis**, a condition where the aortic valve becomes narrowed, leading to obstruction of blood flow from the left ventricle to the aorta. This can cause syncope during exertion due to reduced cardiac output when demand increases.

---

### Evaluation Strategy

- **Cardiac enzymes (C)** would be used to rule out acute myocardial infarction, but this patient has no chest pain and no signs of acute MI.
- **CT angiography (A)** is more useful in evaluating acute aortic dissection or coronary artery disease, which is not suggested here.
- **Carotid duplex (D)** is used to assess carotid artery stenosis, a cause of TIA or stroke, but is less likely in this context of exertional syncope.
- **EEG (E)** is used to evaluate for seizures, but the patient did not have post-ictal confusion, which argues against a seizure.
- **Echocardiogram (B)** is the best option. It is the **gold standard** for evaluating **aortic stenosis**, as it can visualize the **valve anatomy**, assess **valve mobility**, and measure the **pressure gradient** across the aortic valve. It can also detect other structural heart diseases such as hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, which is another common cause of syncope during exertion.

---

### Conclusion

Given the clinical presentation and the need to confirm a structural cardiac cause—specifically **aortic stenosis**—**echocardiography** is the most appropriate diagnostic test in this case.
