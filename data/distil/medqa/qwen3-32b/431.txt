### Clinical Summary

The patient is a 27-year-old woman presenting with **severe hypertension** (185/115 mmHg), **headaches**, **nosebleeds**, and **abdominal bruits**. She is otherwise healthy, denies drug use, and has no relevant family history. She was treated with a **calcium-channel blocker** and a **thiazide diuretic**, but her blood pressure remained **unresponsive**. An **aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR)** was measured and found to be **<20**, suggesting **no evidence of primary aldosteronism**.

---

### Key Features and Their Implications

1. **Young age with new-onset severe hypertension**  
   - Suggests **secondary hypertension** rather than essential (idiopathic) hypertension, which is less likely in a young, otherwise healthy individual.

2. **Abdominal bruits**  
   - This is a **classic sign of renal artery stenosis**, which is a known cause of secondary hypertension. The turbulent blood flow through a narrowed renal artery can produce an audible bruit during physical examination.

3. **Non-response to standard antihypertensive therapy**  
   - The patient did not respond to a calcium-channel blocker and thiazide diuretic, which are typically first-line agents. This may point to an **underlying anatomical cause** (e.g., renal artery stenosis) rather than a primary volume or vascular issue.

4. **Low aldosterone-to-renin ratio (<20)**  
   - This **rules out primary aldosteronism**, which is typically characterized by **elevated aldosterone** and **suppressed renin**, leading to a **high ARR**. In this case, the **low ARR** suggests **activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)**, consistent with **renal artery stenosis**, where **renin is elevated** due to decreased renal perfusion.
