In traumatic aortic rupture, the injury typically occurs due to **shear forces** generated during **sudden deceleration**, such as in a high-speed motor vehicle collision. These forces cause the heart to move forward while the **descending aorta** remains relatively fixed at the level of the **ligamentum arteriosum** (a remnant of the fetal ductus arteriosus). This creates a point of vulnerability just distal to the ligamentum arteriosum.

---

### Anatomical Context

The **aortic isthmus** is the narrow segment of the aorta located just distal to the origin of the **left subclavian artery** and proximal to the **ductus arteriosus** (ligamentum arteriosum in adults). This area is particularly susceptible to **traumatic injury** because:

- It is the **transition zone** between the **ascending** and **descending aorta**.
- It is **anchored** at the ligamentum arteriosum, making it less mobile.
- The **proximal part** (ascending aorta) is more mobile and can shift forward during deceleration, while the **distal part** is relatively fixed — leading to **tensile stress** at the isthmus.

---

### Clinical Relevance

This injury is a **life-threatening condition** that must be diagnosed and treated rapidly. It is most commonly seen in **blunt trauma**, especially **frontal (head-on) motor vehicle collisions**, and is associated with **high mortality** if not promptly managed.
