### Key Differentiation: Breastfeeding Jaundice vs. Breast Milk Jaundice

#### **1. Breastfeeding Jaundice**
- **Timing**: Occurs in the **first 7 days of life**, typically **days 2–4**.
- **Etiology**: Caused by **inadequate intake of breast milk**, leading to **dehydration**, **reduced caloric intake**, and **increased bilirubin production**.
- **Clinical features**:
  - **Infrequent stools** (often <1 per day).
  - **Weight loss or poor weight gain** (not explicitly mentioned here but implied by low stool frequency).
  - **Jaundice** peaking before day 7.
- **Management**: Encourage **frequent feeding** and **adequate hydration**.

#### **2. Breast Milk Jaundice**
- **Timing**: Begins **after day 7** (usually **day 5–7**) and may **persist beyond 2 weeks**.
- **Etiology**: Thought to be due to substances in breast milk that **inhibit bilirubin conjugation** or increase **enterohepatic circulation**.
- **Clinical features**:
  - **No evidence of poor feeding or dehydration**.
  - **Normal weight gain**.
  - **Jaundice resolves spontaneously** without intervention.
- **Management**: **Monitoring** is usually sufficient unless bilirubin is extremely high.
