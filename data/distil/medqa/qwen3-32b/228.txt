The clinical presentation of a 33-year-old woman with episodic finger pain, pallor upon cold exposure, and subsequent pain and numbness upon rewarming is classic for **Raynaud's phenomenon**. The condition is characterized by vasospastic episodes in the fingers (and sometimes toes), typically triggered by cold or emotional stress. The absence of nailfold capillary abnormalities and a negative Allen's test supports a diagnosis of **primary Raynaud's**, as opposed to the secondary form, which is often associated with connective tissue diseases (e.g., scleroderma, lupus) and may show capillary abnormalities.

---

### Key Clinical Features:
- **Intermittent, cold-induced digit pallor and pain**
- **Bilateral involvement**
- **Worsening in winter**
- **Normal physical exam and lab findings**, including hemoglobin, leukocyte count, platelet count, and electrolytes
- **Normal nailfold capillary microscopy**, ruling out secondary causes like scleroderma
- **Negative Allen's test**, indicating intact collateral circulation

---

### Pharmacotherapy Considerations:

- **Oral Aspirin (A)**: May be used in patients with secondary Raynaud's to reduce platelet aggregation, but it is not a first-line treatment for primary Raynaud's.
  
- **Topical Nitroglycerin (B)**: Can be effective for localized vasospasm, but it is less convenient and less potent than oral therapy for generalized symptoms.

- **Oral Nifedipine (C)**: A **calcium channel blocker** is the **first-line treatment** for Raynaud's phenomenon. It effectively reduces vasospasm by relaxing vascular smooth muscle and improving blood flow to the digits. Long-acting formulations are typically preferred to avoid reflex tachycardia.

- **Oral Epoprostenol (D)**: A prostacyclin analog, used in more severe or refractory cases, particularly in those with underlying connective tissue disease or severe digital ischemia. It is not typically used as first-line therapy.

- **Oral Prednisone (E)**: A corticosteroid, not indicated in primary Raynaud’s unless there is a concurrent autoimmune disorder, which is not suggested here.

---

### Conclusion:

Given the patient's symptoms and the absence of secondary causes, **primary Raynaud's phenomenon** is the most likely diagnosis. The most appropriate pharmacotherapy is **oral nifedipine**, a calcium channel blocker that directly targets the vasospasm associated with this condition.
