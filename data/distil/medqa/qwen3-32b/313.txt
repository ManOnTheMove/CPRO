The clinical scenario presents a 72-year-old man with a known history of diabetes, dyslipidemia, and hypertension, who experienced two transient episodes of neurological symptoms (right-sided facial paresthesia, word-finding difficulty, lightheadedness) lasting less than 5 minutes and 3 minutes, respectively. These episodes are consistent with transient ischemic attacks (TIAs), especially in the context of sudden onset and spontaneous resolution.

---

### Key Clinical Findings:

- **Neurological symptoms**: Facial paresthesia, aphasia, and lightheadedness suggest a transient focal neurological deficit, typical of a TIA.
- **Vascular risk factors**: Diabetes, dyslipidemia, and hypertension all increase the risk of cerebrovascular events.
- **Imaging and lab results**:
  - **Normal head CT and MRI**: No acute infarction or hemorrhage.
  - **ECG normal**: No evidence of atrial fibrillation (AF).
  - **Carotid ultrasound**: 70–89% stenosis in the left internal carotid artery and 50% in the right.
- **Recurrence of symptoms during evaluation** reinforces the diagnosis of TIA.

---

### Management Considerations:

In patients with **symptomatic carotid artery stenosis**, the American Heart Association (AHA) and American Stroke Association (ASA) recommend **carotid endarterectomy (CEA)** within 14 days for those with **moderate to severe stenosis (50–99%)**, especially when the symptoms are recent (within 14 days), and the patient is a surgical candidate with acceptable risk.

- **CEA is the most effective intervention** in reducing the risk of stroke in this patient population, particularly in the **symptomatic setting** with stenosis ≥ 50%.
- **Carotid artery stenting (CAS)** is an alternative for patients at high surgical risk or when anatomic factors make CEA unsuitable, but **CEA remains the gold standard** for suitable patients.
- **Anticoagulants** such as unfractionated heparin or enoxaparin are not first-line treatments for TIA in the absence of a cardioembolic source (e.g., AF).
- **Thrombolysis** is not indicated in TIA and is reserved for acute ischemic stroke with MRI-documented infarction, which is not the case here.

---

### Conclusion:

Given the patient's **recent TIA**, **significant left carotid stenosis**, and **no contraindications to surgery**, the most appropriate next step in management is to proceed with **carotid endarterectomy**.
