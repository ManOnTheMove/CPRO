### Clinical Context

This case involves a 25-year-old male with **second- and third-degree burns covering 30% of his total body surface area (TBSA)**. He is brought to the emergency department and later develops **respiratory distress** with **inspiratory stridor**, necessitating **rapid sequence intubation (RSI)**. The question is centered on the **potential complication of using succinylcholine**, a **depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agent**, in this clinical setting.

---

### Pharmacology of Succinylcholine

**Succinylcholine** is used in RSI due to its **rapid onset and short duration of action**. However, it has notable **side effects and contraindications**, including:

- **Potassium release** from damaged muscle tissue.
- **Risk of malignant hyperthermia** (rare but life-threatening).
- **Histamine release**, which may cause transient hypotension or bronchospasm.
- **Cardiac arrhythmias**, especially in the setting of **elevated serum potassium**.

---

### Pathophysiology in Burn Patients

Burn injuries—especially **second- and third-degree burns over 30% TBSA**—can lead to **significant cellular damage and leakage of intracellular contents**, including **potassium**, into the bloodstream. This can result in **hyperkalemia** even in the early post-burn phase.

When **succinylcholine** is administered in such patients, it causes **massive depolarization of skeletal muscle cells**, which further **increases potassium release**. This can rapidly elevate serum potassium levels, potentially leading to **life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias** such as **ventricular fibrillation or asystole**.
