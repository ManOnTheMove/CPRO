### Clinical Scenario Overview

A 15-year-old girl presents with **primary amenorrhea** (no menarche by age 15), **absence of breast development**, and **Tanner stage 1 genitalia**, indicating **delayed puberty**. She has a **high serum FSH level (38 mIU/mL)**, which is a key clue. This level is **elevated**, suggesting **primary ovarian insufficiency**, where the pituitary is overstimulating the ovaries in response to poor ovarian function.

---

### Interpreting the FSH Level

- **High FSH** in a girl with no signs of puberty and no menarche suggests **primary ovarian failure** (not due to hypothalamic/pituitary dysfunction).
- In contrast, **low FSH and LH** would suggest **hypogonadotropic hypogonadism**, such as in Kallmann syndrome or due to extreme weight loss or exercise.
- Given the high FSH, the pituitary is **functioning normally**, and the issue is likely **intrinsic to the ovaries**.

---

### Differential Diagnosis

Key causes of **primary ovarian insufficiency in adolescents** include:

- **Turner syndrome (45,XO)**: A common cause of gonadal dysgenesis and primary amenorrhea.
- **Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)**: Can be idiopathic or autoimmune.
- **Congenital ovarian dysgenesis or defects in ovarian development**.

Given the **absence of secondary sexual characteristics** and **elevated FSH**, the next diagnostic step should be to **confirm or rule out a chromosomal abnormality**, such as Turner syndrome.

---

### Evaluating the Options

- **A: CYP17 gene work-up**  
  This is relevant in **congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)**, but this patient has **normal external genitalia**, so this is **not the best next step**.

- **B: Estrogen levels**  
  Estrogen levels are likely **low** due to ovarian failure, but they are **not diagnostic** and do **not guide management** in this context.

- **C: Gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GnRH) stimulation test**  
  This is used to differentiate between **hypogonadotropic** and **hypergonadotropic** causes of delayed puberty. However, since **FSH is already elevated**, this test is **not necessary**—the pituitary is already active.

- **D: Karotype**  
  This is the **most appropriate next step**. A **karyotype analysis** will determine if the patient has **Turner syndrome (45,XO)** or another chromosomal abnormality. Turner syndrome is a **common cause** of primary ovarian insufficiency and delayed puberty in phenotypically female individuals.

- **E: Luteinizing hormone (LH) levels**  
  While useful for further characterizing the hormonal axis, **FSH is already elevated**, and **LH is not more informative** in this setting.

---

### Conclusion

The **next best diagnostic step** is to **perform a karyotype** to **rule out Turner syndrome**, which is a **common and treatable cause** of primary ovarian insufficiency in phenotypically normal females with delayed puberty and elevated FSH.
