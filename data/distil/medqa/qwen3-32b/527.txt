The clinical scenario describes a 45-year-old male presenting with tinnitus, vertigo, and hearing loss—symptoms classically associated with a vestibular schwannoma. These benign tumors typically arise from the Schwann cells of the vestibular portion of the vestibulocochlear nerve (cranial nerve VIII). While many vestibular schwannomas occur sporadically, a subset of cases are associated with a genetic disorder.

---

The most relevant hereditary condition in this context is **Neurofibromatosis Type 2 (NF2)**. NF2 is characterized by the development of **bilateral vestibular schwannomas**, as well as other central nervous system tumors such as meningiomas and ependymomas. It is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in the **NF2 gene**, also known as the **merlin** or **schwannomin** gene.

This gene functions as a **tumor suppressor**, and its inactivation (due to a mutation) leads to uncontrolled Schwann cell proliferation, resulting in tumor formation. 

---

The **NF2 gene is located on chromosome 22**. Specifically, it is found at the **22q12.2** locus. This genetic defect is responsible for the hallmark bilateral tumors seen in NF2, distinguishing it from sporadic vestibular schwannomas.

---

Given the clinical features and the genetic underpinning of the disorder, the most likely mutated chromosome in this patient’s condition is **chromosome 22**.
