Vital signs are within normal limits, and the physical exam is unremarkable, with no evidence of blood in the stool (guaiac-negative). There is no history of chronic illness or medication use.

---

### Differential Diagnosis

Given the clinical context, the most likely diagnosis is **lactose intolerance**, especially in a patient of Japanese descent, where lactose intolerance is more common due to a reduced prevalence of lactase persistence in this population. The symptoms—diarrhea, bloating, and flatulence after consuming a dairy product—strongly suggest an osmotic diarrhea caused by undigested lactose in the colon.

Other differentials include:
- **Lactose malabsorption** or **lactase deficiency**
- **Carbohydrate malabsorption**
- **Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO)**
- **Celiac disease** (less likely due to lack of blood in stool or malabsorption signs)
- **Infectious colitis** (unlikely due to normal vitals and no fever)

---

### Diagnostic Considerations

The key to diagnosis is determining the **mechanism of diarrhea** (osmotic vs. secretory). This can be done using the **stool osmotic gap**, which is a simple and accessible test in most clinical settings, including emergency departments. A high osmotic gap (>120 mOsm/kg) suggests **osmotic diarrhea**, which is consistent with the presence of unabsorbed solutes (like lactose) in the intestinal lumen.

While the **hydrogen breath test** is the gold standard for diagnosing lactose intolerance, it is more complex and not typically performed in the ED due to the need for fasting and multiple breath samples over time. Therefore, in the acute setting, the **stool osmotic gap** is a more practical first step to determine the mechanism of diarrhea and guide further evaluation.

---

### Next Step in Management

Since the patient presents with symptoms consistent with lactose intolerance and the clinical context supports this, the **most appropriate next step** is to **measure the stool osmotic gap**. This test will help differentiate between osmotic and secretory diarrhea. If the osmotic gap is elevated, it supports the diagnosis of lactose malabsorption, and further steps such as a lactose-free trial or hydrogen breath test can be considered.

---

### Conclusion

The **stool osmotic gap** is a simple, effective, and accessible first diagnostic step in the ED for this patient. It provides immediate insight into the pathophysiology of the diarrhea and helps direct appropriate follow-up.
