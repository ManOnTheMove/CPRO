### Clinical Overview

This 66-year-old male presents with a **persistent dry cough** following the initiation of **lisinopril**, an **ACE inhibitor**, for hypertension. The cough is a **classic side effect of ACE inhibitors**, occurring in approximately **10% of patients**, and is typically **dry and non-productive**, without associated fever or other symptoms. 

The **laboratory findings** also support a medication-related etiology:
- **Hyperkalemia** (K+ = 5.3 mEq/L) is a known effect of ACE inhibitors.
- **Elevated BUN** (from 18 to 22 mg/dL) and **creatinine** (from 1.0 to 1.2 mg/dL) may suggest a **mild decline in renal function**, which is also a potential consequence of ACE inhibitors, especially in patients with underlying renal impairment or volume depletion.

The patient denies any other symptoms, and there is no evidence of infection (e.g., fever, sputum production, dyspnea), which makes **infectious causes or allergic etiologies less likely**. Therefore, the cough is most likely **iatrogenic**, due to the **ACE inhibitor therapy**.

---

### Differential Diagnosis and Next Steps

#### **A: Switch blood pressure medication to losartan (an ARB)**
- **Losartan** is an **angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB)**, which works similarly to ACE inhibitors but **does not cause a dry cough**.
- It is a **direct alternative** in patients who develop cough from ACE inhibitors.
- It also has a **similar effect on potassium and renal function**, but since the patient is asymptomatic and not in acute renal failure, the **benefits of switching outweigh the risks**.
- This is the **most logical and evidence-based next step** for this patient.

#### **B: Switch blood pressure medication to hydrochlorothiazide**
- While **hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ)** is a **thiazide diuretic** that can lower blood pressure and **may help manage hyperkalemia**, it **does not address the cough**.
- Cough is the **primary symptom** and the **most immediate concern**, so a **switch to a medication that does not cause cough is more appropriate**.

#### **C: Chest radiograph**
- A **chest X-ray** is **not indicated** at this time. The patient has **no signs of pneumonia or other respiratory conditions** (e.g., fever, sputum, chest pain).
- The **dry, isolated cough** is most consistent with **medication side effects**, not an infectious or structural cause.

#### **D: Azithromycin**
- An **antibiotic** is **not warranted** in the absence of **clinical signs of infection**, such as fever, sputum, or leukocytosis.

#### **E: Loratadine**
- **Loratadine** is an **antihistamine**, used for **allergic rhinitis**, and is **not effective** for **non-allergic, medication-induced coughs** like those caused by ACE inhibitors.

---

### Conclusion

The **most appropriate next step** in this patient’s management is to **discontinue lisinopril** and **switch to an ARB** such as **losartan**, which avoids the **cough side effect** and is **clinically equivalent** in treating hypertension and diabetes.
