### Clinical Scenario Analysis

The patient presents with a **painless lesion** on the **upper arm**, which evolved from a **papule** to a **blister**, and then **ulcerated**. The presence of a **black eschar**, **edematous borders**, **fever**, and **axillary lymphadenopathy** is highly suggestive of **cutaneous anthrax**, caused by *Bacillus anthracis*. The **black eschar** is a hallmark of this infection, and the **edematous borders** are due to one of the key virulence factors of the bacterium.

---

### Virulence Factors of *Bacillus anthracis*

*Bacillus anthracis* produces **three major toxins**:
1. **Lethal toxin** (protective antigen + lethal factor)
2. **Edema toxin** (protective antigen + **edema factor**)

The **edema factor** is a **calmodulin-dependent adenylate cyclase**. Once inside the host cell, it catalyzes the conversion of **ATP to cAMP**, leading to **disruption of normal cellular signaling**, **inhibition of phagocytosis**, and **increased vascular permeability**, all contributing to **tissue edema**.

---

### Mechanism Comparison

The question asks which **enzyme** has a **mechanism of action** similar to the virulence factor responsible for the edema. Since the **edema factor** is **adenylate cyclase**, its **mechanism of action** is **identical** to the enzyme **adenylate cyclase**.

This is different from other bacterial virulence factors:
- **IgA protease** degrades immunoglobulin A, aiding in evasion of mucosal immunity.
- **Catalase** breaks down hydrogen peroxide, helping bacteria survive oxidative stress.
- **Guanylate cyclase** (as in *Vibrio cholerae* toxin) also increases intracellular cAMP but via a different mechanism (indirect activation).
- **SNARE proteins** are involved in vesicle trafficking and are not enzymatic virulence factors.
