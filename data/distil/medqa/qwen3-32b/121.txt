Barbiturates, such as amobarbital, are central nervous system (CNS) depressants that work by enhancing the inhibitory effects of GABA. Prolonged use can lead to physical dependence, and abrupt discontinuation or reduction can trigger a withdrawal syndrome that mimics the opposite of the drug's effects—namely, overstimulation of the CNS.

---

### Key Features of Barbiturate Withdrawal

Barbiturate withdrawal is a potentially life-threatening condition, especially after long-term, high-dose use. The syndrome typically presents with:

- **Neurological symptoms**: Tremors, insomnia, irritability, and in severe cases, **seizures**.
- **Autonomic hyperactivity**: Tachycardia, hypertension, sweating.
- **Psychiatric symptoms**: Anxiety, agitation, hallucinations, and in some cases, **suicidal ideation**.
- **Delirium**: Confusion, disorientation, and impaired cognition.

The most **immediate and life-threatening** complication of barbiturate withdrawal is **seizures**, which can progress to status epilepticus. However, since this is not one of the listed options, we must evaluate the next most severe complications.
