To address the question, we focus on the newly introduced medication that inhibits **intestinal cholesterol absorption**. The most well-known drug in this class is **ezetimibe**, which acts by inhibiting the **NPC1L1** transporter, thereby reducing the absorption of dietary and biliary cholesterol in the small intestine.

---

### Clinical Context

The patient has multiple cardiovascular risk factors, including **type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and coronary artery disease**. He is already on **rosuvastatin**, a potent **HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor (statin)**, as well as other medications for blood pressure control and secondary prevention of coronary artery disease.

The addition of **ezetimibe** is a common therapeutic strategy to further reduce **LDL cholesterol** levels, particularly in patients who are not meeting their lipid goals on a statin alone or who may be statin-intolerant.

---

### Adverse Effect Analysis

Let’s evaluate the options in the context of **ezetimibe**:

- **A: Hepatotoxicity**  
  Ezetimibe is not known to cause significant hepatotoxicity. While statins may cause liver enzyme elevations, this is not a primary concern with ezetimibe alone.

- **B: Hyperkalemia**  
  Hyperkalemia is more commonly associated with **ACE inhibitors**, **ARBs**, and **spironolactone**. Thiazide diuretics (like hydrochlorothiazide) actually cause **hypokalemia**, not hyperkalemia. Ezetimibe has no direct effect on potassium levels.

- **C: Cutaneous flushing**  
  This is a classic side effect of **niacin** (not ezetimibe). Ezetimibe does not cause flushing.

- **D: Hyperuricemia**  
  Thiazide diuretics like hydrochlorothiazide are known to cause **hyperuricemia**, but there is no strong evidence that ezetimibe significantly increases uric acid levels.

- **E: Cholelithiasis**  
  This is the most clinically relevant adverse effect associated with **ezetimibe**. By reducing intestinal cholesterol absorption, more cholesterol is excreted into the **bile**, potentially leading to **supersaturation** and the formation of **cholesterol gallstones**. This mechanism increases the risk of **cholelithiasis**, especially over prolonged use.

---

### Conclusion

Given the mechanism of action of ezetimibe and its known side effects, the most likely increased risk from adding this drug is **cholelithiasis**.
