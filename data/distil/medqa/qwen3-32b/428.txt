### Clinical Context and Differential Diagnosis

In children, particularly those with recurrent or complicated UTIs and signs of hydronephrosis, the most common underlying condition is **vesicoureteral reflux (VUR)**. VUR is the retrograde flow of urine from the bladder into the ureters and kidneys, which increases the risk of recurrent UTIs and potential renal scarring. Other causes of hydronephrosis include:

- **Obstruction** (e.g., UPJ obstruction)
- **Neurogenic bladder**
- **Dysfunctional voiding**

However, in this case, the history of a prior UTI and the presence of hydronephrosis are classic red flags for VUR, especially in a young female child.

---

### Diagnostic Next Step

The **gold standard** for diagnosing **VUR** is **voiding cystourethrography (VCUG)**. This imaging study involves:

- Catheterization of the bladder
- Instillation of contrast material
- Radiographic imaging during voiding to detect any backward flow of urine into the ureters or kidneys

VCUG is **invasive** but is the **most sensitive and specific** test for detecting VUR in this age group. It also allows for grading the severity of the reflux, which is important for determining the need for long-term follow-up or intervention.

Other imaging options, such as **dynamic renal scintigraphy**, can assess renal function and drainage, but they do **not detect VUR**. **Intravenous pyelography (IVP)** is outdated and not used in modern pediatric urology. **Cystoscopy** is not typically used in this age group for initial evaluation of VUR. **Urodynamic testing** is more relevant in cases of dysfunctional voiding or neurogenic bladder, which are not suggested here.

---

Given the clinical scenario and the goal of identifying a possible underlying cause of recurrent UTI and hydronephrosis, **voiding cystourethrography** is the most appropriate next diagnostic step.
