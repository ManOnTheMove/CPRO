- **Recent long-haul flight**, which is a known risk factor for **deep vein thrombosis (DVT)**.

- **Acute onset of severe dyspnea** following the calf symptoms, indicating **acute PE**.
- **Arterial blood gas (ABG)** shows **respiratory alkalosis** (pH 7.5, PCO₂ 22 mmHg) and **hypoxemia** (PaO₂ 47 mmHg on 6 L O₂), consistent with a large PE.

### Evaluation of Management Options:

- **A. Unfractionated heparin (UFH):**  
  This is the **standard first-line treatment** for **acute PE**, especially in the **emergency setting**. UFH provides **rapid onset of anticoagulation**, allows for **adjustable dosing**, and can be **reversed** with protamine if needed. It is especially preferred in cases of **massive or submassive PE**, where the patient may require **close monitoring** and **potential escalation to thrombolytics or surgery**.

- **B. Streptokinase (thrombolytic):**  
  Thrombolytics like streptokinase are **reserved for massive PE with hemodynamic instability** (e.g., shock or hypotension). In this patient, **blood pressure is preserved**, so **thrombolysis is not yet indicated**.

- **C. Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH):**  
  While LMWH is effective for DVT and PE, it is **not the first-line treatment in the acute setting**, especially when **IV anticoagulation** is needed for **rapid control** or when **thrombolytic therapy** is being considered. It is **better suited for stable outpatients** or inpatients with no immediate risk of deterioration.

- **D. Warfarin:**  
  Warfarin is an **oral anticoagulant** that takes **several days to reach therapeutic levels**, so it is **not appropriate as the initial treatment** in acute PE. It is used in conjunction with **parenteral anticoagulants like UFH or LMWH** once the acute phase is under control.

  Surgical embolectomy is an **invasive procedure** typically reserved for **massive PE with severe hypotension or cardiac arrest**, or when **thrombolytics are contraindicated**. This patient does **not yet meet the criteria** for this intervention.
