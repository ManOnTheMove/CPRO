### Clinical Overview

The patient is a 19-year-old woman presenting with **jaundice**, **fatigue**, and **scleral icterus**. She recently had a **viral infection** and is on **oral contraceptives**. Her **hemoglobin is low (9.5 g/dL)**, and the **direct antiglobulin test (DAT or Coombs test) is positive**. These findings are most consistent with **immune-mediated hemolytic anemia (IMHA)**.

---

### Key Pathophysiology

- **Positive direct Coombs test** indicates **antibodies or complement components attached to red blood cells**, suggesting **autoimmune hemolysis**.
- The **recent viral illness** is a common trigger for **warm antibody IMHA**, which is typically IgG-mediated and causes **extravascular hemolysis** (primarily in the spleen).
- In hemolysis, **haptoglobin levels decrease** because haptoglobin binds to free hemoglobin released from lysed red blood cells. This binding complex is then cleared from the circulation, **reducing plasma haptoglobin**.

---

### Evaluation of Options

- **A: Increased serum C3 complement concentration**  
  → This is **not typical** in warm antibody IMHA. In fact, **C3 is usually decreased**, especially if complement is involved (e.g., in cold agglutinin disease or secondary to systemic lupus erythematosus).  
  ✅ **Incorrect**

- **B: Increased direct bilirubin level**  
  → Direct (conjugated) bilirubin is typically **normal or only mildly elevated** in hemolysis. The predominant bilirubin increase is **indirect (unconjugated)** due to massive RBC destruction.  
  ✅ **Incorrect**

- **C: Decreased haptoglobin level**  
  → This is a **classic finding** in hemolytic anemia. Haptoglobin is consumed in the process of binding free hemoglobin from lysed RBCs.  
  ✅ **Correct**

- **D: Decreased mean corpuscular volume (MCV)**  
  → Hemolytic anemias are usually **normocytic or macrocytic**, especially in the acute phase. Microcytic anemia (low MCV) is more characteristic of **iron deficiency or thalassemia**.  
  ✅ **Incorrect**

- **E: Increased bleeding time**  
  → Bleeding time is associated with **platelet function disorders** or **von Willebrand disease**, not hemolytic anemia. There is no indication of a bleeding disorder in this case.
