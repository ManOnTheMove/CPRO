### Differentiating Otitis Externa vs. Otitis Media

- **Otitis Media** typically presents with **otalgia**, **hearing loss**, **bulging tympanic membrane**, and **possible low-grade fever**, but **not purulent drainage from the ear canal** unless the eardrum is ruptured.
- **Otitis Externa**, on the other hand, is characterized by:
  - **Severe ear pain**, especially with **touch or manipulation**.
  - **Purulent or serous drainage** from the **ear canal**.
  - **Redness and swelling** of the ear canal.
  - **Possible fever** in more severe or spreading cases.

Given the **severe pain on manipulation** and **purulent drainage**, the diagnosis is most consistent with **acute otitis externa**.

### Pathogens in Otitis Externa

The most common pathogens responsible for **acute otitis externa** are:

- **Pseudomonas aeruginosa** – the **most frequent** and **most virulent** pathogen in AOE.
- **Staphylococcus aureus** – also a common cause, especially in **chronic or recurrent** cases.
- **Candida species** – may be involved in **chronic or diabetic patients**, but less likely in acute settings.

#### Why *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* is the most likely agent:

- It thrives in **moist environments**, such as the **external ear canal**, especially in patients who have been **swimming** or **have water exposure**.
- It is the **leading bacterial cause** of AOE.
- It can produce **greenish-yellow purulent discharge**, which is consistent with the patient's symptoms.
- The patient's **diabetes** may not directly increase the risk of Pseudomonas, but it can **exacerbate the infection's severity**.
