### Clinical Presentation and Key Features

The patient is a 40-year-old woman presenting with **chronic fatigue**, **pale and foul-smelling stools**, and **generalized pruritus** over the past 3 months. These symptoms are classic signs of **cholestasis**, a condition in which bile flow is impaired, leading to the accumulation of bile salts in the skin and gastrointestinal tract.

Laboratory findings include:
- **Elevated alkaline phosphatase (ALP)** and **gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT)** – both markers of cholestasis.
- **Elevated total and direct bilirubin** – indicating obstructive or intrahepatic cholestasis.
- **High cholesterol** – common in cholestatic liver disease due to impaired bile acid synthesis and cholesterol excretion.
- **Normal AST/ALT** – suggesting that hepatocellular injury is not the primary concern.
- **Normal imaging (MRCP)** – rules out gallstones or extrahepatic biliary obstruction.

The **liver biopsy** reveals **nonsuppurative destructive cholangitis**, **lymphocytic infiltration**, and **periductal granulomas**, which are characteristic of **primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)**.

### Differential Diagnosis

Given the clinical and histological findings, the key differentials include:

1. **Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)**  
   - Characterized by **chronic destruction of small intrahepatic bile ducts**.
   - **Anti-mitochondrial antibodies (AMA)** are present in **90–95%** of cases.
   - Presents with **cholestasis**, **pruritus**, **fatigue**, **pale stools**, and **elevated ALP/GGT**.
   - Histologically, it shows **non-suppurative cholangitis**, **periductal inflammation**, and **granulomas**.

2. **Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)**  
   - Often associated with **inflammatory bowel disease**.
   - Characteristic **MRCP findings**: “beaded” appearance of the bile ducts due to **focal strictures and dilatations**.
   - **Not** associated with **anti-mitochondrial antibodies**.
   - **More common in young males**, and typically presents with **recurrent cholangitis** and **liver dysfunction**.

3. **Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)**  
   - Presents with **elevated transaminases**, **positive ANA or anti-smooth muscle antibodies (ASMA)**.
   - **Not cholestatic** unless overlapping with PBC (overlap syndrome).
   - **Not consistent with this case**, where transaminases are only mildly elevated and ALP is significantly raised.

4. **Celiac Disease (Anti-endomysial antibodies)**  
   - May cause **malabsorption**, but **not cholestasis**.
   - **Does not explain the histologic findings** or elevated ALP/GGT.

5. **Autoimmune Hepatitis Type 2 (Anti-LKM1 antibodies)**  
   - Usually affects **children** and presents with **hepatitis**.
   - **Not consistent with the cholestatic pattern** in this patient.
