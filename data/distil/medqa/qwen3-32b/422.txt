### Key Pathophysiological Insight

The constellation of symptoms and signs points to **right-sided heart failure**, likely due to **pulmonary hypertension**. This is a well-known complication of **chronic hypoxia** in patients with **COPD**.

In COPD, **chronic hypoxia** causes **pulmonary vasoconstriction**, which over time leads to **pulmonary hypertension**. As the pulmonary artery pressure increases, the **right ventricle must work harder** to pump blood through the lungs. This results in **right ventricular hypertrophy** and, eventually, **right heart failure**, a condition known as **cor pulmonale**.

This is **not** due to left-sided heart failure (as LVEF is normal and there is no pulmonary edema), nor is it due to acute embolic disease (no acute presentation or relevant history).
