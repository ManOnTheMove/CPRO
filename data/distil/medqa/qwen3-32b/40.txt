Her laboratory findings show elevated alkaline phosphatase (ALP), γ-glutamyl transferase (GGT), and bilirubin levels—particularly the direct component—indicative of cholestasis. Her antimitochondrial antibody (AMA) is positive, which is a **hallmark marker** of **primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)**, an autoimmune cholestatic liver disease. The positive antinuclear antibody (ANA) supports a broader autoimmune profile, but the presence of AMA is sufficient for a diagnosis of PBC.

---

### Key Findings Supporting PBC:
- **Fatigue and pruritus** are classic symptoms of PBC.
- **Elevated ALP and GGT** point to cholestasis.
- **Positive AMA** confirms the diagnosis.
- **Normal ALT and AST** suggest a non-viral, non-alcoholic cause.
- **No signs of acute infection, obstruction, or malignancy** (e.g., no jaundice, right upper quadrant pain, or fever).

---

### Management Considerations:
- **Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA)** is the **first-line treatment** for PBC. It improves cholestasis, reduces liver enzymes, and slows disease progression. It is also associated with improved long-term survival and reduced risk of complications.
- **Cholestyramine (option C)** may be used to manage pruritus but does not treat the underlying disease.
- **Liver biopsy (option A)** may provide staging information but is no longer required for diagnosis in the setting of positive AMA.
- **ERCP (option D)** is used to evaluate bile duct obstruction, which is not the case here.
- **Atorvastatin (option B)** is for hyperlipidemia, which is a secondary concern and not the primary issue.

---

### Conclusion:
The most appropriate next step in the management of this patient is to initiate treatment with **ursodeoxycholic acid**, as it directly addresses the underlying pathophysiology of PBC.
