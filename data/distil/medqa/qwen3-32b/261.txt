The patient in question is a 52-year-old woman presenting with mild fatigue and dizziness that have caused her to miss work. She claims to have "very high blood sugar" and has a long-standing history of similar episodes over the past two years, during which she has sought care from multiple physicians and independently purchased a home glucose monitor. Despite three prior laboratory tests showing **normal fasting and post-meal glucose levels**, she remains convinced of a chronic condition and is now socially withdrawn, researching diabetes extensively online.

---

### Clinical Evaluation

- **Symptoms**: Mild fatigue and dizziness (not severe or disabling).
- **Physical Examination**: No abnormalities.
- **Vital Signs**: Normal.
- **Random Serum Glucose**: 128 mg/dL – **not diagnostic** of diabetes (diagnostic threshold is ≥200 mg/dL with symptoms).
- **Family History**: Positive for type 2 diabetes (father and brother).
- **Psychological Features**: Anxiety, social withdrawal, excessive health-related research, and persistent concern despite reassuring lab results.

---

### Differential Diagnosis Analysis

#### A. **Somatic Symptom Disorder (SSD)**  
SSD is characterized by one or more somatic symptoms that are distressing or disruptive in daily functioning, accompanied by **excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviors** related to those symptoms. In this case, the patient has **real physical symptoms** (fatigue and dizziness), but her **response to them is disproportionate** and has led to **significant behavioral and functional consequences** (missing work, social withdrawal, excessive health monitoring). Her **fixation on diabetes**, despite **multiple normal lab results**, supports the diagnosis of SSD. The presence of a somatic symptom (even mild) and the **excessive concern and behaviors** are consistent with SSD.

#### B. **Malingering**  
Malingering involves the **intentional feigning of symptoms** for external gain (e.g., financial compensation or avoiding work). There is **no evidence** in the scenario that the patient is fabricating symptoms for such a purpose. She is genuinely concerned and has sought medical attention multiple times, not to gain from it.

#### C. **Unipolar Major Depression**  
While the patient is socially withdrawn, this behavior is better explained by **health-related anxiety** rather than depressive symptoms. There is **no mention of low mood, anhedonia, or sleep/disturbance patterns** that would support a diagnosis of depression.

#### D. **Illness Anxiety Disorder (formerly hypochondriasis)**  
This disorder is defined by **high anxiety about having or acquiring a serious illness**, despite **minimal or no somatic symptoms**. The patient does report **some physical symptoms** (fatigue and dizziness), even if mild. Therefore, this **does not fully align** with illness anxiety disorder, which typically involves **excessive concern without prominent somatic symptoms**.

#### E. **Impaired Glucose Homeostasis**  
The patient has **normal fasting and post-meal glucose levels**, and the random glucose of 128 mg/dL is **not diagnostic of diabetes or impaired glucose tolerance**. Thus, this is **not the most likely explanation** for her symptoms.

---

### Conclusion

The patient presents with **mild somatic symptoms** that are **not supported by abnormal lab findings**, but she has an **excessive and persistent preoccupation** with a medical condition (diabetes). This over-response and the resulting disruption in her life point most strongly to **Somatic Symptom Disorder**.
