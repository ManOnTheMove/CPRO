DGI is a systemic manifestation of *Neisseria gonorrhoeae* infection, often presenting with **polyarthralgias**, **dermatitis (pustular or vesiculopustular)**, and **tenosynovitis**. ### Differential Diagnosis and Rationale

Other possible causes of acute joint pain and rash include:
- **Reactive arthritis** (Reiter's syndrome) – typically post-infectious (e.g., after GI or genitourinary infections), but less likely here without prior infection.
- **Septic arthritis** – usually monoarticular and more destructive, but less likely to be associated with pustular rash.
- **Cellulitis with septic arthritis** – can cause skin and joint symptoms, but not typically with pustular rash.
- **Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)** – less likely in this age group and without other systemic symptoms.

Given the **clinical features and risk factors**, **DGI is the most likely diagnosis**.

### Treatment of Disseminated Gonococcal Infection

The **Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) 2023 guidelines** recommend the following treatment for **DGI**:

- **Ceftriaxone 1 g intramuscularly or intravenously daily for 7 days**, plus
- **Azithromycin 1 g orally once daily for 7 days**, or

The rationale for **dual therapy** is:
- To cover potential **co-infection with *Chlamydia trachomatis*** (common in gonorrhea cases).
- To prevent **antimicrobial resistance**, especially since gonococcus is becoming increasingly resistant to various antibiotics.
