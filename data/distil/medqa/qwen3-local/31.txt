Carbonic anhydrase is an enzyme that catalyzes the reversible reaction between carbon dioxide and water to form bicarbonate and protons. This reaction is crucial for maintaining acid-base balance and facilitating CO₂ transport in the blood. The enzyme functions by lowering the activation energy (Ea) of the reaction, allowing it to proceed more rapidly under physiological conditions.

Acetazolamide is a known carbonic anhydrase inhibitor. It works by binding to the active site of the enzyme, thereby preventing it from catalyzing the reaction. This inhibition effectively removes the enzyme's ability to lower the activation energy. As a result, the reaction must proceed through a less favorable, higher-energy pathway, which increases the activation energy required for the reaction to occur.

It is important to note that enzyme inhibitors do not alter the thermodynamics of the reaction. The overall free energy change (∆G) of the reaction, which is determined by the difference in free energy between the reactants and products, remains unchanged.
