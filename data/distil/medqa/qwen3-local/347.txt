The 3-year-old boy presents with a well-child examination, no symptoms, and normal growth parameters. The key findings are a **grade 3/6 systolic ejection murmur** along the **upper left sternal border**, a **widely split S2 that does not vary with respiration**, and a **grade 2/6 mid-diastolic murmur** along the **lower left sternal border**. These findings are critical in narrowing down the differential diagnosis.

---

### Key Clinical Clues:

- **Fixed Split S2**: This is a hallmark of **atrial septal defect (ASD)**. In ASD, there is a left-to-right shunt, leading to increased pulmonary blood flow and delayed closure of the pulmonary valve. This results in a **fixed split S2**, which does not change with respiration, unlike the variable split seen in normal physiology or other conditions like ventricular septal defect (VSD).

- **Systolic Ejection Murmur**: This is typically due to **increased flow across the pulmonary valve**, which is a common feature in ASD due to the volume overload of the right ventricle.

- **Mid-Diastolic Murmur**: This is often due to **increased flow across the mitral valve** (due to the increased left atrial volume from the shunt), leading to a **rumbling mid-diastolic murmur**.

---

### Differential Diagnosis Considerations:

- **Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)**: Characterized by a **continuous machinery-like murmur**, not a systolic ejection murmur. Also, the S2 split is not fixed in PDA.
  
- **Benign Heart Murmur**: Typically **non-pathological**, with no fixed S2 split or diastolic components. The presence of these features makes this less likely.

- **Pulmonary Valve Stenosis**: May present with a **systolic ejection murmur**, but **no diastolic murmur** is typical.

- **Coarctation of the Aorta**: Presents with a **systolic murmur in the back**, and may have **hypertension in the upper extremities** and **diminished pulses in the lower extremities**, which are not mentioned here.

---

### Conclusion:

The **fixed split S2**, **systolic ejection murmur**, and **mid-diastolic murmur** are **highly suggestive of atrial septal defect (ASD)**. These findings are classic for this condition and are not typically seen in other murmurs or cardiac pathologies listed in the options.
