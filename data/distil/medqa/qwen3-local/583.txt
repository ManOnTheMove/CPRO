When a surgical incision is made, the body initiates a complex wound healing process that involves several stages: inflammation, proliferation, and remodeling. The final stage, remodeling, is when the scar tissue matures and becomes more organized. During this phase, the composition of the extracellular matrix changes significantly.

---

**Key points about collagen types in wound healing:**

- **Type III collagen** is predominant in the early stages of wound healing, especially in granulation tissue. It provides initial structural support but is less tensile and more flexible.
- **Type I collagen** becomes more dominant as the wound matures. It is the most abundant collagen in the skin and provides the tensile strength necessary for long-term stability.
- **Type IV collagen** is found in basement membranes and is not a major component of scar tissue.
- **Type II collagen** is found in cartilage and not relevant to scar formation.
- **Type III collagen** is not the primary component of mature scars.

Since the patient is asking about the scar 3 months post-surgery, this is well into the remodeling phase of wound healing. At this stage, the scar tissue is primarily composed of **Type I collagen**, which is responsible for the strength and structure of the healed tissue.

---

**Conclusion:**  
The tissue causing the scar is primarily composed of **Type I collagen**.
