The 80-year-old woman presented with sudden dyspnea, cyanosis, drowsiness, fever, tachycardia, tachypnea, and hypoxia (SpO₂ 81% on room air) following a recent femoral neck fracture and bipolar prosthesis surgery. The presence of **petechiae on the anterior chest wall** is a key finding. 

---

### Key Clinical Clues:

- **Recent long bone fracture**: Femoral neck fracture, which is a known risk factor for **fat embolism syndrome (FES)**.
- **Sudden onset of symptoms**: Dyspnea, hypoxia, and altered mental status are consistent with FES.
- **Petechiae**: A classic sign of FES, caused by microvascular occlusion from fat globules.
- **Fever (38.6°C)** and **tachycardia (135/min)**: Common in FES.
- **Elevated creatine kinase (CK 190 U/L)**: May occur in FES due to muscle damage or as a result of rhabdomyolysis, but not diagnostic on its own.
- **Sinus tachycardia on ECG**: Reflects compensatory mechanisms for hypoxia or systemic stress.

---

### Differential Diagnosis Evaluation:

- **Traumatic rhabdomyolysis (A)**: While CK is elevated, rhabdomyolysis typically presents with muscle pain, weakness, and myoglobinuria. Petechiae and respiratory distress are not typical features.
  
- **Thyrotoxic crisis (C)**: Unlikely without a history of thyroid disease. Symptoms like extreme hyperthermia, agitation, and tachycardia would be more severe, and there is no evidence of thyroid dysfunction.

- **Malignant hyperthermia (D)**: Occurs during or immediately after anesthesia. This patient had an uneventful surgery, and symptoms developed 24 hours postoperatively, making this unlikely.

- **Meningitis (E)**: Would present with meningeal signs (e.g., neck stiffness, photophobia), and petechiae may be due to sepsis, but the clinical picture here is more consistent with respiratory and neurological involvement rather than meningitis.

- **Fat embolism (B)**: The clinical presentation aligns with **fat embolism syndrome**, which typically occurs within **24–72 hours** after a long bone fracture. It presents with **respiratory distress, neurological changes, and petechiae** (the classic triad). The presence of hypoxia, fever, and altered mental status supports this diagnosis.

---

### Conclusion:

The most likely diagnosis is **fat embolism** due to the combination of recent femoral fracture, sudden onset of dyspnea, hypoxia, petechiae, fever, and altered mental status.
