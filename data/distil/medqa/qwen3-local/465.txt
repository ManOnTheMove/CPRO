This 6-year-old African-American boy presents with recurrent episodes of severe pain, swelling in the hands and wrists, jaundice, pallor, and signs of dehydration. These findings are highly suggestive of **sickle cell disease**, a common inherited hemoglobinopathy among individuals of African descent.

---

### Key Clinical Clues:
- **Recurrent pain episodes**: Suggests vaso-occlusive crises, a hallmark of sickle cell disease.
- **Jaundice and pallor**: Indicate hemolysis and anemia, both common in sickle cell disease.
- **Dehydration**: Can exacerbate sickling and trigger crises.
- **Swelling of hands and wrists**: May represent dactylitis, which is a classic feature in young children with sickle cell disease.

---

### Genetic Basis of Sickle Cell Disease:
Sickle cell disease is caused by a **single nucleotide substitution** in the **β-globin gene** (HBB), specifically:
- **GAG → GTG** at codon 6 of the β-globin chain.
- This results in the substitution of **glutamic acid with valine**, a **missense mutation**.
- This single amino acid change leads to the production of **hemoglobin S (HbS)**, which polymerizes under low oxygen conditions, causing red blood cells to sickle and become rigid.

---

The clinical presentation and the nature of the mutation are most consistent with a **missense mutation** in the β-globin gene, which causes sickle cell disease.
