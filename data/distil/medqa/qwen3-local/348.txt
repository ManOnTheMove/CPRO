### Key Clinical Findings and Their Significance:

- **Tracheal deviation to the left**: This is a classic sign of **tension pneumothorax**, where increased intrathoracic pressure on the affected side pushes the mediastinum toward the contralateral side.
- **Distended neck veins**: This can occur in both **tension pneumothorax** and **pericardial tamponade**, but the presence of tracheal deviation and unilateral chest findings makes tension pneumothorax more likely.
- **Decreased inspiration on the right**: Indicates a **right-sided pulmonary compromise**, consistent with a pneumothorax.
- **Hypotension and tachycardia**: These are signs of **cardiopulmonary compromise** due to the increased intrathoracic pressure.

---

### Management Priorities:

In the setting of a **penetrating chest injury** and **acute respiratory distress**, the **immediate life-threatening condition** must be addressed. Tension pneumothorax is a **cardiac and respiratory emergency** that requires **urgent decompression**.

- **Needle thoracostomy (Option D)** is the **first-line intervention** for suspected tension pneumothorax. It involves inserting a large-bore needle into the second intercostal space in the **midclavicular line** on the affected side (in this case, the right) to relieve the pressure and allow the lung to re-expand.
- **Pericardiocentesis (Option A)** is used for **pericardial tamponade**, which would present with **Beck’s triad** (hypotension, jugular venous distension, and muffled heart sounds), not tracheal deviation.
- **Intravenous fluids (Option B)** may be necessary for hypotension, but if the cause is tension pneumothorax, fluid resuscitation alone will not resolve the issue.
- **Dobutamine (Option C)** is a **positive inotrope**, used in cardiogenic shock, not appropriate for this patient’s acute respiratory failure.
- **Pain relief and observation (Option E)** is **contraindicated** in a patient with **acute respiratory distress** and signs of tension pneumothorax.
