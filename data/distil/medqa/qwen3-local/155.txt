---

### **1. Venous pH**

During exercise, the muscles increase their metabolic activity, leading to:

- **Increased CO₂ production** due to cellular respiration.
- **Lactic acid production** when anaerobic metabolism occurs (especially in the later stages of exercise).

Both **CO₂** and **lactic acid** are **acidic**, and their accumulation in venous blood leads to a **decrease in pH** (i.e., **acidosis**). 

✅ **Venous pH decreases**.

---

### **2. Arterial pCO₂**

Arterial CO₂ levels are regulated by **respiratory rate and depth**. During moderate exercise, the body **increases ventilation** to match the increased CO₂ production. This **exhales more CO₂**, **lowering arterial pCO₂**.

However, in **steady-state moderate exercise**, the increase in CO₂ production is **matched by the increase in ventilation**, so **arterial pCO₂ may remain relatively stable**.

✅ **Arterial pCO₂ may stay the same**, depending on the **intensity and duration of exercise**.

---

### **3. Venous pCO₂**

Venous blood collects CO₂ from the working muscles. As exercise increases, **muscle metabolism rises**, and **CO₂ is produced and enters the venous circulation**. This leads to a **rise in venous pCO₂**.

✅ **Venous pCO₂ increases**.
