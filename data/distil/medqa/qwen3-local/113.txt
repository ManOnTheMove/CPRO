The 68-year-old woman presents with fatigue, shortness of breath, decreased urine output, and puffy feet, along with laboratory findings of **anemia (hemoglobin 9.8 g/dL), acute kidney injury (BUN 50 mg/dL, creatinine 2.8 mg/dL), and hypercalcemia (calcium 12.9 mg/dL)**. ---

### Key Clues and Differential Considerations:

- **Hypercalcemia** is a **hallmark** of **multiple myeloma** (MM), which is a plasma cell disorder. MM leads to **bone resorption** (causing bone pain and hypercalcemia), **anemia** (from bone marrow infiltration), and **kidney damage** (due to light chain deposition or direct toxicity).
  
- **Kidney failure** in MM can be caused by **light chain (Bence Jones protein) deposition** in the renal tubules or **AL amyloidosis** (a complication of plasma cell dyscrasias). However, **AL amyloidosis** is **a consequence** of **immunoglobulin light chains**, which are the **underlying cause** of the disease.

- **Urine findings** are negative for protein and blood, which might be due to **inability of standard dipstick testing** to detect light chains (Bence Jones proteins), which are not albumin-based. This does **not rule out MM**.

- **Tenderness over the spine and rib** is consistent with **bone lesions** from MM, which are common in this condition.

---

- **A: Antiglomerular basement membrane antibodies** (Goodpasture’s syndrome) — **Unlikely** due to **negative urine protein and blood**, and lack of other hallmark features like hemoptysis or pulmonary hemorrhage.

- **B: Renal deposition of AL amyloid** — This is a **manifestation** of the disease, not the **underlying cause**. While it could explain the renal failure, it is **secondary to immunoglobulin light chains**.

- **C: Immunoglobulin light chains** — This is the **primary underlying cause** of the patient's symptoms. The **light chains** from clonal plasma cells in MM cause **hypercalcemia**, **anemia**, **bone pain**, and **kidney damage** (via direct toxicity or amyloid deposition).
