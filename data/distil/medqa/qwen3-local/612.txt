### Key Considerations:
1. **Overflow Incontinence**: A distended bladder with urinary leakage is classic for **overflow incontinence**, which occurs when the bladder becomes overfilled due to **obstructive uropathy** (e.g., from benign prostatic hyperplasia). 
2. **Immediate Management**: The primary concern is **urinary retention**, which can lead to complications such as **bladder distension, renal impairment, or infection** if left untreated. 
3. **Urinalysis Findings**: The absence of nitrites, leukocyte esterase, and turbidity rules out a urinary tract infection (UTI), which could also cause leakage but would present with different findings.

### Evaluation of Options:

- **B: Surgery** – While surgery (e.g., TURP) may be needed for long-term management of BPH, it is **not the initial step** in acute retention.
- **C: Bladder Catheterization** – This is the **immediate intervention** for acute urinary retention. It relieves the distended bladder, prevents complications, and allows for further evaluation (e.g., post-void residual volume).
- **D: Tamsulosin** – An alpha-blocker that improves urine flow in BPH, but it is **not a first-line intervention** for acute retention. It may be used after catheterization.
- **E: Pelvic Floor Muscle Training** – This is appropriate for **stress incontinence**, not overflow incontinence.

### Conclusion:
The best initial management is **bladder catheterization** to relieve the acute retention and prevent complications. This is the most urgent and appropriate step given the clinical presentation.
