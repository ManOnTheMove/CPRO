The 12-year-old boy presents with fatigue, shortness of breath, and a history of a poor diet (mostly fast food and soda). Physical findings include pallor and a thin build. Laboratory results show **low hemoglobin (11 g/dL)**, **low leukocyte count (4,250/mm³)**, **normal platelet count**, **high MCV (116 µm³)**, and **hypersegmented neutrophils** on blood smear—classic signs of **megaloblastic anemia**. Additionally, **urinary crystals** are noted.

---

### Key Diagnostic Clues:
- **Macrocytic anemia (MCV 116)** and **hypersegmented neutrophils** are hallmark features of **megaloblastic anemia**, which is most commonly caused by **vitamin B12 (cobalamin)** or **folate deficiency**.
- The **patient's diet** (fast food and soda) is likely deficient in **folate**, which is abundant in leafy green vegetables, fruits, and fortified foods. This makes **folate deficiency** a strong possibility.
- **Urinary crystals** are not directly linked to megaloblastic anemia, but they may reflect **dietary purine overload** (e.g., from fast food), which can increase **uric acid** and lead to **uric acid crystals**. This is not a direct cause of the anemia but may be an unrelated finding.

---

### Treatment Considerations:
- **Cobalamin (B12) deficiency** is typically due to malabsorption or dietary insufficiency. However, B12 deficiency is less common in children unless there is a specific cause (e.g., pernicious anemia, strict vegetarianism, or malabsorption).
- **Folate deficiency** is more common in individuals with poor diets, especially in children. Folate supplementation is the **first-line treatment** for folate-deficient megaloblastic anemia.
- **Uridine or purine supplementation** is rarely used in standard practice for megaloblastic anemia and is reserved for rare inherited disorders (e.g., thymidylate synthase deficiency), which are not suggested here.
- A **low-protein diet** would not address the anemia and is not indicated.

---

### Conclusion:
The clinical and laboratory findings strongly suggest **folate-deficient megaloblastic anemia**, likely due to the patient's poor diet. **Folate supplementation** is the most appropriate and effective treatment.
