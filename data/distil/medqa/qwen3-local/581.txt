### Pathophysiology of Viral Myocarditis

Viral myocarditis is an inflammatory condition of the heart muscle (myocardium) caused by viral infections, such as coxsackievirus. During this condition, the virus directly damages the myocardial cells, leading to inflammation and possible fibrosis. This damage can impair the heart's ability to contract effectively, leading to reduced cardiac output and the development of CHF.

---

### Understanding the Options

- **A: Concentric Hypertrophy**  
  This is typically a response to chronic pressure overload (e.g., hypertension), where the heart muscle thickens to maintain adequate pumping. It is not a typical long-term consequence of viral myocarditis.

- **B: Dilated Cardiomyopathy**  
  This condition is characterized by the enlargement and weakening of the heart chambers, particularly the left ventricle. It is a known long-term complication of viral myocarditis. The inflammation and damage to the myocardium can lead to progressive dilation and dysfunction, resulting in CHF.

- **C: Endarteritis Obliterans**  
  This is a form of vasculitis affecting arteries, not directly related to myocardial damage. It is not a consequence of viral myocarditis.

- **D: Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy**  
  This is typically a genetic condition involving abnormal thickening of the heart muscle. While it can be secondary to some conditions, it is not a common long-term outcome of viral myocarditis.

- **E: Restrictive Cardiomyopathy**  
  This is characterized by stiffening of the heart muscle, often due to infiltrative diseases (e.g., amyloidosis). It is not a typical result of viral myocarditis.
