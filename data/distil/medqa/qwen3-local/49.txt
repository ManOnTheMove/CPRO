The patient presents with recurrent epistaxis and easy bruising, along with laboratory findings that include a slightly low platelet count (100,000/mm³), prolonged bleeding time (13 minutes), and a slightly prolonged partial thromboplastin time (PTT, 33 seconds). The peripheral blood smear reveals enlarged platelets, and the ristocetin assay shows no platelet aggregation.

---

### Key Findings and Interpretation:

- **Enlarged platelets**: This is a hallmark of **Bernard-Soulier syndrome (BSS)**, which is caused by a **deficiency of glycoprotein Ib (GPIb)**, a receptor on platelets that binds von Willebrand factor (vWF).
  
- **No platelet aggregation in the ristocetin assay**: This confirms a **defect in the interaction between vWF and GPIb**, which is consistent with either **GPIb deficiency (BSS)** or **von Willebrand disease (vWD)**. However, in vWD, **platelets are typically normal in size**, not enlarged.

- **Prolonged PTT**: This can occur in both **vWD** and **BSS**, as vWF plays a role in stabilizing factor VIII, and in BSS, platelet dysfunction may also contribute to coagulation abnormalities.

- **Normal PT**: This suggests that the extrinsic and common coagulation pathways are intact, which is not typical in vitamin K deficiency or other coagulation factor deficiencies.

---

### Evaluation of Options:

- **A: Glycoprotein Ib deficiency (Bernard-Soulier syndrome)**  
  - **Characteristics**: Enlarged platelets, defective platelet adhesion, prolonged bleeding time, and no ristocetin-induced aggregation.
