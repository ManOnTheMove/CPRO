The patient presents with a transient episode of neurological symptoms (slurred speech and left-sided numbness) that resolved completely within 45 minutes. This is highly suggestive of a **transient ischemic attack (TIA)**, which is a critical warning sign for a potential future stroke. Given the patient’s age (72 years), comorbidities (diabetes, hypertension, ischemic heart disease), and prior cardiovascular interventions, he is at high risk for subsequent cerebrovascular events.

---

### Key Considerations:

- **TIA Characteristics**: Symptoms last less than 24 hours, often resolve within minutes to hours, and are typically due to temporary cerebral ischemia.
- **Risk of Stroke**: Patients with a TIA have a significant risk of subsequent stroke, especially within the first few days.
- **Evaluation Needs**: Immediate workup is essential to identify the underlying cause (e.g., carotid stenosis, atrial fibrillation, or other embolic sources) and to initiate secondary prevention strategies.
- **Current Medications**: The patient is already on **aspirin**, which is a standard antiplatelet agent. However, **clopidogrel** may be considered in certain cases, but this is not the immediate next step.
- **Clinical Stability**: While the patient appears neurologically intact at the time of evaluation, the transient nature of the symptoms and the risk of recurrence necessitate inpatient evaluation.

---
