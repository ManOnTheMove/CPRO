The 15-year-old girl presents with progressive fatigue, weakness, and worsening headaches, along with a blood pressure of 185/95 mm Hg. Laboratory findings show **high renin activity (130 ng/mL per hour)**, **high aldosterone (60 ng/dL)**, and **low potassium (2.9 mEq/L)**. This constellation of findings is highly suggestive of **secondary hyperaldosteronism**, where aldosterone is elevated due to **activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)**.

---

### Key Interpretation of Findings:

- **High renin and high aldosterone**: This is atypical for **primary hyperaldosteronism** (e.g., Conn's syndrome), where aldosterone is high and renin is low. In this case, **both are elevated**, suggesting **secondary hyperaldosteronism**.
- **Low potassium**: This is consistent with **aldosterone-induced potassium excretion**.
- **Hypertension**: A direct consequence of **aldosterone-induced sodium retention** and **RAAS activation**.

---

### Differential Diagnosis for Secondary Hyperaldosteronism:

- **Renal artery stenosis**: A common cause of secondary hyperaldosteronism, especially in younger patients. The **kidneys release renin** in response to reduced perfusion, which activates the RAAS, leading to **elevated aldosterone**.
- **Renin-secreting tumors (reninoma)**: A rare cause of secondary hyperaldosteronism. These tumors are typically found in the **juxtaglomerular apparatus** of the kidney and secrete large amounts of renin.
- **Volume depletion**: Can also stimulate renin release and subsequently increase aldosterone.

---

Given the high renin and aldosterone, the next logical step in evaluation would be to **identify the source of the renin overproduction**.
