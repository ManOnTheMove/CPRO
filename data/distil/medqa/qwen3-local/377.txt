The clinical presentation of the newborn includes micrognathia, a broad nasal bridge, short philtrum, cleft palate, intermittent muscle spasms, and a harsh systolic murmur. These findings are consistent with **DiGeorge syndrome**, which is caused by a **deletion in chromosome 22 (22q11.2)**. 

Key features of DiGeorge syndrome include:
- **Facial dysmorphism**: Micrognathia, broad nasal bridge, and short philtrum.
- **Cleft palate** and other craniofacial abnormalities.
- **Congenital heart defects**: Commonly ventricular septal defects (VSD) or tetralogy of Fallot, which can present as a harsh systolic murmur over the lower left sternal border.
- **Hypocalcemia**: Due to thymic and parathyroid dysfunction, leading to muscle spasms (tetany).
- **Immune deficiency**: Often associated with the syndrome but not mentioned here.

Other options:
- **Deletion in chromosome 7** (e.g., Williams syndrome) typically presents with supravalvular aortic stenosis and a "elfin" facial appearance, not cleft palate.
- **Nondisjunction of chromosome 21** (Down syndrome) includes hypotonia, microcephaly, and heart defects, but not the specific facial features or hypocalcemia described.
- **Nondisjunction of chromosome 13** (Patau syndrome) causes severe abnormalities, but survival is usually short, and the facial features differ.
- **Deletion in chromosome 5** (Cri-du-chat syndrome) includes a high-pitched cry and microcephaly, but not the described cardiac or cleft palate features.

The combination of cleft palate, micrognathia, heart defect, and hypocalcemia strongly points to **DiGeorge syndrome**, caused by a **chromosome 22 deletion**.
