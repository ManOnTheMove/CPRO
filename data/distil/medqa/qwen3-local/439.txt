These clinical features are highly suggestive of **patellofemoral pain syndrome (PFPS)**, a common condition in active young individuals, especially athletes. PFPS is often due to overuse, muscle imbalances, or biomechanical issues affecting the patellofemoral joint.

---

### Evaluation of Options:

  Aspiration is typically used to drain fluid from the joint or to obtain samples for diagnostic purposes (e.g., in cases of infection or gout). The patient does not present with signs of effusion or joint swelling, making this option inappropriate.

- **B: Lidocaine injection**  
  While lidocaine may provide temporary pain relief, it is not a treatment for the underlying pathology. It may be used for diagnostic purposes (e.g., to confirm a specific source of pain), but it is not the most appropriate management for a chronic condition like PFPS.

  Surgery is generally reserved for severe or refractory cases, such as structural abnormalities (e.g., patellar dislocation, cartilage damage), or when conservative treatments fail. Given the clinical presentation and the absence of red flags for more serious pathology, the most appropriate management is **quadriceps exercises** to address the underlying muscle imbalances and biomechanical issues.
