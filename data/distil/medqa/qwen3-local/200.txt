### Analysis of Options:

- **A: Exogenous thyroxine intake**  
  While exogenous thyroxine can cause hyperthyroidism with suppressed TSH, it does not typically cause a diffuse goiter or elevated TSI. The thyroid scan would not show increased uptake in this case, and the presence of TSI antibodies is inconsistent with exogenous hormone use.

- **B: Graves’ disease**  
  This is an autoimmune disorder characterized by the production of TSI, which stimulate the thyroid to produce excessive thyroid hormones. The clinical features (elevated T3/T4, low TSH, goiter, bruit, tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss) and the presence of TSI antibodies strongly support this diagnosis. While exophthalmos is a hallmark of Graves’ disease, its absence does not exclude the diagnosis.

- **C: Hashimoto’s thyroiditis**  
  This is an autoimmune hypothyroid condition. The lab findings (elevated TSH and low T4) are inconsistent with the patient’s presentation. Moreover, the presence of TSI antibodies is not typical of Hashimoto’s.

  While pregnancy can cause transient hyperthyroidism due to hCG stimulation, the severity of symptoms, the presence of TSI antibodies, and the thyroid scan findings are not consistent with this scenario.

- **E: De Quervain thyroiditis**  
  This is a self-limiting inflammatory condition of the thyroid. It typically presents with a painful, tender goiter and transient hyperthyroidism. However, the thyroid scan would show decreased uptake, not increased, and TSI antibodies are not associated with this condition.
