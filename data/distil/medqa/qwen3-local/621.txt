### Analysis of the Case

A 15-year-old boy presents with a 2-month history of left hip pain that started after a fall. The pain is worsening, radiates down the thigh, and is exacerbated by movement. He has an antalgic gait, tenderness in the left groin, and limited internal rotation of the hip. Flexing the hip causes external rotation and abduction, which are classic physical findings of **slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE)**.

#### Key Clinical Clues:
- **Age and obesity**: SCFE typically affects adolescents, especially those who are overweight.
- **Trauma history**: A fall, even minor, can precipitate SCFE in a susceptible individual.
- **Physical exam findings**: Limited internal rotation and the "hip flexion test" (external rotation and abduction with hip flexion) are highly suggestive of SCFE.
- **Lab findings**: Leukocyte count and ESR are within normal or mildly elevated ranges, which is consistent with SCFE rather than an acute infection.
- **No significant fever or systemic symptoms**: This makes septic arthritis less likely.

---

### Differential Diagnosis Considerations

- **Septic arthritis**: Unlikely due to normal WBC and low-grade fever.
- **Hip fracture or stress fracture**: Less likely given the gradual onset and absence of direct trauma to the femur.
- **Osteochondritis dissecans**: Could present with pain and limited motion, but usually affects the knee more commonly.
- **Hip synovitis**: May cause pain and limited motion, but typically resolves more quickly and without the specific physical findings described.

---

### Most Likely Diagnosis

**Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)** is the most likely diagnosis based on the clinical presentation, physical findings, and risk factors (age, obesity, and recent trauma).

---

### Management of SCFE

The **standard and urgent treatment** for SCFE is **surgical pinning of the femoral head** to prevent further slippage and complications such as avascular necrosis or osteoarthritis.
