The 7-year-old girl presents with a midsystolic ejection murmur, a loud S1, and a widely fixed split S2. These findings, along with the echocardiogram showing a left-to-right shunt across the interatrial septum, are classic for an **atrial septal defect (ASD)**. 

### Key Pathophysiology of ASD:
- A left-to-right shunt increases pulmonary blood flow, leading to **volume overload** of the right heart and pulmonary vasculature.
- Over time, this can result in **pulmonary hypertension** due to increased pressure in the pulmonary arteries.
- If uncorrected, this may progress to **Eisenmenger syndrome**, where the shunt reverses, causing cyanosis and right-to-left flow.

### Surgical Correction:
- Repairing the ASD eliminates the left-to-right shunt, reducing the volume overload on the right side of the heart and the pulmonary circulation.
- This directly prevents the development of **pulmonary hypertension**, which is a major long-term complication of untreated ASD.
