The 9-year-old boy's presentation includes lower extremity deformities, hip dislocations, paralysis below the L2 nerve root, and bladder/bowel incontinence. These findings are consistent with a congenital spinal abnormality, specifically **spina bifida**, a neural tube defect. Spina bifida occurs when the neural tube fails to close properly during early fetal development, leading to structural and neurological abnormalities.

During pregnancy, **maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (AFP)** is a key biomarker used in prenatal screening for neural tube defects. In cases of spina bifida, the fetal liver produces excess AFP, which leaks into the amniotic fluid and then into the maternal circulation, resulting in **elevated maternal serum AFP levels**.

Other options are less relevant:
- **Beta-human chorionic gonadotropin (beta-hCG)** is part of the triple or quad screen but is not specifically elevated in spina bifida.
- **Estriol** is a form of estrogen and is part of the quad screen but is not directly linked to neural tube defects.
- **Lecithin and sphingomyelin** are phospholipids used to assess fetal lung maturity, not related to neural tube defects.

Thus, the substance most likely to be elevated in the mother's serum during pregnancy is **alpha-fetoprotein**.
