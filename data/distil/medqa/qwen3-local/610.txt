### Key Clinical Clues:
- **Recurrent joint and bone pain**: Common in SCD due to vaso-occlusion and ischemia.
- **Dactylitis (swelling of hands and feet in childhood)**: A classic early sign of SCD.
- **Stroke in a sibling**: SCD increases the risk of cerebrovascular accidents due to sickled cells blocking cerebral vessels.
- **X-ray findings**: Subchondral lucency, sclerosis, and joint space narrowing of the humeral head are **classic radiographic features of avascular necrosis (AVN)**, a common complication of SCD.

### Pathophysiology:
In SCD, sickled red blood cells can occlude small vessels, leading to **ischemia and infarction of bone trabeculae**. This results in **avascular necrosis**, which is most commonly seen in the femoral and humeral heads. The x-ray findings described (subchondral lucency, sclerosis, joint space narrowing) are consistent with AVN.
