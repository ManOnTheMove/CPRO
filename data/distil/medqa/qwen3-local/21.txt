### Pathophysiology of Coronary Artery Disease

Atherosclerosis, the underlying cause of coronary artery stenosis, is a chronic inflammatory process that begins with **endothelial dysfunction**. This is the earliest and most critical step in the development of atherosclerotic plaques. Endothelial dysfunction leads to:

- Increased permeability of the vessel wall
- Promotion of leukocyte adhesion and infiltration
- Oxidative stress and inflammation
- Impaired nitric oxide production, which normally helps maintain vascular tone and prevent platelet aggregation

Once endothelial dysfunction occurs, **monocyte infiltration** (option B) follows, leading to the formation of foam cells. Subsequently, **smooth muscle cell migration** (option A) and **fibrous plaque formation** (option E) occur. **Platelet activation** (option C) is more commonly associated with **acute thrombosis** following plaque rupture, which is the final step in the progression to an acute myocardial infarction (AMI), not the initial step in the pathogenesis of the stenosis itself.
