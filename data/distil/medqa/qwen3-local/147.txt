Key findings include worsening facial pressure (particularly over the cheeks and forehead), green-tinged rhinorrhea, a low-grade fever (100.1°F), and a nasal exam showing edematous turbinates and purulent discharge. These clinical features point toward a bacterial etiology.

---

### Key Diagnostic Clues:

- **Duration**: Symptoms have persisted for **3 weeks**, which falls within the **acute** timeframe (≤ 4 weeks). This rules out **chronic rhinosinusitis (E)** and **subacute rhinosinusitis (C)**, which are defined as lasting 4–12 weeks and >12 weeks, respectively.
  
- **Nature of Rhinorrhea**: **Green-tinged rhinorrhea** and **purulent discharge** are hallmark signs of **bacterial infection**, not viral or allergic causes. This supports **acute bacterial rhinosinusitis (B)**.

- **Fever**: A low-grade fever (100.1°F) is a systemic sign of infection, further supporting a bacterial process rather than a purely viral or allergic condition.

- **Clinical Course**: The patient had a prior viral infection (common cold), which is a common **precipitating factor** for secondary bacterial rhinosinusitis. This aligns with the concept of **"double sickening"**, where symptoms worsen after initial improvement.

---

### Elimination of Other Options:

- **Acute viral rhinosinusitis (A)**: Typically presents with clear rhinorrhea, no fever, and resolves within 10 days. The presence of purulent discharge and fever makes this less likely.
  
- **Allergic rhinitis (D)**: Presents with clear rhinorrhea, sneezing, and itching, but **not** purulent discharge or fever.

- **Subacute bacterial rhinosinusitis (C)**: Defined as lasting **4–12 weeks**, which is not consistent with the 3-week duration here.

---

### Conclusion:

Given the clinical presentation, duration, and the presence of purulent discharge and fever, the most likely diagnosis is **acute bacterial rhinosinusitis**.
