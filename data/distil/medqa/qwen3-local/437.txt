### **Key Diagnostic Considerations**

The clinical presentation is consistent with **intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy (ICP)**, a condition characterized by pruritus and elevated bile acids, often recurring in subsequent pregnancies. The key features here are:

- **Pruritus without rash** (common in ICP)
- **Jaundice** (due to elevated bilirubin)
- **Elevated direct bilirubin and bile acids**
- **Normal liver enzymes** (AST and ALT are only mildly elevated)
- **Absence of gallstones or biliary obstruction** (confirmed by normal ultrasonography)

ICP is not associated with structural biliary disease, so procedures such as **endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)** or **laparoscopic cholecystectomy** are not indicated.

---

### **Management of ICP**

The **first-line treatment** for ICP is **ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA)**, which:

- Reduces pruritus
- Lowers serum bile acid levels
- Improves liver function
- Is safe for both mother and fetus

While **early delivery** (e.g., at 37–38 weeks) may be considered in severe cases, it is not the **immediate next step** unless there are clear fetal or maternal complications (e.g., fetal distress, severe pruritus, or very high bile acids). In the absence of such complications, **UDCA** is the most appropriate initial intervention.
