The 13-year-old girl presents with a linear rash on her hand and forearm following a recent hike. The rash consists of erythematous papules and vesicles, and she has no systemic symptoms such as fever, swelling, or difficulty breathing. These findings are highly suggestive of **contact dermatitis**, likely due to exposure to a plant such as poison ivy, oak, or sumac, which are common in hiking environments and cause a linear rash due to the distribution of the allergenic oil (urushiol).

---

### Key Considerations:

- **Clinical Presentation**: The linear pattern, presence of vesicles, and localized nature are classic for **allergic contact dermatitis**.
- **Differential Diagnosis**: While shingles (herpes zoster) could present with a vesicular rash, it typically follows a dermatomal distribution, not a linear pattern from environmental exposure. Viral rashes are less likely given the timing and lack of systemic symptoms.
- **Treatment Goals**: The primary treatment for allergic contact dermatitis is **avoidance of the allergen** and **reduction of inflammation**. Topical corticosteroids are the mainstay for localized, non-severe cases.

---

### Evaluation of Options:

- **A: Oral prednisone** – A systemic corticosteroid, but not first-line for localized, non-severe dermatitis. It is reserved for more widespread or severe cases.
- **B: Topical clobetasol** – A **high-potency topical corticosteroid**, effective for reducing inflammation and itching in localized contact dermatitis. It is appropriate for this case given the limited area and severity.
- **C: Topical tacrolimus** – An immunomodulator used for atopic dermatitis, not typically the first-line treatment for allergic contact dermatitis.
- **D: Phototherapy** – Used for chronic conditions like psoriasis or eczema, not for acute allergic reactions.
- **E: Azathioprine** – An immunosuppressant used for autoimmune or chronic inflammatory conditions, not for acute allergic reactions.

---

### Conclusion:

Given the clinical presentation and the need for a targeted, effective treatment, **topical clobetasol** (Option B) is the most appropriate choice for this patient. It is a high-potency corticosteroid that effectively addresses the inflammation and itching associated with contact dermatitis.
