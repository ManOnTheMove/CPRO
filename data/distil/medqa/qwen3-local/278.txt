The patient presents with symptoms and findings consistent with a lymphoproliferative disorder, particularly B-cell lymphoma. The key diagnostic clues are:

1. **Clinical features**: Low-grade fever, weight loss, night sweats, and lymphadenopathy (cervical, axillary, inguinal) suggest a systemic lymphoproliferative process.
2. **Hepatosplenomegaly**: Enlarged liver and spleen are common in advanced lymphomas.
3. **Histopathology**: Nodular proliferation of **centrocytes and centroblasts** (typical of follicular lymphoma) and **CD20 positivity** (B-cell marker) confirm a B-cell lymphoma.
4. **Genetic abnormality**: A **reciprocal translocation of chromosomes 14 and 18** is pathognomonic for **follicular lymphoma**. This translocation leads to **overexpression of the BCL-2 gene**, which is located on chromosome 18.

---

**BCL-2** is an **oncogene** that encodes a **mitochondrial protein** involved in **inhibiting programmed cell death (apoptosis)**. Normally, BCL-2 prevents apoptosis by blocking the release of cytochrome c from mitochondria. In follicular lymphoma, the translocation places the BCL-2 gene under the control of the **immunoglobulin heavy chain promoter**, leading to **constitutive overexpression** of BCL-2. This results in **resistance to apoptosis**, allowing malignant B cells to survive and proliferate.
