The laboratory findings include a **prolonged partial thromboplastin time (PTT)** (45 seconds, normal range 25–35 seconds), a **prolonged bleeding time** (10 minutes, normal range 2–7 minutes), and a **normal platelet count** (180,000/mm³). ---

### Key Diagnostic Clues

- **Prolonged PTT** and **prolonged bleeding time** with **normal platelet count** suggest a **defect in the intrinsic clotting pathway** or **platelet function**.
- **Von Willebrand disease (vWD)** is the **most common inherited bleeding disorder**, and it fits this clinical and laboratory profile:
  - **Prolonged PTT** due to reduced von Willebrand factor (vWF), which is a carrier for factor VIII.
  - **Prolonged bleeding time** due to impaired platelet adhesion.
  - **Menorrhagia** is a hallmark of vWD in females.

Other possibilities such as hemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency) or B (factor IX deficiency) are less likely in a female and would also cause a prolonged PTT, but they are **X-linked** and **rare in females**.

---

### Treatment Considerations

The treatment options given are:

- **A: Oral prednisone** – Not typically used for vWD, more for immune-mediated thrombocytopenia (ITP).
- **B: IV immunoglobulins** – Also used in ITP or certain autoimmune disorders, not vWD.
- **C: Vitamin K injection** – Used for vitamin K deficiency or warfarin reversal, not relevant here.
- **D: Tranexamic acid** – An **antifibrinolytic** drug used for **menorrhagia** and can be used for **nosebleeds** in vWD.
- **E: Desmopressin (DDAVP)** – A **vasopressin analog** that **increases vWF and factor VIII levels** and is the **first-line treatment** for mild to moderate vWD, especially for acute episodes like nosebleeds or before surgery.
